Chapter 457  
Definition of Creature (3)  
~*~

In front of the main gate of the county community, the air hung thick with tension.

“Cheuk, you’ve shrunk a lot. What should we do with these?”

“Kill them? Or… should I take her alive?”

The voices of the bodyguards were distorted, as if something thick and fleshy had grown over their throats. But more unsettling than their voices was the aura radiating from them.

Normally, when facing a predator—be it man or beast—you could sense the killing intent: sharp, focused, predatory. But this was different.

It wasn’t the energy of a killer. It wasn’t even that of a wild beast.

It was something else—something alien. An unknown creature. Or perhaps a grotesque fusion of multiple organisms, twisted into one.

*‘I can’t afford to hesitate this time either.’*

Quan tensed, ready to spring.

The moment the bodyguards revealed their true forms, gravity surged outward in three directions.

*Slash the one on the left first—use the recoil to accelerate—eliminate the others before they react.*

Tick. Tick. Tick.

A faint sound, nearly imperceptible, reached Quan’s ears—detectable only to those attuned to the Schema. It was the slow, deliberate scrape of a long fingernail being dragged across the palm of the bodyguard facing Armand.

“Everybody—stop moving!”

A loud, commanding shout erupted from beyond the front door.

Quan instantly planted his feet and twisted his body to counteract the sudden burst of external gravity. But it was too late—he tasted the metallic tang of black blood in his mouth.

Chin! Chin!

The heads of the two bodyguards on the left snapped off cleanly and tumbled to the ground.

Shiina shuddered. One moment, Quan had been standing beside her. The next, he was already deep in enemy territory.

She hadn’t even seen him move.

Most swordsmen relied on explosive propulsion for ambushes, but Quan moved like a vacuum—drawing himself forward through suction. His acceleration was instantaneous, requiring no preparation, no warm-up.

*Thank the gods he’s not my enemy.*

“This bastard! I’ll kill you!”

The surviving bodyguards roared and charged. Quan raised his sword horizontally, ready to meet them.

“Stop it, you fools! Didn’t you hear my brother?”

The bodyguards froze mid-charge.

There was no visible transformation yet, but their faces were already twisting, distorting toward something monstrous.

With a soft click, the iron gate swung open. A tall, middle-aged man stepped out.

He wore a long coat lined with soft, mink-like fur. Stuffed weasels, their lifeless eyes staring blankly, adorned both shoulders like grotesque ornaments. His hair hung in greasy strands past his shoulders, and he wore pointed, dark-tinted glasses that concealed his eyes.

This was Frankwine—leader of the Night Blind, a man said to dominate every hunting ground near the mainland.

Armand stepped forward.

“Are you in charge here?”

Frankwine stroked his beard, studied Armand for a moment, then grinned, revealing a row of sparsely spaced gold teeth.

“They’re just hot-blooded boys. Yes, I’m the captain. What brings you to me?”

“I have a matter to discuss with the munitions community.”

“Aha! So it’s business. Of course, business is essential. Come in, come in.”

“Brother! They killed two of my comrades! Are you just going to let them walk in?”

Frankwine glanced at the two headless corpses, shrugged.

“What’s the matter? When men fight, some die. Heads get cut off. That’s how it goes. What’s there to fuss about?”

“But brother—”

Frankwine’s expression darkened.

“Are you going to keep whining? If you’re dead, you’re dead. If you’re alive, do your job and stop complaining.”

The bodyguards glared at Armand and the others, teeth clenched. But they stepped aside.

Ruthless. Absolute.

That was Armand’s first impression of the Night Blind.

As they walked toward the office, Frankwine gave a brief tour of the facilities.

Armand had suspected it, but seeing it firsthand confirmed the scale.

They were producing everything—weapons forged from alchemical elixirs, consumables, medicines essential for hunters.

*‘This is nothing like the enemies I’ve faced before.’*

Cautious, Armand probed.

“Thank you for your time. I know you’re busy.”

“Ah, I drank too much last night. Still feeling it. But what if we play? Got anything cheaper than shit? Kuck.”

Frankwine laughed, swinging the office door open with the flourish of a hotel manager from Armand’s world.

Inside was a cramped, dingy room—cockroach-sized, the walls reeking of stale cigarettes and spilled alcohol.

“Wait here. I’ll finish up and be right back.”

After ushering Armand and his group inside, Frankwine turned and walked down the hall to a room at the end.

Inside, a tall, blond-haired man lounged with his legs stretched across the desk. He stood abruptly at Frankwine’s entrance.

“Ah, brother. You’re here?”

“What did I say? I told you if you sat at my desk one more time, I’d kill you.”

“Who knew you’d take so long? Anyway, what about those bastards?”

Meatgun—second-in-command of the Night Blind.

The munitions community had over three hundred members, but Meatgun was the only one bold enough to stand up to Frankwine.

Frankwine pulled a crumpled cigarette from his pocket and lit it.

“Worth the wait. Leave the rest to the men. Do you have the estimate from Raysis? Hand it over.”

Meatgun pulled a folded paper from his inner coat pocket.

Frankwine unfolded it. Ten creature names were listed, split between first and second ranks—all notoriously difficult to hunt.

“Geuk… I’m crazy, but that woman’s even more insane. How the hell is she expecting to get these? Send her men out, and they won’t even leave bones behind.”

“But it’s an unconventional offer. She said she’d adjust the telomere levels of a hundred members to Level 5.”

“Hmm. Level 5.”

Telomere level referred to the fundamental life-sustaining power of an organism. The higher the level, the greater the combat potential.

At Level 5, a being could live up to four hundred years—assuming no explosive biological degradation.

A hundred such subordinates? That would secure the Night Blind’s dominance for centuries.

But what puzzled Frankwine was why Raysis—known for her paranoia and hostility—would propose such generous terms.

“What’s your play, girl? I sent only the apex predators of the local food chain.”

In nature, the fittest survive. But survival alone didn’t make a species supreme.

True apex predators were those whose traits compensated for every weakness.

“I know it’s twisted,” Frankwine mused, “but isn’t it too specialized? Such distinct traits wouldn’t integrate well with other creatures.”

“She must have found a way. She’s not clumsy. And this offer—whatever her motives—it clearly benefits the Night Blind.”

Frankwine crumpled the note and stuffed it into his pocket.

“So, should we accept?”

“Yes. Regardless of her intentions, this is a golden chance to strengthen the Night Blind. If you have any ambition to rule Purgatory, you seize this.”

Frankwine suddenly swung his arm.

His fist slammed into Meatgun’s chin.

Meatgun’s body shot backward like a cannonball, crashing into the wall with a thunderous crack. He slumped to the floor, dazed, a massive fissure spreading behind him.

As Meatgun staggered to his feet, clutching his forehead, Frankwine turned to the door.

“If you sit in my chair again, I’ll kill you.”

Thud.

The door slammed shut.

Back in the office, Armand and the others exchanged uneasy glances.

Another stench—sharp, fishy, unnatural—mingled with the stale alcohol and smoke.

Shiina waved her fan.

“What the hell is that smell?”

The men exchanged glances but stayed silent.

Ethella tilted her head.

“I know, right? What kind of fish is that…?”

Armand quickly changed the subject.

“It’s strange. Why would the head of the munitions community suddenly treat us so… generously?”

Shiina nodded.

“I agree. The bodyguards were bad enough, but Frankwine’s demeanor was off. Something’s shifting—somewhere we can’t see.”

Etella and Quan nodded in silent agreement.

But whatever was happening, they had no way of knowing.

Moments later, Frankwine returned, smiling coolly.

“Haha! Sorry, got held up with work.”

Shiina, as if waiting for her cue, snapped, “Why is this office such a mess? And what *is* that stink? It’s giving me a headache.”

Frankwine blinked, then burst out laughing, as if just realizing.

“Haha! Right! Yesterday… I played a little hotter than usual.”

Armand gestured to a chair.

“Sit. I have something to tell you.”

Frankwine sat, feigning modesty. But his mind raced.

All four of them—clearly exceptional. And yet, two were young women, untouched by the brutality of Purgatory.

*Outsiders. From another world.*

Which made things easier. Frankwine wasn’t from Purgatory either.

“Yes, why would such dangerous people—the ones who just slaughtered two of my best men—come to this dump?”

He hadn’t forgotten his subordinates’ deaths.

But his grief wasn’t personal. It was transactional—like calculating the price of meat lost in a shipment.

“We want to rent a factory in the munitions community.”

“Hoho. Straight to business. What are you making?”

Armand revealed only what was necessary.

“A large Tagis.”

He added that they would cover all associated elixirs and materials—and pay the rental fee in elixirs as well.

Frankwine licked his lips.

He liked the offer. To a merchant, every deal was gold.

But the timing was odd.

A sudden request from Second Commander Raysis. Now, this—production of a massive Tagis, something the First Command would surely get involved in.

*Something’s happening on the rebel side.*

Armand pressed.

“The deal will be worth your while.”

Frankwine snapped out of his thoughts and grinned broadly.

“If it’s something big, of course you’d come to us. Now I see—you’re not just anyone. But… elixirs alone won’t do. We’ve been doing very well lately.”

“Then?”

Elixirs weren’t currency, but they were the most common medium of exchange on the mainland.

Armand didn’t understand—why wouldn’t elixirs suffice?

Frankwine pretended to ponder, then walked to his desk and scribbled something on a notepad.

He tore off the page, crumpled it, and tossed it onto the table.

Armand slowly unfolded it.

A beam of light pulsed faintly from beneath the bandages covering his eyes.
