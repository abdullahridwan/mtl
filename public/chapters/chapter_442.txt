Chapter 442  
Task Force (4)  
~*~

Shirone and the others followed Mahatu through a winding, labyrinthine path deep within Kergo. When the local warriors realized they were messengers of God, they knelt in unison—a sign of deep reverence. Even after the group passed through, the tribespeople greeted them with warmth and respect.

Unlike Kadum, who had offered vast tributes in exchange for eternal life, Mahatu had managed Kergo’s resources with transparency and wisdom. As a result, the lives of the people had improved dramatically. The faces of those passing by were no longer hollow with hardship, but alive with energy and hope.

Though Shirone had never fought for Kergo, his act of opening the Guffin’s Gate and ascending to Heaven had made him their savior in the eyes of the people.

“I’d like to hold a festival in your honor,” Mahatu said, “but the situation is urgent.”

“Yes,” Shirone replied. “We need to reach Heaven as soon as possible.”

Mahatu didn’t press for details about Heaven. To him, the safety and future of his people came before divine mysteries.

Sein spoke up. “How many people are on the waiting list? I heard it was already full.”

In theory, anyone could enter Heaven once an Unlocker opened the Guffin’s Gate. But to prevent chaos, Maze had established a trial chamber—only warriors of exceptional merit could pass through. If the list was full, it meant many had already been deemed worthy.

Mahatu ran his tongue over his lips thoughtfully. “Actually, not many. About seven.”

“That’s strange,” Sein said. “Then why block new entrants?”

Mahatu hesitated. “That’s… something I can’t explain. You’ll understand when you see it for yourself.”

Gaold wasn’t concerned. Their only objective in Kergo was the Guffin’s Gate. If there were skilled warriors waiting to ascend, he’d consider taking them—but only if they could match the strength of the current team. Given the high level of their group, that seemed unlikely.

“It doesn’t matter,” Gaold said. “Let’s go see them. We’ll sort it out quickly.”

As if to show sincerity, Mahatu quickened his pace.

They arrived at a massive iron gate, its surface carved with intricate, ancient sculptures.

“This is the Warrior’s Chamber,” Mahatu explained. “Those who wish to enter Heaven wait here.”

He pushed open the heavy door. Gaold stepped in first, the rest of the party following behind.

Inside, seven warriors sat scattered across the chamber, each watching Gaold with sharp, wary eyes.

One swordsman, seated with a longsword resting across his lap, spoke first. “Are you a warrior… or an angel?”

Gaold ignored him. He scanned the faces of the seven, then turned to Sein.

“They need to be at least Level 6 certified,” he said. “Anything below that isn’t worth evaluating.”

A wizard in the corner frowned. “You speak as if being Level 6 is some great achievement.”

“And what if I do?” Gaold replied.

“Hah! Is that why you’re so arrogant? I’ve killed certified Level 6 wizards—countless times. Skill isn’t measured by rank. It’s measured by how much hell you’ve endured. That’s what separates life from death.”

The wizard stepped forward, revealing five magical rings glowing on his right hand.

The swordsman interjected, annoyed. “Hey, I asked first.”

“Does it matter?” the wizard shot back. “Anyone with skill should be allowed. Wasn’t that the rule?”

He turned to Gaold, raising a hand. “Let me show you the strength of someone who’s survived the harshest trials.”

Gaold didn’t respond. His index finger snapped toward the wizard.

A split second later, the air gun exploded with a sharp crack.

The wizard’s body slammed into the wall, blood spraying from his mouth. He crumpled to the floor, head bowed, unable to move.

“Next,” Gaold said.

Shirone frowned. He hadn’t intended to kill anyone—not like in the Association’s underground bunker—but he knew better than anyone the raw power of Gaold’s air gun.

“I agree with what he said,” Shirone admitted quietly.

An elderly wizard stepped forward, rolling his neck. “There is strength beyond mere levels. But for those without real skill, such talk is just delusion. I’d be different, though—”

He didn’t finish.

Gaold had already cast teleportation. In the blink of an eye, he appeared in front of the wizard and struck him across the face with the back of his hand.

The blow snapped the old man’s head sideways. He staggered like a puppet with cut strings, then collapsed to the ground.

“Next.”

The remaining five warriors froze. None moved.

This wasn’t a group of fortune-seekers hoping to sneak into Heaven’s outskirts.

This was a task force—highly trained, deeply committed, and now thoroughly intimidated.

When no one stepped forward, Gaold turned to the swordsman who had spoken first.

“You. Didn’t you say something earlier?”

The swordsman clenched his jaw, glaring at Gaold. Then, slowly, he relaxed. He raised both hands in surrender.

“Alright, I give up. Acknowledging your strength is… fine. Aww!”

Gaold lunged forward like a striking hound and punched him square in the jaw.

The impact sent the swordsman’s head snapping back. His body skidded across the floor before coming to a stop.

“All of them are just talk,” Gaold said, brushing his hands together. “No real power.”

The atmosphere in the chamber turned icy.

Gaold glanced at the four remaining warriors, who stared at him in stunned silence. Then he turned toward the door.

“All of you—get out. Unless you want to end up like them.”

Without another word, the four scrambled for the exit, limbs flailing in their haste to escape.

Only the unconscious warriors and Shirone’s party remained. A chilling silence settled over the room.

Gaold turned to Mahatu. “That’s that. Clean and simple.”

“There’s still one more,” Mahatu said.

He stepped aside, revealing someone who had been waiting outside.

A familiar figure walked in.

Armin of Gwangan—the Eternal Reflector.

“Oh,” Gaold said, raising an eyebrow. “So that’s the bait.”

To catch a big fish, you needed good bait.

And Armin was perfect.

Shirone’s expression hardened. How long would he be treated like a child before his intuition was finally trusted?

Yet when he saw Armin’s face, his voice softened slightly.

“Why are you here? Did you forget what I asked?”

“No,” Armin said. “I remember. I didn’t come to interfere with your life. I came to keep a promise—to myself.”

Gaold crossed his arms. “Sorry, but that won’t work. I don’t work with the White Line. I can’t trust that.”

“The Ivory Tower has withdrawn,” Armin said calmly. “Isn’t that enough?”

Shirone’s eyes widened.

The Ivory Tower—the magical utopia accessible only to the world’s greatest wizards—had withdrawn from the conflict. If Armin had truly abandoned it, his resolve was deeper than anyone expected.

“But why?” Shirone asked. “And Keira…?”

Armin remained silent about the details. But a shadow passed over his face—something heavy, unresolved.

“Shirone,” Armin said, “nothing in this world is more important to me than you. Heaven is dangerous. You shouldn’t go there alone—not even for the Oliopher School, who raised me.”

“Shiina-san is Level 5 certified,” Quan said quietly.

“Mr. Quan…?”

Shirone was surprised. Quan rarely spoke.

“I don’t know the full details of your relationship,” Quan continued, “but I won’t let you risk your life. And you don’t need to worry about Mr. Shirone. No one will die before I do.”

The wizard’s role was clear: survive until the mission’s end.

For a swordsman, dying first—no matter the reason—was a wound to pride.

‘So it’s Quan…’

A demon of the blade who had felled two members of Fengzhang, the continent’s strongest swordsmanship group.

To know such a warrior was protecting Shirone brought comfort in the midst of following the unpredictable Gaold.

But as an older brother, Shirone couldn’t help but feel a pang of sorrow.

‘What right do I have…?’

Except for Shirone, everyone welcomed Armin’s arrival.

A master of time magic who had mastered even the speed of light.

His stop magic—the ability to freeze time—was the most powerful crowd control in existence.

“Fine,” Gaold said. “If it has nothing to do with the White Line, I accept. But make this clear—I’m in charge. Disobey me, and you’re out. Understand?”

Armin nodded. “Understood.”

With Shirone as leverage, joining the team was the best move.

With the ascent to Heaven imminent, the final team of ten was now complete—ready to reclaim the labyrinth.

‘Wow,’ Shirone thought. ‘When you put it all together, it’s incredible.’

He still couldn’t believe he was part of this group.

Regardless of faction—White, Red, or Black—this was a task force of the world’s finest.

* * *

Mahatu handed out the remaining rooms.

Each of the ten team members received their own quarters. After dinner, they gathered in the largest room, now serving as a war room.

The Guffin’s Gate would open at 6:00 AM the next morning.

They could leave immediately, but after the long journey, rest was essential. Once in Heaven, even a moment’s pause could mean the difference between life and death. Better to be fully prepared.

Gaold spread out the map Nan Kang had given him across the table.

It was a detailed schematic of Heaven, marked with colored dots indicating threat levels.

“Heaven is in the midst of a cataclysm,” Gaold said. “A revolution has erupted among the subjects. Rebels are active across multiple zones. This map shows real-time threat levels—Level 2 and above. Well,” he added dryly, “or it did. This data is three months old.”

His Royal Guard spies in Heaven had gone silent after sending this last update.

Sein spoke first. “Based on our confirmation of Eastas, we need to revise our strategy. Our top priority is assessing Maze’s current condition. In some cases, even changing the coordinates of the Akashic Records might not help.”

Ethella added, “If Miro was taken from Heaven, doesn’t that mean the enemy has reset their status? They’re already preparing.”

“Exactly,” Gaold said. “A stealthy preemptive strike won’t work. We’ll have to adapt. My plan is to cause maximum disruption, then infiltrate in the chaos.”

Armin asked, “Creating a disturbance in Heaven? There aren’t many ways. Are you planning to use the rebels?”

“That’s one option,” Gaold admitted. “But the rebels won’t move easily. What we need is the ancient weapon Tagis. If we can disable Heaven’s interception system—Aegis—they might reconsider.”

Armin shook his head. “Even if Tagis could break Aegis, the rebels are unlikely to launch a full-scale war. Most were subjects—they know Heaven’s power better than anyone. If Mecca and Norr united, they could challenge even Heaven. But fear… ingrained fear is hard to overcome.”

“Fear can be broken,” Gaold said. “Anyway, Heaven’s current state will be nothing like we expect. We’ll have to adapt as we go. Besides,” he added, glancing at Shirone, “we have the Masterpiece.”

Shirone frowned. “What do you mean? Masterpiece?”

Sein turned to him. “It’s you, Shirone. If you succeed, you could create an effect equivalent to mobilizing the entire rebel army. So tell us—how exactly are you going to blow up Heaven?”

All eyes turned to Shirone.

He swallowed hard, nerves tightening.

This was something that had to be explained before they left—but the atmosphere was different now than when he’d told only Gaold.

Now, every word was a strategic decision. A single misstep could cost lives.

Gaold gave a small, encouraging nudge with his chin. “Heh. Don’t hesitate, Shirone. In my opinion, it’s the craziest magic I’ve ever heard.”

‘Which is exactly why I’m so nervous…’
