Chapter 443

Task Force (5)

“It’s okay to speak freely, Shirone. It’s just us now. We’ll listen and judge for ourselves.”

Encouraged by Zocre’s words, Shirone repeated the explanation he had already given to Gaold.

“First, this spell is based on Ataraxia.”

“Of course,” Zocre said. Starting with Ataraxia was the only way—just a 1% chance, but it was the only path that could possibly lead to destroying Heaven.

“The casting direction will be aimed skyward. That’s why we’re spreading a magic circle above her head.”

“Hoo? Straight up into the sky?”

It was undeniably the beginning of something unprecedented.

As the others leaned in, anticipation rising, Shirone continued.

“Ataraxia is trigger-based magic, so its range isn’t fixed. I concluded that firing upward would best exploit that flexibility.”

Fleur narrowed her eyes. “Understood. But… what exactly are you firing?”

“A laser.”

“A laser?” The word echoed through the group. Everyone knew what a laser was—but the sky was empty. There was nothing to destroy up there.

“Yes,” Shirone confirmed. “I’ll use Ataraxia to amplify the laser’s linearity and shoot it as high as possible.”

Fleur pressed further. “How high are we talking?”

Shirone paused. “It’s a simple calculation, but roughly… about 6,000 kilometers.”

“Six thousand?” Shiina gasped.

Sein’s Iron Wheel spun rapidly, processing the numbers. Even before Shirone finished, Sein had already begun running simulations in his mind. Equations formed and resolved with mechanical precision.

Shirone spread his arms wide. “At that altitude, the laser will transmit information to compress God’s Particles into a dense mass. With Ataraxia’s amplification and trigger mechanics, we can create a mass equivalent to about ten tons—on a planetary scale.”

Chee-hee-hee! Sein’s Iron Wheel whirred faster, confirming the data.

Shirone continued. “It’s… perhaps a form of creativity. The shape doesn’t matter in theory, but based on omnipotence principles, it manifests as a spear of light—ten tons in weight. And that…”

“It’s falling,” Sein finished.

“Yes,” Shirone said simply. “That’s all.”

“That’s all?”

The group fell silent.

The mechanism was absurdly simple—far simpler than anyone had expected. Yet, within that simplicity lay the terrifying potential to obliterate Heaven.

“But… is it really possible?” Fleur asked, turning to Gaold.

Gaold, in turn, looked at Sein. Now, it was up to Sein’s analytical mind—the Servant-class intellect—to judge.

“It’s possible,” Sein said without hesitation. “A ten-ton mass falling from 6,000 kilometers. Assuming Heaven’s gravity is similar to ours, it would take about fourteen minutes to reach its target. During that time, gravitational acceleration would steadily increase its velocity. By terminal velocity, it would exceed fourteen kilometers per second.”

Fleur’s eyes widened. “Fourteen kilometers… per second?”

At that speed, once the object entered visual range, there would be no time to react. No defense, no evasion.

Sein continued. “The impact would be equivalent to a 10-ton spear striking at over 41 Mach. The kinetic energy alone, converted into explosive force, would amount to approximately 2.19 million kilobusters. That exceeds Heaven’s structural durability. If it hits Arabot at the center, all Seven Heavens will be reduced to rubble.”

No one doubted Sein’s calculations. His precision was absolute.

And yet, the number refused to settle in their minds.

2.19 million kilobusters?

The idea that a mere student could wield such power was beyond comprehension.

“See?” Gaold said, a grim smile forming. “I told you it was insane magic.”

Sein nodded slowly. “What impressed me most when I first heard it? There are no shaky assumptions. No hand-waving. It clearly defines what can and cannot be done. At first, I thought the senior might be spouting nonsense—but no. It’s not Shirone’s magic that destroys Heaven.”

He paused.

“It’s universal gravitation. The planet’s own gravity pulls the spear down. Heaven is destroyed by the very laws it claims to transcend.”

Shirone turned to Gaold. “I was going to call it *Shining Spear*.”

“The name doesn’t matter,” Gaold said. “The real issue is trajectory, landing point, and timing.”

Sein nodded. “I can calculate all of that. Once we reach Heaven, I’ll gather observational data. Within three days, we’ll have a clear picture of planetary motion. Then we just set the launch time accordingly.”

With that, the team began reshaping their strategy around this new variable. *God’s Punishment*—as the spell was now being called—had such a profound impact that the entire mission framework shifted.

While the others debated tactics, Shiina watched Shirone with quiet pride.

Gaold admired how the spell leveraged natural laws, but Shiina saw something deeper.

Anyone could understand that dropping ten tons from 6,000 kilometers would cause massive destruction.

But the real challenge wasn’t the fall—it was the lift.

To raise that mass, you needed potential energy equivalent to the kinetic energy of impact. And that energy had to come from somewhere.

Thus, *God’s Punishment* wasn’t just gravity. It was Shirone’s magic.

The laser, amplified by Ataraxia, transmitted data to Godot, triggering the compression of God’s Particles into a high-density mass. The result looked simple, but the underlying mechanics required flawless understanding and execution—something impossible for anyone without Shirone’s complete mastery of Ataraxia.

‘You’ve squeezed every last drop from your knowledge,’ Shiina thought. ‘Compressed your ideas like diamonds, with no gaps, no waste.’

That was why Shiina could sincerely celebrate his disciple’s leap.

“But there are conditions,” Shirone said, cutting through the discussion.

“Ataraxia was given to me by Archangel Ikael. To cast this spell, I must first meet with Ikael. It’s bound by contract.”

No one would break a team contract lightly.

Yet, human nature being what it was, there was a temptation to ignore the restriction—especially now that they had access to a weapon of such overwhelming power.

Even the most level-headed among them felt it.

For *God’s Punishment* to activate, Ikael had to make contact with Shirone.

Depending on the circumstances, that could be easy—or impossible.

Sein disliked uncertainty. He preferred control.

“I’ll honor the contract,” he said. “And you’ve earned our help. But is there truly no alternative? If we abandon Ikael, could we achieve something similar? Even if it means sacrificing everyone—so long as you survive.”

Eliminating the variable was preferable.

That was how highly Sein valued *God’s Punishment*.

Shirone shook his head. “I won’t meet Ikael unless everyone is in danger. But if there’s no danger, then we don’t need *God’s Punishment* at all. The moment the situation becomes life-threatening, the motivation to meet Ikael will exist. Understand—under those extreme conditions, there’s no need to impose additional restrictions on Ikael or the spell.”

Sein exhaled. “Hmm. So that’s what you meant.”

If the situation was dire enough to justify destroying Heaven, then it was already desperate enough to justify contacting Ikael.

Even a small chance of survival would make the risk worthwhile.

“Fine,” Sein said. “I’ll accept that. As long as my teammates’ safety doesn’t depend on Ikael’s involvement, I’m satisfied.”

It was the best compromise they could reach.

As the others shouted “Ankera!” in unison, Gaold stepped forward with a scowl.

“Maybe this is a return to the beginning of everything,” he muttered.

His eyes burned with fury.

“I will destroy your gods.”

Without hesitation, he strode into the black orb.

Even if he lost everything, he would pour himself entirely into this moment—so that, in the end, he would have no regrets.
