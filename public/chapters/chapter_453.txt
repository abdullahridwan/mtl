Chapter 453

Second Command (2)

---

“Oh, this can’t be…”

The gatekeeper stood frozen, speechless.

As far as he knew, Shirone was a Nephilim who wielded light magic. Yet it was dark magic that had obliterated Oprica. And now, in broad daylight, beneath a blazing sun, the spirit of light saturated the world—making the use of dark power all but impossible unless one stood beyond the natural laws.

“Are you going to continue?” Shirone asked.

Fleur stepped forward, drawing the magic wand *Phoenix* from Kubrick and spinning it once in her hand. She had nearly unleashed its full force earlier—if Shirone hadn’t acted first, the phoenix would have erupted and reduced the prison to rubble.

The gatekeeper’s face twisted in frustration.

“Don’t talk nonsense. We are Norse. Don’t be swayed by baseless myths!”

As he cast the sound magic *Ferry*, a harsh, crow-like cry split the air. Moments later, three Norse warriors descended on gusts of wind magic, landing behind the gatekeeper. They were Norr guards stationed nearby, summoned by the signal.

“Hey, why’d you call all of us?” one of them asked, a bald man scanning the scene with narrowed eyes. He spotted Shirone and the others and sneered. “What, those kids?”

“The ones from First Command,” the gatekeeper replied. “That blonde is Shirone—the Light of Area 73.”

“Shirone?” The bald warrior’s expression darkened. “*That* Shirone? The one attached to the Mecha faction?”

Fleur stepped forward, unimpressed. “Got someone else in mind? We’re busy too. If we’re doing this, let’s get it over with.”

The gatekeeper turned to his men. “Don’t be alarmed. Their magic system is different from ours.”

“I know,” the bald guard muttered. “And worse.”

Fleur raised *Phoenix*, but Shirone moved ahead of her.

“I’ll handle this, Senior.”

Regardless of how the Norse viewed him, Shirone was a pivotal figure in the war against Heaven. To gain access to their headquarters—and the tens of thousands of Norse subordinates within—he needed to demonstrate the Nephilim’s strength now.

“Fine,” Fleur said, stepping back. “Just make it quick.”

Shirone walked slowly toward the gatekeeper.

“Take us to headquarters. There’s no need to keep fighting pointlessly, is there?”

The gatekeeper smirked. “Heh. You live up to your reputation. But you should know—our true strength begins *now*.”

He turned to his comrades. “Prepare *Mahagard*.”

The bald guard blinked. “*Mahagart*? Seriously? You’re using *that*?”

“Didn’t you hear? He uses strange magic. Best to end this cleanly.”

“Tch. Fine.”

The bald warrior reluctantly obeyed. At the entrance to the headquarters, the gatekeeper’s word was law.

The other three guards fell into formation behind him. Unlike modern magic, which emphasized individual expression, ancient magic often relied on unity—allowing for powerful collective spells when executed in perfect synchronization.

Sensing the thickening air, Shirone compressed photons in front of him, forming a dense core of light.

The four Norse cast in unison.

“*Mahagard*!”

A windstorm erupted—dense, crushing, and deafening, like a jet engine at full throttle. It wasn’t just wind; it was a physical force capable of tearing limbs from bodies and slamming victims into walls with bone-shattering impact.

Shirone gritted his teeth and fired.

The *Photon Cannon* tore through the storm—a single, focused beam of light that ripped the air apart, distorting the atmosphere as it went. With a thunderous crack, it shattered *Mahagard* completely.

“Agh!”

The backlash sent shockwaves rippling through the area, flapping the Norsemen’s cloaks and ringing in their ears. The sheer sonic pressure was enough to disorient.

“M-Mahagard…?”

The collective spell of the Norse tribe was as refined and devastating as the Mecha’s three-stage combat system. It required flawless coordination—something only the most disciplined could achieve. For it to be dismantled by a single opponent was a crushing blow to their pride.

Fleur had seen it coming.

“That was stupid. I didn’t account for the density difference.”

No matter how powerful the wind, it couldn’t displace a beam of light compressed into a single point. That was why modern magic had evolved—air magic now split into *press* and *blow* types to adapt to various situations.

“Still… he’s gotten stronger fast.”

The *Photon Cannon* now carried far more force than it had during his time at the Magic Association. Perhaps the senior year had sharpened him. Training in shotgun movement and laser judo had clearly paid off.

Watching Shirone’s growth, Fleur felt a quiet pride—as if it were her own.

---

Shirone and the others followed the gatekeeper deeper into the forest.

Though stunned by Shirone’s display, the Norse fighters weren’t broken. Their loyalty belonged solely to their commander, and they had absolute faith in her leadership. But even so, the fact that one man had shattered their most advanced collective spell forced them to reconsider.

“We’ll guide you,” the gatekeeper said, “but don’t expect us to accept you. The commander will never allow an alliance with the Mecha.”

Fleur frowned. “What exactly caused the split? Was there a specific reason?”

It wasn’t unusual for Mecha and Norse to clash—different philosophies, different methods. But outright separation? That suggested something deeper.

“The Mecha are arrogant,” the gatekeeper said. “They believe they alone know the truth. The Norse have our own way. But they refused to acknowledge it. To them, war is only about victory. They don’t want to work with anyone they see as weak. And we’re done being used.”

He stopped, brushing aside thick foliage.

“Here. This is our headquarters.”

Shirone looked ahead—only a sheer cliff face met his eyes.

“This is it?”

The gatekeeper cast *Ferry* again. A sharp, birdlike cry echoed through the air.

Then, across the cliff, dozens of hidden openings split open. Norse guards emerged from within, revealing themselves. The ground trembled as the rock itself began to shift—transforming, reshaping—until the cliff became a towering fortress.

A stone door slid open at the base, revealing a descending staircase carved into the mountain.

“It’s ancient magic,” Fleur murmured.

The Norse, who lived in harmony with nature, had used their affinity to reshape the entire mountain into a stronghold.

Inside, they were met by elite warriors—clearly the best among the Norse rebels.

From the main entrance strode a tall, striking woman with painted lips and golden hair, moving with regal confidence. It was Reisis, the Norse archmage and Second Commander of the rebellion.

“What’s going on? Purin’s birdsong?” she asked, her voice cool.

The gatekeeper pulled her aside and whispered. Fleur activated her drone’s audio enhancement, routing the sound into a private channel. A warning flashed—retinal sensors couldn’t capture audio. The Norse were using magic to contain the sound.

“Hmm. Understood,” Reisis said after a moment.

She turned and walked toward Shirone. Fleur remained alert—Reisis radiated ancient magic, her Spirit Force reading at 32%.

But then, unexpectedly, Reisis’s stern expression softened.

“Round,” she said, using Shirone’s title. “The Light of Area 73.”

Before anyone could react, she stepped forward and embraced him tightly.

A heavy, blood-like perfume clung to her.

Without another word, Reisis led Shirone and Fleur into the inner stronghold. Clove and Gardlock remained outside. The two visitors were escorted to the commander’s office.

The room was surprisingly refined—carpeted floors, elegant sofas facing each other. A far cry from the austere Mecha halls.

Reisis took the central seat—the place of honor. Shirone and Fleur sat on separate sofas, positioning themselves for a quick escape if needed.

Reisis noticed their tactical seating and smiled faintly.

“So. Why are you here?”

Shirone met her gaze. “You already know. First Command is proposing an alliance.”

Reisis rested her chin on one hand, crossing her legs. “Allies? And what would that accomplish? What’s the goal?”

Shirone relayed Sain’s argument—the one that had convinced Crud. Disable the Aegis system, and the path to Heaven would open.

Reisis listened, her expression growing thoughtful.

“Hmm. Paralyzing Aegis… That would benefit us too.”

“Once Heaven is vulnerable,” Shirone added, “we can create new variables. Why not join forces? Move together?”

“But first,” Reisis said, her eyes glinting, “I have a question.”

She leaned forward slightly. “If we take Heaven… who becomes king?”

Shirone blinked. “King?”

“Exactly. Who is this alliance *for*? We worked together at the start of the rebellion. But now? The Mecha and the Nephilim are divided. Do you really think my people will follow *me* if I order an alliance? We’ve already broken once. You reached out first—but how do I know you won’t stab us in the back the second time?”

From the Norse perspective, the suspicion was understandable.

Shirone hesitated. He had no easy answer.

Reisis smiled. “So I have a suggestion.”

“Go on.”

Her index finger pointed at Shirone like a blade.

“The Light of Area 73. *You*—the symbol of the rebel war—join the Second Command. If you do, I’ll actively persuade my people to accept the alliance.”

Fleur tilted her head. “Not a bad idea. But does it really matter? Once we’re allied, we’ll be united anyway.”

“Exactly. It *shouldn’t* matter. That’s why I’m offering it. A Norse isn’t foolish enough to make a proposal the other side will refuse. But we need assurance. A symbol. The Norse fight for freedom—having *you* among us would convince them.”

Fleur glanced at Shirone. After a moment, she gave a small nod.

Shirone understood. Just as the Mecha and Norse had their own burdens, his team needed to move the rebels somehow.

“Fine,” Shirone said. “Let the Norse use my name when the war is over.”

Reisis clapped her hands together, smiling brightly. “Excellent! Then let’s register you right away.”

“Register?” Shirone asked.

“Of course. Now that you’re joining the Second Command, we need to measure your Spirit Force.”

---

Shirone and Fleur stood before two adjacent changing rooms.

Spirit Force measurement required several steps—including a full physical examination.

Fleur spoke before entering. “If anything happens, prioritize getting back here. The most important thing is completing the registration.”

“Understood. Be careful, Senior.”

They entered their respective rooms. Their drones followed, splitting to monitor both.

The door clicked shut behind Fleur. She looked around—empty, save for another door leading inward.

A Norse woman approached.

“Remove your clothes and hand them over. I’ll return them after the inspection.”

“How much do I need to take off?”

“All of it. We sterilize everything in a disinfection chamber. We also need blood and body hair samples.”

Fleur unbuttoned her blouse slowly.

Even now, Reisis couldn’t be fully trusted. If this was a trap, being naked wouldn’t help. But Fleur wasn’t worried—her clothes offered no real defense anyway.

Still, one question nagged at her.

*Why take blood? What are they planning to do with it?*

She removed the last of her clothing and handed it over. The woman placed it in a basket.

“Oh—and the ring too,” the woman said. “Take it off.”

Fleur’s expression turned icy.

Her eyes, calm until now, sharpened.

She raised her ring finger, staring at the woman. “This stays on. If sterilization is the goal, a ring is irrelevant.”

The ring contained *Phoenix*—a mana amplifier. Removing it wasn’t an option.

The woman hesitated. “Ah… right. Never mind. This way, please.”

She turned and hurried out.

Only then did Fleur relax.

She had deliberately tested the woman. If they had insisted on the ring, it would have confirmed her suspicion—the inspection was a cover.

But the woman had backed down too easily. No hidden intent, no tension.

*Did I overthink it?*

Fleur followed her into the next room.
