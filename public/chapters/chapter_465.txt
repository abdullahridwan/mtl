Chapter 465  
Opportunity for Revenge (7)  
~*~

Back in her room, Lacey unclasped her cape.

A crimson cloth slid from her shoulders like liquid and pooled on the floor. Beneath the torn fabric, burns from the photon cannon’s impact glowed like embers across her skin.

With a scowl, she tore off the rest of her clothes and stood before the mirror.

What a grotesque body.

A monstrous form—twisted, dry, dull in color—clung to her neck like a parasite. It bore no trace of truth, goodness, or beauty. No one could call it anything but hideous. And yet, this was the result of desperate measures, the price of survival.

“It’s disgusting…” she whispered.

Reisis had once been a subject—more than that, an outstanding citizen. But she had been marked for experimentation with anecdotal alcohol, a forbidden substance tied to the creation myth, strictly prohibited by Heaven.

The transformation was inevitable. She would melt. Become a giant.

To fear divine magic was itself a sin, but Reisis had known the truth: anecdotal alcohol was humanity’s most terrible tragedy—a fate worse than death.

Like others before her, Reisis had obtained the stimulant *Epines* through a secret channel. No one knew if it truly worked. But for someone with nothing left to lose, even a rumor was worth clinging to.

She had held the vial like a lifeline, sat on a bench in the plaza, and prayed.

*Please… let my spirit survive within the giant’s body.*

Then, a voice answered.

“Do you want to live?”

A middle-aged man stood before her—kind-faced, with a well-kept beard. She had heard rumors of scholars who dared to study the anecdotal magic not as divine law, but as a phenomenon to be analyzed. He was one of them. And he offered her a secret.

“If you want to live, take this before drinking Ilhwa’s sake.”

He handed her a small gourd, no bigger than a finger.

“What’s inside?” she asked.

“A fly.”

“Ugh!”

Reisis recoiled, but the man’s voice turned stern.

“Swallow it alive. Only then will you survive.”

The thought made her stomach churn. But death loomed. She couldn’t afford to hesitate.

When the time came, Reisis opened the vial, tipped the bottle to her lips, and tilted her head back. Something hard wriggled into her mouth. She gagged, fought the urge to vomit, and forced it down.

Then Kergoin arrived. He stripped her bare and led her to one of the eight glass spheres standing before the giant’s statue.

*Please… please save me.*

As plasma flooded the sphere, Reisis squeezed her eyes shut, trembling. Her body dissolved, merging with the black liquid, entangled with countless other consciousnesses.

It wasn’t death. But it wasn’t life, either—just a hopeless void where sensation blurred into madness.

Eventually, the newborn giants from each district gathered and began their march toward Jotunheim.

Days passed. Dawn broke.

Then, one of the giants—taller than the rest—collapsed, screaming. Flesh and bone separated, melting like porridge. From the steaming ruin, only Reisis remained, kneeling in the muck, gasping.

“Ugh! Ugh!”

When consciousness returned, the first thing she felt was joy.

The man had been right.

She had survived—her spirit intact, reborn from the giant’s corpse.

*I’m alive! It’s okay!*

*Thud! Thud!*

The ground shook as other giants passed by. She jumped to her feet—lighter now, stronger. But thirst clawed at her throat.

Then, to her surprise, she sensed it: spring water, several kilometers away.

*My senses… they’re sharper. Is this the power of a giant?*

She ran. Faster than she ever had. But no matter how far she went, she couldn’t catch her breath.

Finally, she reached Ongdalsaem, bent forward to drink—

“Quaaaaaaagh!”

Her scream tore through the forest.

In the water’s reflection, she saw her face.

And realized it was her own.

What could be more cruel than the horror of recognizing your own monstrous image?

Reisis shrieked like a madwoman.

“No! Give me back my body! Bring it back!”

At that moment, the man who had given her the fly stepped out of the trees. The one who had turned her into this.

Years had passed. But now, seeing him again, she remembered—

Perhaps he was the only one who had ever truly seen *her*.

“Why?!” she screamed. “Why did you do this to me?!”

“You asked to live,” he replied calmly. “What could be more important to a living thing?”

“Don’t mock me! If you saw yourself like this—ugly, deformed—could you live with it?!”

The man laughed.

“It doesn’t matter. You are still *you*. By destroying form, you’ve gained infinite form.”

Reisis stared, broken, as he turned to leave.

In the crushing realization that there was no return, one question surfaced.

“…Who are you?”

The man paused. Turned his head slightly.

“Satan.”

Back in the present, Lacey stared at her reflection with cold, lifeless eyes.

How many beings had she absorbed to maintain even a semblance of humanity? Norse. Kergoin. Mecca. Even fragments from the Land of the Earth.

They had brought her power—legendary weapons, vast knowledge—but at a terrible cost.

Her body betrayed her. Parts twisted without warning. Limbs moved against her will.

“Shirone…” she whispered. “You are my salvation.”

Shirone’s spirit could contain all qualities. With him, she could finally merge the most beautiful forms in existence.

She would take the body of an angel.

* * *

Second Thousand Rakia. City of the Fallen Angels.

A prison since ancient times for angels who abandoned duty, craved power, or twisted divine law for their own ends.

Kariel hovered above the city, his gaze sweeping over the wastrel angels below, squandering their eternal lives in idleness.

“Heh. Useless creatures.”

He had come seeking aid from Girshin, commander of the giant corps. Rejected.

But fallen angels were still angels. They were bound to obey Ankera’s will.

Almost all of them.

One exception remained.

Ikasa. Angel of Desire.

The same fallen angel who had captured Amy’s group during the Area 73 incident and offered them to the Buddha. At the time, she had hoped to earn reinstatement by delivering three “virgins” to Kariel. But when Shirone escaped to the original world, her punishment was doubled.

While countless others lazed in decadence, Ikasa alone suffered—chained to a colossal iron ball, doomed to circle endlessly in an infinite orbit 300 meters beneath the earth.

“Sigh… sigh…”

The iron ball—twenty meters in diameter—rumbled along its grooved path, hurtling forward at terrifying speed. In its wake, a blur of motion: Ikasa’s face, eyes covered, mouth sealed, her body bent backward like a drawn bow.

Kariel watched the arc of the ball complete one full rotation. Then, as it returned, he reached out.

A flash. His weapon—a celestial asteroid—unfolded into a ring, intercepting the iron sphere like a wall. With a thunderous *crash*, it halted.

“Huh… huh…”

Ikasa gasped, her breath ragged, her body trembling from the strain.

Kariel stepped forward and sliced through the bindings over her eyes.

She blinked, then burst into tears.

“…You… you came…”

The archangel had arrived. The one they all feared. The one no fallen angel could ignore.

“Ikasa,” Kariel said. “There is a task for you.”

She nodded, still on her knees.

“Shirone has returned. This time, you may find it… entertaining.”

“Hah… hehehe!”

A guttural laugh of pure hatred erupted from her throat.

Kariel smiled faintly and dismantled the rest of her bindings.

Ikasa, still on the floor, didn’t pause to recover. She lifted her head, bowing deeply.

“Use me, Archangel of Birth.”

“Kill Shirone,” Kariel commanded. “Make it painful. Bring me his broken face. There is an angel I must show you.”

“That… is my joy.” Her voice trembled with anticipation. “But I am lowly. I do not know where the abominable Shirone is.”

Kariel opened the Great Law and accessed Jebul’s central processing unit.

The map of Purgatory flickered—engulfed in red light. The search radius expanded at an alarming rate.

“Babel is scanning the skies above Purgatory,” Kariel said. “You will have all coordinates by the night of the full moon. That is your chance.”

Ikasa’s eyes flared.

The full moon.

The peak of the negative season’s law in Purgatory. The one time when a fallen angel like her could reach her full power.

*Wait for me, Shirone.*

Her gaze burned with the hunger for revenge.

* * *

Around midnight, Fleur slipped through the door.

She was Jonah—no need to show her face.

Confirming the hallway was empty, she closed the door behind her with practiced ease and moved deeper into headquarters.

One was better than two when stealth mattered. Less risk of exposure.

Now was the perfect time. Shirone had been dispatched as part of the 2nd Command’s effort to unite the rebels.

*Something’s off,* Fleur thought. *Why all this secrecy? What are they really doing?*

Staying close to the wall, she turned a corner—then froze.

Down the corridor, two sentries walked and talked.

“Hey, sleepy. Don’t you ever sleep? What’re you eating at this hour?”

“Whatever I want. It’s livestock anyway.”

One of them carried a tray piled high with food. He paused near a torch bracket on the wall.

His companion frowned.

“What? Why’d you stop?”

“Thought I saw something move over there.”

He handed the tray to his partner and stepped toward the shadow.

Nothing.

“Must’ve been dreaming,” he muttered.

“Probably mistook the torchlight for something,” the other said.

The sentry shrugged and returned.

Moments later, a narrow flue dropped from the ceiling where they had stood. A drone detached from the shadows and silently landed on one sentry’s shoulder.

*Phew. That was close. Almost got caught.*

But it had worked. She’d heard everything.

*They’re feeding someone… at this hour? Who?*

Fleur followed.

When the sentry reached the wall and touched the torch hanger, the stone slid open—revealing a hidden staircase.

Fleur waited a full minute. Then, mimicking the gesture, she opened the passage and descended.

The space below was far larger than expected. Corridors branched in every direction. Dozens of laboratories lined the halls.

*Where are the sentries now?*

She activated an algorithm to amplify sound within her spirit zone. Voices filtered in.

“You eat like a pig. Lucky bastard. Where else could you have a life this good? I’m jealous.”

“Heuk. If you’re so envious, go join him.”

A pause.

“Damn… I hate this. You’re literally talking about *cattle*.”

As the sentries moved away, Fleur approached the heavy iron gate at the end of the hall.

It was designed to open only from the outside.

*What in the world is down here?*

She pressed her ear to the cold metal.

A part of her wanted to turn back. But curiosity won.

She peered through the crack in the unlocked door—

And froze.

“This… this…!”

Her breath caught.

Behind the gate, rows of human bodies lay in stasis—connected by tubes, their life force visibly draining into a central machine.

Pandora’s box had been opened.

And there was no going back.
