Chapter 412
The Meaning of Survival (4)
~*~
Stage 3.
The simulation unfolded as a swamp stretching to the horizon. At first, the students hovered in the air using flight magic, but one by one, they succumbed to the poisonous miasma rising from the mire and plummeted into the water.
The trial truly began then. Those who fell into the swamp screamed, their faces contorted in agony beyond recognition. Even without knowing the exact nature of the hazard, it was clear they were enduring excruciating pain. Meanwhile, the students still hovering in the air gritted their teeth, holding their breath. They all knew they would eventually have to descend into the swamp.
The Magic Association had designed this survival program to prove a point: the raw power of nature cannot be overcome by magic alone. True survival depends not on spells, but on a wizard’s sheer will.
As the toxic gas thickened, students in the air began to vomit. Some were already losing their grip, their bodies swaying before they too gave way.
“Sigh.”
Shirone heard Nade’s heavy breath. Nade slowly tilted his head, looked over at Shirone, and spoke.
“See you later, Shirone.”
With Nade’s fall, the rest of the students began to drop out of the sky like rain.
Shirone couldn’t bear to watch any longer. A muffled voice escaped his lips.
“...Nade.”

* * *
Eliminated students began crawling out of the virtual zone one by one. They placed ice towels over their heads, sat away from the simulation terminals, and tried to rest.
Collie, monitoring the control panel, announced, “Stage 3: Severe Cold has concluded. Currently, there are eight survivors on the Red Team and seven on the Blue Team.”
Olivia nodded. “Hmm. Surprisingly, quite a few made it through. This year’s graduating class is certainly at a high level.”
“Class Three has outstanding students, and Fermi and his group are still holding strong. Besides, there aren’t any slouches in Class Two,” another instructor noted.
Alpheas frowned slightly. “Stage 4… That’s the cutoff, isn’t it? The balance of this exam will hinge there.”
The Magic Association had set Stage 4 as the official benchmark for certified wizards. It was the point where participants experienced actual death, not just simulated pain. They would die dozens of times over.

* * *
*Whoosh!*
Listening to the wind howling from the abyss, Shirone was in the midst of an endless fall.
Having survived the extreme natural environments of the previous stages, the fourth stage presented a completely different challenge: a mechanical hellscape.
It was inside a massive vertical shaft whose depth could not be measured. Hundreds of thousands of blades rotated in tight formation, leaving no gap. They sliced falling students into pieces.
Death here was absolute and inescapable.
Each time a student’s mental state broke under the trauma, their connection to the virtual zone severed, and they were eliminated one after another.
Shirone gritted his teeth. Had he already been dismembered and reconstructed ten times? The simulation seemed to mock him: *Is this how far you’ll go?*
A massive blade descended, leaving a blurred afterimage in its wake. There was no surviving that.
‘Hold on! Hold on!’
Shirone pushed through the blade’s afterimage, but his body was already cleaved into three parts.
How could one describe the horror of watching one’s own severed legs fall past one’s face? Those who had never truly died could never comprehend it. Human language failed to capture it.
Other students fared no better. One was impaled by a blade jutting from the shaft wall, her fall halted momentarily before she was discarded and sent plummeting toward her next death.
“Hah… hah…”
With his body forcibly reconstituted, Shirone gasped for air, his lungs burning as if a newborn taking its first breath.
‘Ugh! Just let me die!’
Anyone would have lost their mind seeing dozens of interlocking drills spinning like a floor of teeth.
*Clang!*
Shirone fell onto them.
*Grind. Crush. Splatter!*
In an instant, his body was pulverized and drained away like pulp.

* * *
“Cadet! Damn it!”
Nade jolted awake in the virtual zone, frustration radiating from him.
Iruki, Amy, and even Fermi and the others couldn’t handle it. They collapsed, rolling on the floor in agony.
Their heads felt like they were splitting open. Vomiting was common, followed by long, ragged sighs.
“It’s driving me crazy. Why do I always get stuck at Stage 4?”
Amy wiped her mouth, her expression grim.
Maintaining consciousness in the virtual zone after experiencing death required an obsession that transcended life and death itself. Seekers. Religious fanatics. Killers. Whatever the background, this was now a domain for minds that had crossed into territory beyond normal human limits.
No wonder Iruki had been eliminated.
“You die when you die. What’s next?”
Nade’s pragmatic mind couldn’t grasp it. He shouldn’t have to create something to die for. The stage defied logic, clashing with his aversion to uncertainty.
The trial clearly met the Association’s expectations.
Many students were eliminated at Stage 4, crawling out of the virtual zone with phantom wounds that left them pale and trembling.
“Ugh, I’m dying. Hey, are you okay?”
Nade approached Iruki, rubbing the back of his neck. Iruki looked up, his eyes wide with disbelief. “No way. Did you fall too?”
“Yeah. My head’s splitting, and I’m done for.”
Iruki frowned. “Why?”
“Why? What do you mean?” Nade replied, bewildered.
Iruki had assumed Nade would breeze through the trial. But as he considered Nade’s words, he had to agree. *Maybe this is just a natural result.*
Nade actively suppressed thoughts about death. Perhaps it was a psychological defense mechanism. If his mind truly dwelled on mortality, he wouldn’t be able to function as a normal human.
“No. Forget it. I’m already dead anyway.”

* * *
Collie glanced back at Alpheas. “Thirty seconds until they enter Stage 5: King of Kings. Shall we activate the observation feed?”
“Hmm. King of Kings…”
Beyond this point, the trial could no longer be explained by theory. Having passed the ‘death threshold’—the official benchmark for professional wizards—the simulation entered a realm of pure psychological and physical trauma. It was necessary to open the feed to monitor the students’ conditions; the mental damage here could be fatal.
“I’ll open the vision.”
“Understood. I’ll activate the master device.”
As Collie manipulated the controls, a large, concave holographic screen materialized in the observation room. During the graduation exam, a massive hologram had been projected from the ceiling from the start, but even with the current setup, the situation inside the simulation could be clearly observed.
[Entering virtual simulation: Stage 5, King of Kings.]
“Ugh! What is that?!”
When the feed flickered to life, the eliminated students gathered before the screen recoiled in horror.
A sea of repulsive insects swarmed endlessly. Hundreds of millions of them moved as one, the sheer mass shifting like a living terrain. The survivors were likely being buried alive somewhere in that writhing mass.
Stage 5: King of Kings.
It was a realm of terror, revulsion, and agony so profound that one would gladly choose death over enduring it.

* * *
The mere sight of tangled insects triggers a primal fear in humans, but this trial went far beyond observation.
The simulation made the insects penetrate skin, nerves, and intestines, delivering visceral, lifelike sensations.
The agony of insects burrowing into every orifice and consuming flesh didn’t simply result in death; it broke the mind.
As more students succumbed, the mounds of insects resembling graves began to churn.
A figure, coated in writhing insects, suddenly scrambled to his feet, screaming and thrashing.
“Aaaaaaa!”
In the observation room, Eider collapsed, his body mirroring the screams erupting from the simulation.
“…Aaaaagh!”
He shook violently, rolling across the floor, clawing at his limbs as he tried to crawl away.
“Ouch! Aww! Aww!”
No one rushed to help him. Approaching someone in such a severe shock state could trigger a worse psychological backlash.
Still, Eider’s innate talent allowed him to gradually regain control.
“Hah… hah…”
At the threshold between the virtual and the real, he panted, tears streaming down his terrified face. “Oh god, that was terrifying.”
“…”
How could it not be?
The eliminated students watching him felt a grim sense of relief that they had only reached Stage 4.
On the other hand, the observers’ expressions were grim.
Eider had consistently scored well in survival evaluations since last year, earning him a spot on the Blue Team.
But as expected, he couldn’t survive Stage 5 either. Meanwhile, Shirone—the one they needed to watch most closely—was still inside the King of Kings.
‘Can Shirone endure what Eider couldn’t?’
Nade thought the same thing. With an ice pack pressed to his neck, he turned to Iruki. “Eider made it to Stage 5, right? He’s young and talented, isn’t he?”
“Regardless of his skills, he’s undeniably the youngest in our graduating class. His raw talent is unquestionable.”
The difficulty of King of Kings dwarfed the previous cutoff. Starting with Eider, students began dropping out in rapid succession.
Canis frowned, straightening her posture. A moment later, Arin slowly opened her eyes. Unlike Eider, they managed their mental state with calm discipline.
“Canis, are you alright?”
“So-so. It’s harder than I expected. After Stage 4, the virtual zone becomes too sensitive.”
“The bugs secrete toxins. It’s hard to keep your mind steady when they burrow into your gut and trigger every nerve.”
‘They’re talking like they’re experiencing paralysis. These guys are insane.’
Arin, an expert in spiritual fields, analyzed their condition. But the eliminated students were beyond absurd. With worms crawling through their intestines and toxins flooding their systems, it was natural they looked unhinged.
But that was precisely why it was called *gyeongji*.
It was a sensation that could never be understood without reaching a depth that defied numerical measurement.
*Everyone is incredible.*
While the eliminated students gathered before the simulation feed, Maya remained at the recovery area. Though she had been eliminated first, her recovery was the slowest, and no one paid her any mind. The harshness of competition was understandable, but it saddened her that she wasn’t even considered a competitor.
She approached Shirone’s friends. They were the only seniors in her year who had shown her any kindness.
“Amy, are you okay? How are you feeling?”
Hearing Maya’s voice, Amy turned her head. “Yeah. I think we’ll be fine. How about you?”
“I’m feeling a bit better now. Sorry. I was eliminated in Stage 2.”
Maya lowered her head, ashamed. She felt terrible for failing to meet Shirone’s expectations, the one who had accepted her onto the team.
“It’s fine. Honestly, I was pretty shaken up too. Don’t worry about it.”
Amy offered comfort. She knew exactly what Stage 2 pain felt like; Maya couldn’t be blamed.
“By the way… will Shirone be okay? He hasn’t come out yet.”
Amy glanced at the observation feed. All the eliminated students had fallen at Stage 5, leaving only two figures lying side by side. Shirone from the Red Team, and Fermi from the Blue Team.
“Anyway, the balance held until the end. Whoever survives there takes the win.”
Maya looked up at the holographic screen. The sight was repulsive even from a distance, but staring at the feed made her stomach churn. *How can anyone survive in a place like that?* She had been terrified even at Stage 2: Ice Jade. Yet someone was still struggling inside such a nightmare. *If it were me, I would have just died.* Maya couldn’t bear to watch, but she forced herself to keep her eyes on the screen. She had to see Shirone through to the end.
“H-hey! Look over there…!”
Boyle pointed at the hologram, his eyes wide with shock.
The writhing mass of insects parted, and Shirone slowly rose to his feet.
“My God…”
Maya’s face drained of color.
The other eliminated students were equally stunned, mouths agape, unable to look away.
Shirone’s condition was so horrific it was hard to look at. In some places, flesh was stripped away to expose bone; in others, skin was swollen and rotting.
Maya couldn’t fathom how the human mind could endure such torture.
Shirone’s body hung limp, his arms drooping and his head bowed.
A faint smile touched Alpheas’s lips. The insects swarmed over Shirone, tearing through his skin without him flinching.
“Remarkable. That’s complete stillness.”
It wasn’t that he felt no pain or revulsion. It was a dehumanized state of absolute focus, maintaining concentration regardless of the circumstances.
