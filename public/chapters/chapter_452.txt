Chapter 452

Second Command (1)

Shirone traveled toward the Second Command headquarters alongside Fleur, guided by Clove and Gardlock. As they rode a beam of light that bridged the distance, fragmented information poured in through a drone relay from base.

“According to recent research,” came Guardlock’s voice, translated through the device, “the angels’ ‘looking down’ isn’t merely observation—it’s a form of spatial consumption. It’s as if they reach into reality and pull the place they desire directly before their eyes, like opening a drawer in your own room and peering inside. So how did the rebels avoid detection?”

The drone, manufactured in Heaven, had been retrofitted with the languages of countless border regions, including the obscure dialects of the Night Blindness sect.

Fleur tilted her head. “Maybe… they locked the drawer?”

Gardlock nodded. “That’s the Mecha method. They generate a self-developed magnetic field that distorts the surrounding landscape, making it impossible for angels to focus their gaze.”

Shirone added, “So the Second Command’s approach is to make *many* drawers?”

“Exactly,” Gardlock replied. “The Second Command operates as a decentralized network—scattered, mobile, and nearly invisible. It’s an old stealth strategy, but far safer than centralized strongholds.”

Fleur asked, “Where exactly is it located?”

“I’ve never been there myself,” Shirone admitted. “After serving in the 13th Battalion, I was transferred directly to First Command. But unlike Mecha, which values leadership and hierarchy, Norr emphasizes raw individual power. Here, the strongest mage becomes the commander—regardless of rank or experience.”

Fleur frowned. “Then the commander must be incredibly strong.”

“So I’ve heard. Her name is Ray Sis. Rumor has it she possesses a Spirit Force level of 32 percent.”

Fleur blinked. “Spirit Force? That’s the measure of Jung affinity, right? And it’s much deeper than mere Spirit Zone alignment?”

“Precisely. And 32 percent is extraordinary. Most Norr mages average only 7 to 8 percent. Even a 1 percent increase in affinity causes magical power to grow exponentially. At 32 percent, a single drop of water conjured by her could sustain a hundred people.”

Shirone tried to picture Ray Sis, though the image remained hazy. Still, he felt a quiet approval. “I’m glad we’re making this journey. Mecha excels against giants, but falters against fairies. Norr, on the other hand, is built to counter them. Unity isn’t just beneficial—it’s essential for survival.”

As they spoke, the beam of light carried them to their destination.

Particles of light danced above a transparent stream that shimmered like crushed diamonds. Guardlock stepped forward, pressing his palm against the water’s surface. With a pulse of energy, he activated the Light Elijah spell, carving a passage through the veil of illusion.

Fleur watched the light seep through his fingers. “Do we have to go further from here? This is already the third checkpoint.”

“This is the end,” Guardlock said.

They had to be cautious. If their route had been compromised, any information could have leaked. These words were spoken only once they were certain they were safe.

Fleur nodded and moved closer to Shirone.

It had been easier before, when seniors like Giraseong were around to shield them. Now, she bore the full responsibility of protecting Shirone.

As she recalled the conversation she’d had with Gaold the previous night—after leaving the bunker where she’d negotiated with Crude—her resolve hardened.

“Fleur.”

“Yes, President of the Association.”

Gaold, who had summoned her, glanced briefly at Shirone before speaking.

“I’m concerned. Other teams are balanced with veterans and newcomers. But you and Shirone… you’re different.”

Fleur didn’t flinch. She knew Gaold wasn’t worried about their safety—he feared the mission failing.

“I understand completely,” she said. “I’ll risk my life to ensure I don’t become a burden to those above me.”

Gaold hesitated. “If things go wrong… you know what happens.”

He didn’t finish.

To Fleur, that hesitation meant everything. Gaold, a man who moved like a machine, had faltered—like a gear grinding to a halt. And in that moment, she felt she had already earned his trust.

She smiled gently. “Don’t worry. If one life must be lost on this mission, I know it should be mine. As a supporter, no matter what happens, I will bring Shirone back—alive.”

Shirone, after all, was the linchpin. The Terminator. The key that could unlock survival even if the rest of the team fell.

It shamed her, as a mage, to weigh a life so coldly. But the mission demanded it.

Gaold studied her in silence. Then, without a word, he turned and walked away—his mouth twisting into a bitter, almost vicious smile.

“Cheuk… trust me.”

The words, barely spoken, struck deep into Fleur’s heart.

She watched his broad back recede, expression blank. Then, slowly, she raised a hand in salute—her smile returning.

*It’s okay to die. I’m on the best team.*

“…There…”

Crude, who had been observing from a distance, turned away with a dry cough. Even in war, he preserved the fragile dignity of human emotion—the belief that people should still live like people.

“If there’s anything uncomfortable…”

“There isn’t,” Fleur said.

Her smile vanished. She turned away, face cold and composed.

Meanwhile, Shirone idly played with a school of killifish, nudging them with his foot. At nineteen, he looked too young for the weight he carried.

*I will protect him.*

But she knew the truth—words like “best team” meant nothing in the crucible of war. The only thing that mattered was completing the mission, by any means necessary. And for that, Shirone needed more than protection. He needed resolve.

Shirone looked up as Fleur approached.

“Listen,” she said. “You’ve come this far. That makes you a mage, even if you haven’t officially graduated. From now on, I’m treating you as a professional. That means no hesitation, no wavering. Stay focused on the mission. I’m not asking you to do anything unreasonable—because in war, nothing is unreasonable. Confusion is fatal. Just hold to your purpose.”

Shirone stared at her, bewildered.

But he understood.

Seniors like Giraseong would die without hesitation to protect him—a boy without even a formal mage’s license.

The most agonizing truth was knowing that—and still being forced to send them to their deaths without flinching.

*I must live. That’s the mission.*

He nodded, jaw set.

“Yes. Don’t worry.”

No one wanted this burden.

But if he didn’t carry it, someone else would have to.

Shirone made his decision. No—his vow.

No matter what happens… do not hesitate.

“Depart.”

A blinding flash of light erupted from Elijah’s core, shooting into the sky like a beacon.

* * *

The world shimmered, bathed in warmth.

When the light faded, the valley with its crystal stream was gone. In its place stood a dense forest, sunlight filtering through a thick canopy.

Fleur looked around. “Is this the headquarters?”

“No,” Gardlock said. “A gatekeeper will lead us the rest of the way. We’re not there yet.”

It was a precaution—another layer of security to prevent pursuit. Their enemies were always watching.

Gardlock raised a finger to his lips and whistled.

The sound magic activated, echoing through the trees like the sudden chorus of a hundred birds.

Moments later, a figure emerged from behind a tree.

“Who are you?” The man’s voice was cold, detached. “Weren’t you supposed to report your arrival?”

He had blue hair, one eyebrow sharply arched. The left side of his head was shaved; the right hung down to his shoulder, partially covering one eye.

Gardlock pointed at Shirone. “From First Command. You’ve probably heard the rumors. This boy—”

“Ah. I see.”

The gatekeeper raised his hand and cast a spell.

*Forest Magic: Oprica.*

The trees behind him shuddered. Vines erupted from the ground, growing at an unnatural speed, lashing toward Shirone and the others.

“Keugh!”

Clove and Gardlock stumbled back, falling to the ground in shock. Shirone and Fleur stood still—the vines had not targeted them.

Had they remained calm a second longer, they might have realized the attack wasn’t meant to harm.

The vines plunged into the earth, then arched upward, weaving into a dome of living wood. In an instant, the group was imprisoned in a cage of twisted, arching branches.

*This is ancient plant magic.*

Shirone recognized the technique. Even certified Fourth-Grade mages in Kazura had struggled to achieve such rapid growth. This man, though perhaps not stronger than Bossun, wielded ancient magic amplified by high Jung affinity.

*It won’t be easy.*

Shirone frowned. From the start, there had been no real negotiation—only hostility from a group that had chosen armed resistance.

The gatekeeper cast Oprica again, summoning another vine and pointing it at the group like a weapon.

“What business do traitors of Norr have here?”

Clove shouted, gripping the bars of their prison. “What is this?! Do you even know who this is? This is Shirone—the Light of Area 73!”

The more hostile the faction, the fiercer the espionage.

The gatekeeper *did* know who Shirone was.

“And?”

“So what? Aren’t you at war? Don’t you know what his presence does for rebel morale? And yet you question his arrival?”

A spear-like branch shot through a gap in the cage, stopping just short of Clove’s face.

“Kuk!”

She recoiled, slamming her back against the opposite wall.

Oprica required over 10 percent Jung affinity to cast. With only 7 percent, Clove couldn’t hope to resist the gatekeeper’s overwhelming presence.

“The Light of Area 73?” the gatekeeper sneered. “When did you start criticizing Norr? When did you decide to fight alongside us? We don’t need Mecha’s mechanical might. How can you possibly challenge angels with nothing but gears and wires?”

Shirone stepped forward. “We’re not here to debate you. We came to speak with your captain. I doubt you have the authority to decide the fate of all Norr.”

The gatekeeper turned his gaze fully to Shirone.

He’d heard the legends—the boy who defeated Lee Ki-rin, the fairy department’s leader. But Norr valued strength above myth. To them, the “Light of Area 73” was either a fraud or a child drunk on power.

“Maybe not,” the gatekeeper said. “But as gatekeeper, I have authority. Whether you’re Nephilim or the so-called Light, strength decides everything in war. Mecha might cry in their wooden prisons, but Norr doesn’t even snort.”

Shirone sighed. He raised his palm and placed it against the prison.

Light gathered in his hand, glowing faintly.

The gatekeeper took a step back.

He’d heard the rumors—Shirone could wield light without Jung affinity. And destroy anything with it.

“Hah! Let’s see—!”

The gatekeeper, a master of ancient magic entrusted with guarding the rebel base, summoned every vine around him. He twisted them into sharp spears, ready to strike.

*What now?*

But the counterattack never came.

The light in Shirone’s palm darkened, shifting from gold to a deep, pulsing black. The vines began to writhe, their forms twisting grotesquely.

*Amgu.*

The gatekeeper gasped, releasing his hold. He stared in disbelief as the thick, hemispherical prison groaned, its structure warping.

*Crunch. Sigh.*

With a sound like bones breaking, the cage tore from the earth.

*Deuk. Deuk. Deuk.*

It twisted violently, folding inward like a vortex, and was sucked into a rift in space—devoured completely, as if swallowed whole by an invisible maw.

Clove and Gardlock turned pale.

They’d seen Shirone shatter a rock with light before, but this—this was different.

“Master,” Fleur whispered, “what kind of magic was that?”

Shirone didn’t answer.

The gatekeeper could only stare, dumbfounded.

And every Norr mage watching from the shadows now fixed their eyes on Shirone—not with suspicion, but awe.
