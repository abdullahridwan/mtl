Chapter 439  
Task Force (1)  
~*~

The Magic Association.

Sakiri, an investigator from the Inspection Division 3 who had previously interrogated Kanis and Arin, made his way toward the underground prison camp reserved for special criminals under the Association’s jurisdiction. Walking beside him was Blenner, the Third Deputy Director of the National Intelligence Service.

The Third Division of the NIS—officially known as the Daecheonguk Information Management Department—was a covert organization composed entirely of black agents whose existence and ranks were classified from the public. The fact that its head had personally come to the Magic Association could mean only one thing: they were after something powerful enough to stop Gaold’s plan.

That something was the confession of Isabelle, a Third-Class Archmage.

“Honestly, it’s a mess. A complete mess,” Blenner muttered, adjusting his coat. “Gaold—what a disgrace to the kingdom. I never liked him. After dragging everyone through hell, he just walks away like it’s nothing.”

Sakiri didn’t respond. He knew better than to trust Blenner’s polished demeanor. Everyone in the Inspection Division was aware that Rufist, the current interim head of the Magic Association, had deep ties to Blenner’s faction.

“Isabelle, though… she’s something else,” Blenner continued, almost admiringly. “Beautiful face, sharp mind. A real gem. Pity wizards like her don’t come around often.”

It grated on Sakiri to hear a non-magician speak so casually about a Third-Class Archmage—one he himself respected. But he kept his expression neutral. Emotions were tools, not truths. When necessary, he could shut them off completely.

“I’ve heard you’re the best on this floor,” Blenner said, clapping a hand on Sakiri’s shoulder. “You handle the interrogation. I’ll cover the aftermath. Just make sure she talks.”

“It won’t be easy,” Sakiri replied flatly. “Even if she’s not a combat mage, she’s still one of the most powerful wizards in the Association. She’s not the type to break under pressure or give false testimony out of fear.”

Blenner chuckled. “That’s exactly why I brought *you*. Sakiri of the Third Inspection Division—the man who says no criminal can stay silent once he gets his hands on them.”

“I’ll do my best.”

Sakiri’s voice was dry, his mind already working.

Rufist had long seen Gaold as a thorn in his side. In private, he’d made it clear he wanted Gaold eliminated—by any means necessary. Only then could he solidify his position as the permanent head of the Magic Association.

But things hadn’t gone as planned.

Gaold’s reputation within the Association wasn’t as tarnished as Rufist had hoped. Yes, he was reckless, volatile, even dangerous—but he was also undeniably strong, and in his own twisted way, honest. That unpredictability had thrown Rufist’s calculations off balance.

Worse still, Isabelle had publicly defended Gaold during the National Emergency Hearing, risking her life to vouch for his innocence. That act alone had shaken the Association’s leadership. A Third-Class Archmage, one of their most respected figures, had stood by Gaold when no one else would.

Now, with public opinion wavering, Rufist was desperate. He needed a justification—something undeniable—to mobilize the full force of the Association against Gaold. That was why he’d reached out to Blenner.

And why Blenner had come.

Sakiri narrowed his eyes slightly.

“This confession,” he said carefully. “How far should it go? Is it enough if she admits to being part of the Old Guard and sheltering Gaold?”

Blenner smirked. “A little higher, I think. We need her to confess to involvement in the plot to assassinate Her Highness. Whether she orchestrated it or just funded it—she’ll say it was her doing.”

Sakiri understood.

They weren’t just looking for leverage. They wanted a clean slate—every trace of dissent erased.

No one needed to know what happened down here.

That was why Sakiri had been assigned to conduct the interrogation alone. No observers. No records beyond what *they* allowed.

“As long as I don’t die, I can do anything,” Blenner said, almost cheerfully. “Just get the confession. I’ve installed video recording devices. Activate Obscura B and send the footage to me. And remember—you’re special.”

Sakiri’s mind clicked into place.

The entire torture session would be recorded. And Obscura B? That wasn’t just a storage device. It was a private archive—accessible only to certain high-ranking officials.

A secret recording of a beautiful, high-ranking archmage being broken… who wouldn’t enjoy that?

While the kingdom teetered on the edge of chaos—while Gaold’s fate hung in the balance—some powerful figures were more interested in their own pleasures.

Sakiri almost smiled.

Perhaps that, too, was human nature.

If you have the power to do something, you do it.

They reached the prison cell for special offenders. Sakiri stopped before the heavy iron door, his gaze fixed on the cold, unyielding metal.

Blenner showed no interest in what lay beyond.

“Get it done quickly,” he said. “You have 48 hours to secure a confession.”

“Don’t worry,” Sakiri replied. “I came all this way for this.”

Blenner patted his shoulder once more, then turned and walked away. The echo of his footsteps faded down the corridor.

Only when the silence settled did Sakiri approach the door.

He placed his hand on the security device embedded in the wall. The Spirit Zone activated, scanning his identity. With a metallic clank, the lock disengaged.

He pulled the heavy iron door open.

Inside, Isabelle sat on a simple steel chair bolted to the floor, her back against the wall. Once the pride of the Magic Association—impeccable in dress, demeanor, and discipline—she now looked broken. Her hair hung in tangled strands over her face, her wrists bound by mana-suppressing cuffs.

Three video recording devices were mounted in the room—one on each side wall, and one near the door. The one facing her blinked red, actively recording.

“It’s strange,” Sakiri said, stepping inside. “Meeting you here.”

Isabelle didn’t respond. She sat motionless, her breath shallow, her eyes empty.

Sakiri ignored her silence and began checking the devices. He unbuttoned his sleeve as he moved.

“Do you know what terrifies me most about humanity?” he asked, not expecting an answer. “It’s that we’re given unlimited freedom. Truly. Humans can do *anything*—no matter how absurd, how cruel. You can strangle a child who’s never harmed you. You can burn down a city just to watch it burn. And you’ll do it, not because you’re forced, but because you *can*.”

He turned off the device on the left. Then the one on the right.

Finally, he stood before the last active recorder.

“Reset Obscura B,” he said, pressing the button.

The red light died. The device powered down, ready to restart.

Only then did Isabelle lift her head.

Her eyes—once sharp and commanding—now held only resignation. But they focused on Sakiri, clear and steady.

“Tomorrow,” she said quietly, “would have been your son’s birthday.”

Sakiri froze.

He had two daughters now. But yes—there had been a son. Born with Tris Disease, a rare illness that slowly consumed the body. He had lived for two years. Died two years after that.

Sakiri had been a regular at the memorial shrine ever since.

He exhaled slowly.

“Well,” he said, “that’s life. Shall we begin?”

He reached for the switch on the wall.

*Click.*

Overhead, the incandescent lights flared to life, flooding the cell with harsh, clinical brightness.

*Thud! Thud! Thud! Thud!*

The walls groaned as hidden panels slid open, revealing a long corridor lined with every imaginable instrument of torture—blades, clamps, electric rods, psychological stimulators.

“I usually prefer dark torture chambers,” Sakiri said, walking toward the rack. “But I like working in the light. Torture is a kind of communion. It might feel… undignified… to see your body break down in full view. But you’ll get used to it. Just give up.”

He picked up a pair of surgical scissors, testing the edge with his thumb.

“Now,” he said, turning back to Isabelle. “Where should we start? What are you weakest against? Pain? Foreign objects? Fear? Bodily distortion? Or… shame?”

Isabelle spoke, her voice calm.

“You’re kind, Sakiri.”

He paused.

“I work with duality,” he replied. “No point in tormenting you before we begin. Let’s start with water torture. For the recording’s sake, it’s better if you’re clean first.”

Isabelle tilted her head slightly.

“If not… why are you hesitating?”

Sakiri’s fingers stilled on the scissors.

“Hesitating?”

“You know the truth. No matter what you do to me, I won’t recant my testimony from the hearing. But your skill—*The Balance of Truth*—is different. I don’t know its activation conditions, but you could use it right now. Why waste time with theatrics?”

Sakiri stared at the scissors in his hand.

Then he sighed.

“Haa… as long as people like you or me keep surviving, I suppose.”

*Clatter.*

The scissors dropped to the floor.

Sakiri stood still for a long moment. Then, slowly, he turned to face Isabelle.

“… Why did you do it?”

Isabelle blinked, as if the question surprised her.

“Why did you come here? You’re not even part of the Royal Guard. You’re using Gaold, Rufist, the entire Association—as tools for your own ends. Rufist seized power for ambition. Gaold fights for revenge. But you… why did you protect Gaold?”

Isabelle was silent for a moment. Then she smiled—bitter, faint.

“I don’t know either.”

“You… don’t know?”

“I don’t, Sakiri. It wasn’t a calculated decision. Yes, both Gaold and Rufist are selfish. Arrogant. But when I had to choose… I just liked Gaold a little more than Rufist.”

She looked up at the ceiling.

“That’s all.”

Sakiri clenched his jaw.

Then, without a word, he turned and walked back to the torture rack. His face was tight, twisted—not with anger, but with something deeper. Disgust. Disappointment.

*Damn it. There was such a simple way.*

Since when had mages become puppets for politicians?

Where were the wizards who once fought with iron will, who stood by their beliefs no matter the cost?

“… 48 hours,” he said, picking up a long, needle-like awl—20 centimeters of polished steel.

“In that time, Rufist will move the Association against Gaold, regardless of public opinion. If you confess, it’ll be faster. But if you hold out…”

Isabelle finally looked at him.

“Forty-eight hours. That’s tight.”

“Rufist is impatient. He’d prefer to become president with the Association’s full support. But as long as Gaold lives, his position is unstable. So yes—if you endure, you buy time.”

Sakiri ran a finger along the awl’s tip, then fixed Isabelle with a cold stare.

“I’ll preserve your dignity—the last shred of it—as a human being, as a woman. Not because I like you. But because I refuse to let perverted old men get their kicks from watching a Third-Class Archmage break. The pain, though… that I can’t spare you.”

Isabelle didn’t flinch. She simply looked up at the ceiling again, lost in thought.

*Damn you, Gaold. How many people are you dragging into this?*

*… Why did I do it?*

*Maybe I’ve caught your madness too.*

The red light on the recording device blinked back on.

Sakiri activated the other two devices. Then he stepped into the center of the room—where all three lenses focused on him and Isabelle.

He cleared his throat.

“This is the official interrogation of Isabelle, Manager of the Magic Association’s Library Division. Interrogator: Sakiri, Third Inspection Division. Commencing now.”

For the first time since her capture, Isabelle’s eyes flickered with life.

*Go, Gaold. Go beyond good and evil. Transcend the limits of mankind. Reach the ultimate realm.*

Sakiri grabbed her hair and yanked her head back.

Her neck arched, exposed.

*Hold on. Just 48 hours.*

A scream tore through the prison corridor.

And deep beneath the Magic Association, the recording devices rolled on.
