Chapter 436

Final Decision (6)

Thud. Thump.

The buildings of Istas shifted and stacked upon one another, reassembling into a colossal, mass-like structure—two thick, pillar-like legs rooted into the earth, supporting a towering form that resembled a slumbering giant.

Sein stepped forward and opened a warehouse door embedded in the giant’s right leg. He turned to Alfeas.

“If we go in, reactivate Istas.”

Only those who had not ascended to Heaven could verify the true nature of the labyrinth’s space-time. At least, that was Alfeas’s belief—and he intended to fulfill his role.

“Let’s do it.”

Gaold’s usual smirk had vanished. His expression was grim, focused.

*We’re finally going to see Miro.*

It was almost fortunate she was in samadhi. The rage he’d suppressed for twenty years threatened to erupt.

Once everyone had entered the warehouse, Alfeas reignited Istas.

Following Sein’s lead, the cube-shaped structures began their intricate dance—merging, separating, shifting in complex patterns.

Thud. Creak. Thud.

The heartbeat of Istas pulsed through the air.

With each step, Sein’s amber eyes flickered. His left iris twitched side to side in precise 15-degree increments, while the right rotated in segmented bursts, each section accelerating independently. He adjusted his stride length, not his speed—a method of manipulating time by controlling space.

“This way,” he said.

He turned sharply at the warehouse’s center, opening a door that led not to an exit, but to another warehouse. The passage connected seamlessly.

The moment they crossed the threshold, the warehouse they’d just left collapsed behind them, swallowed by shifting blocks that pushed it far out of reach.

This process repeated endlessly—door after door, warehouse after warehouse, each step deeper into the labyrinth’s folded reality.

*Incredible. I couldn’t replicate this even if I were a Servant.*

The world moved. The warehouse moved. Time moved.

Shirone felt the very concept of space unraveling. Sein wasn’t just walking—he was navigating four-dimensional space-time.

When Sein finally stopped, Shirone’s eyes shimmered with emotion.

Before them hung a sign:

**Supernatural Psychic Research Society**

It was crooked, as always—constantly reset, yet always tilting back on its own, defying balance.

The door creaked open. Inside, the floor still bore the scuff marks of agents who had stumbled through in haste.

Equations Iruki had written on the blackboard were half-erased. The picture books Nade had refused to throw away lay in tattered pieces.

Sein walked to the sofa.

“The Supernatural Psychic Science Research Society,” he said. “Twelve seconds into the future. At that space-time coordinate lies the upper layer of Istas. From here, Armand—it’s your turn.”

Armand stepped past Sein.

As a Scale Wizard who manipulated time and space, he already understood what the upper layer was.

“Seven seconds,” he began. “Six. Five.”

A rushing wind roared, as if they’d been caught in a storm’s current. The air itself seemed to warp.

This was the effect of approaching the overlapping space-time coordinates.

The furniture and papers of the research society blurred, their forms melting under the pressure. On the table before the sofa, a stone gate flickered into view—an afterimage of the Guffin’s Door Shirone had seen in Galliant.

Whoosh! Whoosh! Whoosh!

“Four seconds. Three seconds.”

The world around them faded, growing pale and translucent. In contrast, the stone gate sharpened, its colors deepening, its edges solidifying.

Objects flickered, then vanished entirely from the shifting space-time.

“Two seconds. One second.”

The final moment arrived. The surroundings disappeared completely. Only the stone gate remained, now fully materialized.

“Now.”

Armand cast his spell.

**Stop.**

“Oh my god…”

Shirone’s breath caught.

The sensation was the same for both Etel and Shirone—overwhelming, profound.

The upper layer of Istas, hidden in a specific point of space and time, had been frozen in place by Armand’s magic.

Twenty years ago, a genius girl had shocked the world. And humanity had imprisoned her in a sealed coordinate, a place no one could find.

This was the truth behind Istas’s upper echelons—long rumored, never confirmed, until now.

*So that’s what he meant.*

Shirone finally understood Sein’s words.

The origin of the cube. A place countless agents had searched for in vain.

Armand turned from the gate.

“As long as time remains stopped, the door will stay open. But you should move quickly. If the spell breaks, anyone still inside will be trapped in the labyrinth of time and space.”

The risk seemed remote, but the stakes were life and death. Caution was warranted.

Shirone had escaped the maze’s space-time before—but only because he was an Unlocker. And Miro, currently in deep samadhi, couldn’t assist.

Gaold wasn’t thinking about risks.

His face was flushed, his heart pounding. The name of the first-class archmage—colorless, weightless—echoed in his mind.

“Let’s go, Sein.”

“Alright.”

Sein’s irises spun in opposite directions—a sign of simultaneous calm and excitement.

It was this perfect balance of emotion and reason that had elevated him to the pinnacle of anti-magic mastery.

“You said Shirone, right?” Armand said. “I hope he’s the one.”

The upper layer’s door, like the Guffin’s Door, required an Unlocker to open.

There were two Unlockers on the team. But with Armand maintaining the stop magic, the task fell to Shirone.

Shirone activated the Immortal Function.

The runes on the stone gate flared with light, then shattered into tiny cubes that scattered like dust, forming a sphere before vanishing.

As the gate dissolved, a massive black hole opened in its place.

“Who… goes in first?”

No one knew why, but the question felt necessary.

It didn’t matter now—but in the future, this moment might be remembered as something significant.

“The one who broke the seal should enter first,” Armand said, smiling. “Shirone, you go.”

He gently pushed Shirone forward.

Shirone hesitated.

Then stepped through.

Darkness.

Then—light.

A vast temple unfolded before him, its two-kilometer-high pillars stretching toward a ceiling lost in shadow.

There was no doubt. This was the labyrinth of time and space.

*I never thought I’d return here.*

One by one, the others followed through the black hole.

Just as Shirone had been the first time, everyone stood in stunned silence, overwhelmed by the sheer scale of the temple—something beyond human comprehension.

“What…? Wait, but…”

Shirone spun around, scanning the group. “What’s wrong?”

“The maze,” Gaold said. “It’s not here.”

“Shirone, maybe this is…” Etel began.

“No,” Shirone said. “This is the maze’s structure. It’s exactly as it was a year ago.”

Gaold cast Fly.

With a sharp pop, his body shot forward, jetting through the temple at blinding speed. His magic wasn’t flight in the traditional sense—it was a burst of compressed air, propelling him like a missile.

He swept the entire chamber, then returned, his face twisted in fury.

“Those damn bastards…”

“Calm down,” Sein said. “Nothing’s happened yet. It’s just… there’s no maze in the maze’s space-time.”

There was no labyrinth in the space-time of the labyrinth.

It was impossible.

No one could enter or leave this place. And if Miro was in samadhi, she couldn’t have escaped on her own.

No—had that been possible, Gaold wouldn’t have waited twenty years.

“It’s been hijacked,” Sein said.

“Maybe Heaven took Miro.”

Gaold’s voice was low. “Is that even possible? Not a single molecule has ever crossed from Heaven.”

“There is.”

Armand bent down, picking up a fragment from the floor—a piece of stone that had broken off the ceiling during a space-time disturbance.

He rubbed it between his fingers.

“I heard there was a crack in the labyrinth’s space-time recently. The Ruler of Law even visited the Tormia Kingdom. The Akeanis Shrine’s mage handled it and filed the report.”

“But that was just a mental projection. To abduct a living human, a crack isn’t enough.”

“The Ivory Tower agrees. But that’s human logic. Heaven—especially the archangels—might have found another way.”

Sein rested his chin on his hand.

“Why take Miro?”

It seemed like a strange question.

Heaven was preparing for the final war. They had long sought to infiltrate the mortal world, ever since the labyrinth’s space-time was created.

Shirone frowned. “Isn’t it obvious? To invade this world.”

Etel shook her head. “No. Maybe not.”

Sein nodded. “Taking Miro alone won’t accomplish that. Even killing her wouldn’t destroy the labyrinth’s space-time.”

Shirone’s eyes widened. “Indestructible?”

“The space-time of the maze is based on *cowardice*—a dimension built from fear. It endlessly generates new layers within itself. That infinity creates a boundary between space and time. And Miro amplified it further with Sammaegyeong. The theory is that even if Miro dies, the inertia of samadhi-gyeong will continue to reinforce the space-time. Honestly, I don’t fully understand it.”

It was like the mind after death—consciousness weakened, pulled by the strongest inertia of one’s life.

That was why, even if the Virtual Zone was deactivated, those who couldn’t escape fear would remain buried in their own minds forever.

“No,” Shirone said. “I think I get it.”

Sein didn’t scoff. But his eyebrow twitched—the closest he ever came to surprise.

At Alfeas Magic School, only one person had ever passed *cowardice*: Miro. The memory of that uproar—the birth of a world-class genius—was still fresh.

*Hmph. Shirone might actually deserve it.*

Gaold cut in. “Fine. They can’t kill the maze. But they took Miro anyway. They must have found a way. We don’t have time. As soon as the team’s ready, we go to Heaven.”

Sein didn’t object. He had walked the black line for twenty years to reach this moment.

But still—

*Is this… normal?*

The feeling nagged at him.

Was today’s outcome just a tragic coincidence—random, inevitable?

Then why did it feel so wrong?

Why did his mind itch with the sense that something had been twisted, subtly, over the past nineteen years?

*Why now? Why this? Is it even rational to think this way? The world doesn’t bend to desire. Attributing meaning to chance is superstition.*

He wanted to believe that.

But Sein couldn’t let it go.

It wasn’t illogical. It was intuition—something deeper, quieter, that slipped past reason.

*Something… is off.*
