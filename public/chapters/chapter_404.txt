Chapter 404  
One More Step (1)  
~*~

The senior students were summoned by Collie one by one for individual interviews.

Shirone’s rank in the graduating class was twenty-third, so his turn came when the interview order reached number twenty-three.

The official purpose of the interviews was to assess each student’s character and academic attitude, but no matter how the school phrased it, the real purpose had not changed: registering their majors.

“Nice to see you, Shirone.”

Collie offered him a handshake. Unlike at yesterday’s briefing, his expression held the same warmth and benevolence Shirone remembered from before.

But Shirone no longer behaved like a child in front of him.

“Yes. I look forward to working with you.”

They sat facing each other across the table. Collie looked down at the papers in front of him and adjusted his glasses.

“Take this first. It’s the schedule for your individual combat matches.”

The document trembled slightly in Shirone’s hand. It felt as though he had been handed a confidential file that no one was supposed to see.

The papers listed the dates of the matches and the opponents he would face. His first opponent was Dorothy.

‘Dorothy…’

The strange, otherworldly girl who always carried a tin doll in her arms.

In terms of combat power, Shirone did not think he would be pushed back. But as Flew had advised him, there was no need to inflate his opponents in his imagination before fighting them.

Next, he checked his friends’ schedules.

Iruki, Nade, and Amy. All three had been scheduled for the latter half of the year, after classes resumed following vacation.

‘Competing against friends is going to be another variable.’

Personally, Shirone intended to do his best no matter who his opponent was.

However, if the other students formed factions and began sharing points among themselves, he would have to think about things differently.

Collie pulled out the next document.

“Now, we need to register your major…”

It was understandable why his words trailed off.

The Red Line’s database had evaluation criteria for Unlockers, but Shirone’s ability was unique.

God Particles, which gave mass to light.

The magic Shirone produced through that principle was fundamentally different from any existing magic in the world.

In short, there was no precedent by which to evaluate him.

“After the senior class promotion test, the school sent an official letter to the Red Line. We judged that you would need a separate evaluation form. But even that is rather ambiguous. In a sense, your major belongs to a field that only you can study in the entire world.”

During the senior class promotion test, Shirone had been evaluated on his unique ability, the spells he could use with it, his achievement in the Spirit Zone, and his theoretical understanding.

“The only things the school can confirm are the spells you used at that time. The problem is that there’s no basis for determining how your magic will develop from here, so the standards become vague.”

“Yes. That makes sense.”

Even if Shirone had been the evaluator, he would have faced the same problem.

If they evaluated Ataraxia, what level would count as master difficulty? What about Photon Cannon? Would it be judged as a narrow shot or a wide-area attack?

And the school did not even know he had developed a spell called Gwangik.

Perhaps not all of those circumstances would work in his favor.

“So it seems the Association settled on a principle. In other words, they will measure only the phenomena created by the combination of light and mass. Read through it once and tell me what you think.”

Shirone examined the evaluation sheet Collie handed him. Twenty items regarding God Particles were listed one after another.

There were fifteen active magic items and five passive magic items. The active items were mainly for attack and defense, while the passive items were related to movement.

As Shirone read through them one by one, his expression hardened.

It did not take long for his face to twist completely.

‘Are they seriously asking me to do this?’

It was too difficult.

Of course, it looked as if he could easily earn three or four points on each item, and there were even some high-level items where he might be able to score seven or more. However, level ten—the master difficulty—was far beyond what he could reach at his current level.

Shirone’s gaze traveled down the page and stopped at the note at the bottom of the form.

All items measure magic to which God Particles have been applied. In addition, attack magic will only be recognized for evaluation if it has an impact value of 20 or higher.

An impact value of 20 was roughly the average force of the Photon Cannons Shirone had cast in real battles so far.

In other words, even if God Particles were applied, if the mass was reduced to an extremely low level, the spell would not meet the evaluation standard.

“Whew.”

Shirone returned to the first item.

Item 1 measured rate of fire, and even the lowest difficulty—worth only one point—required a staggering sixty shots per minute.

‘Sixty shots per minute, each with an impact value of 20?’

Of course, the current Shirone could do it. But that did not mean it would be easy.

When he fought the Ice Queen in the ancient ruins, he had shown a rate of fire of about one hundred and twenty shots per minute.

The problem was that he had achieved that by pushing himself to the limit.

According to the master difficulty standard, he would need two hundred shots per minute.

To receive ten points, he would have to cast attack magic more than three times per second and maintain that pace for a full minute.

Shirone narrowed one eye and scratched his head.

Collie seemed to understand how he felt. He clasped his hands together and waited for Shirone to organize his thoughts.

Item 2, projectile velocity, was no different.

The master difficulty was 140 meters per second. By Shirone’s estimate, that was twice as fast as his current Photon Cannon.

‘What is this? Why did they set it like this?’

According to Amy’s evaluation sheet, even fire magic was not held to such an extreme standard.

For fire magic, it was understandable that the heat-related item would be difficult, since caloric output was the core of flame magic. But the other items were set at a level based on the average ability of students.

God Particles, on the other hand, were being treated too harshly. It was no different from telling him not to graduate from magic school this year.

One only had to look at the passive item for “illumination.”

The master difficulty required a staggering 500,000 lumens. That was a number impossible to reach with ordinary Shining Magic.

However, the Association had judged that it was possible if he handled God Particles.

With Shining Impact, he had raised the illumination to 300,000 lumens.

But 500,000 lumens…

Even if the people at the Association were mistaken, they were mistaken by an absurd margin.

At 500,000 lumens, it would no longer be an auxiliary spell. It would simply be attack magic.

It would be powerful enough to penetrate the eyelids and deal fatal damage to the retina unless the target covered their eyes with their hands.

Shirone looked at Collie and pointed to the evaluation sheet.

“Sir, this is…”

“Yes. These are the evaluation items presented by the Association to strengthen and measure your main specialty. Is the difficulty higher than you expected?”

“It’s not just high. Even if the graduating class is treated as semi-professional, this is a bit…”

Collie nodded as if he understood.

“Shirone, I believe you are an excellent student. But when it comes to your main specialty, I cannot say how much better you are than others. There is no one to compare you to.”

Collie looked down at his own copy of the evaluation sheet and continued.

“From the Association’s point of view, setting the difficulty too high does not create a particularly dangerous problem. You are the only one who suffers from it. Lowering the difficulty, on the other hand, requires caution. What if they set the standards too low and you pass too easily? That could lead directly to questions of fairness across every magic school under the Red Line.”

Shirone understood that there were practical issues. It was only natural that the Association would be more cautious about setting the standard too low than too high.

“But couldn’t the opposite also be true? If I’m the only one given an unusually high difficulty, wouldn’t that be unfair as well?”

Collie’s lips shifted slightly from side to side.

“Yes. The Association is conservative. I am conservative too. In your words, you might call us kkondae.”

“No, I wasn’t trying to criticize—”

“That is the first assumption.”

When Collie cut him off, Shirone immediately closed his mouth.

“Now I’ll tell you the second assumption. Perhaps the evaluation sheet you are looking at right now is the correct standard.”

“The correct… standard?”

“It is not about seeing how much stronger you are than other students. It is about determining the level of your major itself. For example, fire magic is more difficult than air magic. Everyone has access to air, but not everyone has access to fire. Differences in affinity lead to differences in omnipotence, and omnipotence is closely connected to talent. That is why the main-skill evaluation for fire magic is more difficult than the one for atmospheric magic. On average, there are more talented people in that field. Do you think that is unfair?”

Shirone did not answer.

“Shirone, society is not so simple. Suppose the Association releases an official notice saying they urgently need one fire mage. Dozens of fire mages will gather, and the one with the highest level will be chosen. But none of the rejected applicants will claim it is unfair because they are stronger than other kinds of mages. That is the kind of world this is.”

Shirone swallowed.

“It does not matter that your difficulty level is higher than someone else’s. The ability you possess is different from theirs to begin with. You have a powerful ability that no one else can imitate. So this is what the people at the Association are saying: if you can give mass to light, then shouldn’t a professional in that field be able to do at least this much?”

“…”

Collie folded his hands on the table.

“Now you have a choice. As your homeroom teacher, I have presented two assumptions about this evaluation sheet. One: the Association is conservative and full of old fogeys. Two: the Association’s assessment is correct. The judgment is yours. If you choose the former, you can register for a different department as your major. If you choose the latter, you can accept this evaluation.”
