Chapter 461  
Opportunity for Revenge (3)  
~*~

Deep beneath the Rebel 2nd HQ, hidden within a secret underground laboratory, Reisis did not reprimand Shirone and Fleur for the deaths of two SWAT team members.

Though some of his subordinates had demanded Shirone be removed from duty to preserve the commander’s honor, their curiosity had quickly shifted to a far greater matter—the confirmation of Babel’s existence.

After confining Shirone and Fleur to their quarters, Reisis had taken fresh blood samples from both.

On the surface, the reason given was precaution—concern over possible infection by an unknown pathogen. But the truth was far more calculated. Reisis intended to use Shirone’s blood to enter Babel and secure a clone of the transformed mage.

*Angel’s Tomb… Was that truly Babel? I always thought it was just a myth.*

But now, with Shirone having returned from that place, Reisis could no longer dismiss it as legend.

*If the myth is real, then I have a good idea of what spirit Shirone encountered there.*

He walked to a heavy iron cage on the far side of the lab. Inside, a grotesque creature thrashed violently against the barbed wire lining the bars.

“Kick! Kiik!”

Each impact sent a shudder through the biologists monitoring it, their pens trembling as they recorded the creature’s behavior.

Garas—the King of Breeding.

An extraterrestrial species whose reproductive instinct was 100,000 times stronger than that of humans. It could mate with up to 1,027 known Norse species, including humans—male or female. Biologically, only males existed, yet they recognized all genders as potential mates.

According to academic reports, successful reproduction between Garas and humans occurred in just 2% of attempts. But the species had no fixed reproductive boundaries. It could crossbreed with anything—from insects to colossal beasts.

What made this possible was the “protein palace,” an organ capable of manipulating amino acid sequences, combined with rapid cell division that allowed instant formation of reproductive organs.

Despite possessing intelligence ten times that of humans, Garas had never developed an independent civilization—its entire existence consumed by the drive to reproduce.

Was this the ultimate expression of evolved intellect? Or merely a natural adaptation to its hostile environment?

No one knew. But one thing was certain: Garas was a species defined solely by reproduction. It was classified as a highest-risk quarantined entity in Purgatory, its ability to break down biological barriers between species making it a catastrophic threat if ever released.

“Commander,” one biologist said, voice tight with unease, “why bring something like this here? If they escape, command will fall. No—this entire region will become a Garas colony.”

“It’s under control,” Reisis replied calmly. “And without this, we can’t create Shirone’s clone.”

Earlier attempts to combine Shirone’s genetic material with a Norse egg had failed due to subtle evolutionary mismatches. But Garas’ genetic adaptability could bridge that gap perfectly.

The biologist stared, stunned. “Then… we’ll begin today?”

“No. First, clone Fleur.”

The scientist’s face paled. “Fleur? But… no, that’s—”

The combination of Shirone, Fleur, and Garas could produce explosive results—clones with unprecedented potential.

Even among scholars who had long abandoned ethics for progress, a deep unease settled in their chests.

They were still human. Flesh and blood. They feared pain. They feared death.

*Is this woman even sane?*

That was the only thought echoing in the biologist’s mind.

“Commander,” he said slowly, “do we really need to go this far?”

Reisis cut him off. “This is no longer just research. It’s an exhibition. Victory demands sacrifice. You’re helping me because you’ve already accepted that.”

“But this—”

“Enough.” Reisis’ voice turned cold. “I don’t have time. If the Babel Shirone found is the one I know…”

“You mean the weapon the ancient Gaians built to attack Heaven?”

“Exactly.”

The biologist fell silent.

Reisis added, “And don’t worry. There won’t be any need to alert headquarters. The one who sealed Babel away was Archangel Kariel himself.”

* * *

Niflheim.

The frozen world of the dead.

Before Gaold stretched an endless ice sheet, ringed in the distance by jagged, frozen mountain ranges. It was a realm so merciless that any ordinary creature would freeze solid before even glimpsing its horrors.

Only because Zulu had summoned a Tier 2 monster—the Salamander—had they survived. The three-meter-long beast lay flat on the ice, radiating heat across their small camp.

Yet even its fire struggled. The ice refused to melt, not even dampening. The Salamander itself shivered, its flame flickering. Each time it weakened, the creature’s true form—a massive, lizard-like being—briefly revealed itself.

Gaold exhaled, a plume of frost bursting from his lips. “How many?”

Kang Nan checked the pouch at her belt. “Twenty-four.”

“Right.”

Black Elixir—collected slower than expected.

Crude, the first commander of the Rebels, had claimed a 50% success rate in capturing demons and obtaining the elixir. In reality, the odds were closer to 20%.

Still, twenty-four vials were a fortune. On the mainland, they could buy anything.

But compared to the rewards rumored at the end of penance, it was nothing. And the cost had been immense—six days of relentless travel through Niflheim, draining their strength with every step.

*Cold.*

Kang Nan exhaled another long cloud of frost.

To wander Niflheim for six days was madness for a human. Zulu hadn’t slept once—she’d maintained the Salamander’s summoning without pause.

Her gaze drifted to Gaold’s broad back.

*How cold must he be?*

If Kang Nan was suffering, Gaold endured a thousand times more. Her fingertips were numb, nearly paralyzed by frostbite. Gaold’s entire body was exposed, yet he showed no sign of pain.

*How can you not even flinch? How are you still standing?*

“At this rate,” Gaold said, voice low, “we’ll collapse before we reach our goal.”

He turned to their guide—a gaunt old man with a rat-like face and a scar running down his cheek.

The man survived here by having traded everything for a contract with the Ice Spirit. But even with such a pact, without sufficient magical skill, finding anything in Purgatory was impossible.

So he worked as a guide—leading the desperate and greedy into the frozen hellscape.

The job paid well, but the risks were extreme.

The terms were harsh: the guide took no part in combat, but claimed half of all harvested items.

Gaold had initially refused such unfair terms—until he realized how low the actual chance of obtaining Black Elixir truly was.

Niflheim was vast. The frequency of encountering evil spirits was shockingly low.

Wander aimlessly, and exhaustion or freezing would claim you long before your pouch was full.

That’s why Gaold had decided to aim for “Hell”—the domain of Niflheim’s ruler.

“How much further?” Gaold asked. “Hell, or whatever it’s called.”

The guide pointed ahead, then flinched, as if bracing for doom. “A little more… but—no! I can’t go further! Let’s turn back!”

He was terrified of Gaold’s group.

Niflheim was a land of death, avoided by all—yet every few years, a handful of madmen came seeking fortune.

The guide had led six such expeditions—once every two years. Each time, they’d hunted ghosts on the outskirts, praying for elixir to drop. One vial meant survival. Two meant two more years of comfort.

But Gaold’s group was different.

From the start, they’d ignored the outskirts. For six days straight, they’d charged toward Hell, cutting down nearly two hundred Grim Reapers.

Grim Reapers—the strongest of the Triple S-class evil spirits on the mainland.

The guide had nearly soiled himself when he first saw the god of death: a towering figure on a five-meter black horse, wielding a massive scythe.

He’d come for the thrill, lured by dreams of wealth. But now, all he wanted was to go home.

“You’re really going through with this?” the guide asked, voice cracking.

Gaold nodded.

“Then… fine. I’ll take you all the way. But my job ends at Hell. Beyond that, I don’t know the path.”

“Understood.”

Gaold turned and resumed walking.

To the others, he was a mountain—immovable, unbreakable. But up close, his face was hollow, drawn thin by cold and exhaustion.

Zulu stopped sucking on the black elixir vial she’d been sipping from and pointed ahead.

“There’s something.”

The guide looked up—and his expression changed.

For the first time, awe replaced fear.

*This is it. I’ve lived for this moment.*

His ability to guide here wasn’t from maps or magic. It came from the frozen corpses of those who had come before. After decades in Niflheim, he could read the dead like landmarks.

As Gaold’s group advanced, they saw them—over a thousand figures, frozen in ice like statues.

Different species. Different eras. All frozen in terror.

“Is this the entrance to Hell?” Kang Nan asked.

The guide hesitated. “Wait. I’ve never been this far either. According to my notes…”

He pulled out a worn notebook, flipping through pages filled with meticulous records. He turned his body slightly, as if guarding the secrets within.

As the only true Niflheim expert on the mainland, his notes were invaluable.

“Ah… the Cathesis race, frozen with one eye closed…”

He scanned the frozen figures, then moved toward a bipedal creature with a lizard’s face and a long tail.

“The direction the index finger of its left hand points to.”

The guide frowned. “Damn it… it only has three fingers. Which one’s the index?”

Assuming the middle finger was meant, he pointed to a spot fifteen degrees off their current path.

“If you go that way, Hell will appear. So here… I—Hee!”

He yelped as Gaold suddenly turned and shoved him forward.

The party radiated menace.

“I-I didn’t mean to—!”

A thick mist began rising from the ice.

A familiar sight—but this time, the air was different.

Then, from the fog, the shape of a black horse emerged.

Its cry pierced the air—higher than a woman’s scream, sharper than ice cracking.

On the saddle, wreathed in black smoke, sat the Grim Reaper. A four-meter scythe rested over his shoulder.

Forty of them.

The largest single group they’d faced.

“Damn it! I’m going to die here with twelve black elixirs!”

The guide collapsed to his knees, tears freezing on his cheeks.

No one comforted him.

Even Zulu felt the weight of this battle. It was on another level entirely.

“I’ll release the Salamander,” she said.

But both Kang Nan and Gaold were already drained. To fight now would be suicide.

Yet Gaold stepped forward—alone.

“I’ll wait here,” he said. “You’ll only get caught in it.”

“Are you insane?” the guide shrieked. “What can you do alone? And I’m not weak enough to get dragged into a fight!”

“I know,” Gaold said, voice low. Then he paused, swaying slightly as if on the verge of collapse. He looked back at Kang Nan.

“But this time… it’ll be different.”

Kang Nan’s expression turned to stone.

Because he was right.

From Gaold’s forehead, his hair was rapidly turning white—not from age, but from power surging through him.

Something had awakened.

And the ice trembled beneath his feet.
