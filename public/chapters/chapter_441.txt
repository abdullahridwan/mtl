Task Force (3)  
~*~

A mysterious bird, Kaidra, soared toward Galliant Island, its sharp eyes locking onto a cargo ship cutting across the open sea. It dipped lower, circling silently.

Below, Zulu released Kydra, and the four—Zulu, Shiina, Quan, and Shirone—plummeted toward the waves at breakneck speed. Zulu and Shiina activated fly magic, while Shirone unfurled her wings to slow her descent. Quan, though unfamiliar with such aerial feats, kept his gaze fixed on the ship below, unflinching.

His ability—generating external gravitational force through muscle vibration—was no less refined than any master wizard’s craft. Shiina watched him with quiet admiration. This was the death magician, a man once hailed as an unrivaled prodigy in swordsmanship a decade ago—now a shadow of his former self, as if he’d lost not just his edge, but a part of himself.

*Come to think of it… they look alike.*

The four landed lightly on the deserted aft deck of the vessel. The moment their feet touched, Zulu summoned Autoprism, cloaking them all in invisibility.

“As expected,” Zulu murmured, “it’s easier when talented people work together.”

How many could infiltrate a ship adrift at sea and vanish toward Galliant without a trace? If Sein had been here, he could have silenced every sound with Equilibrium. But even without him, this team was far from ordinary.

Inside the cargo hold, sacks of rice and dried goods filled the space. The four slipped into the corner, backs pressed together for cover.

“The moment we dock,” Zulu said, “Autoprism won’t hold. Jonah’s surveillance will be active. Flu is waiting at the mid-junction—you’ll have to exit unseen.”

Shiina handed Quan a robe. Zulu could mask his face well enough with his current attire, and Shirone could rely on her Diamond Armament to conceal her identity. The rest would have to depend on timing and silence.

An hour later, the ship moored at the harbor. Outside, the warehouse erupted in commotion as sailors rushed in. The four seized the moment, slipping through the open door just as the workers entered.

On deck, dockhands from the island bustled about, loading and unloading cargo. No one spared a glance for the hooded figures moving among them.

Down at the dock, the scene was chaotic—ship mechanics, freight agents, warehouse supervisors, and traders swarmed the piers. Among them, Jis, a man with a long-standing connection to Shirone, darted between groups, hawking tours.

“Now! We’ll carry your load! And guide you to the Kergo Ruins—Galliant’s finest attraction! Interpreters available!”

Shirone pulled up the hood of her Diamond Armament robe and turned her head slightly toward Jis.

*It’s the same, Jis. Is Yuna doing well?*

The place hadn’t emptied out just because the parrot thieves were gone. Life here had simply adapted, surviving in its own way.

“Huh? Who is it?” Jis turned, and Shirone dipped her head slightly before following the group away.

She wanted to say hello—but what could she say? She was leaving the world behind, stepping toward the edge of everything.

*Be happy, Jis.*

Jis stood frozen, staring after her. Tears welled in his eyes.

“Ugh, why are there so few customers today?” a nearby worker grumbled. “Hey, Jis—what’s wrong? You look like you’ve seen a ghost.”

“No… I just feel strange. Something about those people…”

His friend frowned, eyeing the hooded figures. “Don’t dwell on it. They look dangerous. Get on the wrong side of them, and you won’t even find your bones.”

Another chimed in, “Ten years as a solicitor, and my gut says the same. Those people reek of danger.”

Jis couldn’t look away until they vanished into the crowd.

A wave of nostalgia washed over him.

*Come to think of it… are you living well?*

“Hey! Jim here! You got a handyman?”

A tall man stepped off the ferry, dragging two heavy bags. Jis snapped back to reality and waved, sprinting toward him.

“Yes, yes! Coming!”

~*~

**Kergo Ruins**

Gaold, Kang Nan, Sein, Zulu, Flu, Etela, Shiina, Quan, and Shirone—the nine members of the operation—finally stood together.

No formal briefing was needed. They’d all been briefed en route. A few agents were already inside the ruins, but they’d been neutralized by the team waiting in position. Still, caution was key.

“Keep your faces covered,” Zulu advised.

But Gaold, the central figure, flipped back his hood and tore off his robe, tossing it to the ground.

Kang Nan gritted his teeth. “Put it back on when you’re ready to speak civilly.”

“Too stifling,” Gaold snapped. “And if we don’t move now, it’ll be too late. We’re heading into the Kergo Autonomous Region.”

Sein added, “If someone’s posted at Guffin’s Door, I’ll assess them by skill. One of us might need to step in.”

They made their way to the terraced altar on the east side of the central temple—the very place where Shirone had once activated the Immortal Function and passed the labyrinth trial.

A native Kergo warrior stood guard at the entrance, but he wasn’t the same man Shirone remembered. Chief Khadum had long since drained his lifespan, and the tribe had clearly changed in his absence.

When Shirone had visited a year ago, this man had radiated the fierce aura of a warrior priest. Now, faced with Gaold and his companions, he swallowed hard, visibly nervous.

Even if this guard wasn’t a high-ranking warrior, he should have sensed the lethal presence of nine battle-hardened individuals. Instead, he trembled.

Gaold strode forward and spoke in fluent Kergo:

“Keltia, Lo Hoima. Akrasia, Widmia Benzene.”

*Knock on my door, and it will open.*

The phrase echoed in Shirone’s memory—a forgotten code from a past trial.

But the native shook his head, face grim. “Not now. Too many are waiting to ascend to heaven. Return in three days.”

Gaold’s expression darkened. With a flick of his wrist, he struck the man across the face. The native flew back, crashing to the ground, unconscious.

Shirone stared at the fallen guard. “Are you angry at what he said?”

“No,” Sein replied. “Gaold doesn’t understand Kergo.”

“… …”

Without another word, Gaold activated the ancient device embedded in the altar. The stone walls groaned and collapsed inward, revealing a hidden passage.

Kang Nan muttered, “But I hate Kergo.”

Inside, a spiral staircase wound down into darkness. After twenty minutes of descent, they reached a long corridor. Two Kergo warriors guarded an iron gate—tattooed, bare-chested, and far more aggressive than those above.

“Who are you?” one demanded. “How did you get in? The entrance to heaven is closed—”

Gaold struck the first man’s chin with the back of his hand. The warrior crumpled.

The second lunged forward, eyes blazing—but Gaold turned and backhanded him too. Both guards fell, blood trickling from their noses.

Shirone watched, uneasy. This wasn’t how it was supposed to be. In the Achievement and Sacrifice Room, those who awakened the Immortal Function were welcomed, not beaten. There was no need for violence.

“Can’t we just do things the right way?” she asked. “I know a little about these people. If you reveal all eight eyes of an angel—”

“Angel?” Gaold turned, towering over her.

“Shirone, that’s just a toy made by my classmate.”

She fell silent.

She felt the rage burning inside Gaold—not just from the moment, but from years of buried pain. And now, she understood why he hated Kergo.

Beyond the iron gate lay an octagonal chamber. Three warriors stood guard, and in the center, a priest with a white tribal tattoo—symbol of his rank—stepped forward.

Even to Shirone, this man radiated a different level of power than the others.

But Gaold ignored him completely, walking straight to the altar where eight floating orbs hovered—the core of the maze.

The priest’s face twisted in outrage. “Who dares enter the sacred chamber of the angel? A stranger—”

Gaold snatched one of the orbs and slammed it into the altar.

A simple press of air—no magic, no incantation—but the altar shuddered with a deep, resonant *thud*.

The priest staggered, eyes wide. He looked around at the trembling chamber, then at the orb.

And there it was—the white light. The same light Shirone had failed to see a year ago, now blazing brightly.

She stared, stunned.

Not only had the light activated, but the number displayed was unlike any before:

**1,008,000**

“Over a million points…” she whispered.

Gaold stepped toward the priest, who stood frozen. Gaold locked eyes with him and said one word:

“Move.”

The priest’s body responded before his mind could protest. He stepped aside.

This number shattered the limits of divine language.

When they reached the Kergo Autonomous District, warriors already waited—armed, alert, and furious.

They raised their spears in unison, eyes blazing.

“This is sacred Kergo land! Invade, and we will kill you—no matter the reason!”

Gaold stepped forward, palm open. A sphere of compressed air formed above it.

Compared to Heaven’s subjects, these warriors were almost quaint. Devoted to Ankera, yes—but their strength was nothing next to Gaold’s.

To him, they were all just *Kergo*.

“Lower your weapons!” a voice boomed.

A broad-shouldered man pushed through the ranks. His black tattoo had turned gold.

Shirone recognized him instantly.

Mahatu—once a rival to Chief Kadum, now clearly the new chieftain. The golden mark confirmed it.

The warriors knelt, but their fury remained.

“Chief! These are heretics who attacked our priest! Why do you let them pass?”

Mahatu met Gaold’s gaze for a brief moment, then turned to the group behind him.

“It’s been a while, Mr. Shirone.”

He spoke in fluent continental language—a necessity now, given his role in negotiating with the Galliant government.

“Mr. Mahatu,” Shirone said. “You’ve become chief.”

“I’m not sure I deserved it,” Mahatu replied. “But it happened.”

The warriors seethed.

“To attack a priest is the gravest sin!” one shouted. “Chief, explain! Who are they?”

Mahatu turned to Shirone, eyes searching. “Why didn’t you prove the power of an angel?”

“There were… circumstances,” Shirone said.

Mahatu nodded slowly. He’d heard the rumors. More and more people came to Kergo, drawn by whispers of heaven. But seeing Shirone’s companions—especially Gaold—told him everything.

*What in hell are these monsters?*

Mahatu, Kergo’s greatest warrior, could feel it. Each of these nine had honed their power to the edge of human limits. Gaold, in particular, was beyond human.

Even Shirone, who prided herself on unshakable will, felt a chill just meeting his gaze.

Tensions simmered. The warriors refused to lower their weapons. Gaold stood motionless, a storm barely contained.

“Punish the heretic!” one warrior roared. “We’ll fight! We fear nothing like you!”

“Then come,” Gaold said coldly. “Come and see.”

Kang Nan rubbed his temples, watching Gaold argue with men who didn’t even speak the same language.

*As expected, I shouldn’t have let this man lead.*

But he understood—sometimes, Gaold’s way was necessary.

And wasn’t this the day he’d waited twenty years for?

Even the ugliest duckling couldn’t complain today.

“Enough!” Mahatu bellowed. “How dare you defy my order?”

“Chief!” another warrior cried. “We’ll die for you! But to compromise with heresy is to deny Kergo itself!”

“Fool,” Mahatu snapped. “Do you even know who stands before you?”

The warriors turned to Shirone.

A year ago, they’d been imprisoned under Kadum’s rule. They didn’t recognize her.

Mahatu raised his hand. “This is Shirone-sama—the messenger of God, who defeated the Kadum faction and saved the half-angel people.”
