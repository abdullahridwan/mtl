Chapter 447

Second Purgatory (4)

“Shirone?”

Kanya’s voice trembled.

It *was* Shirone—there was no mistaking him. But how could he be here?

At that moment, it didn’t matter.

If the man on the screen was truly Shirone, then he was the spark of hope the rebels had been waiting for.

—*Unit 5 collapsed! Fall back and cover!*

The radio crackled with urgency, snapping Kanya back to reality. She turned sharply toward the Multivision display.

Most of the aircraft were down, immobilized by the earlier assault. But a few still had power—and every one of them was aiming directly at Shirone.

“Wait, no!”

Kanya lunged for the radio. “Don’t shoot! He’s not an enemy!”

No response. She repeated the warning, but the silence from the other end offered no relief. Her eyes darted back to Shirone.

He stood tall, unflinching, his presence radiating quiet defiance.

“This… this…!”

With a sharp press of the emergency release, the tempered glass of the cockpit exploded outward.

Kanya scrambled out from under the wreckage, dragging Piper free from Kuroi’s synchronization machine. She stumbled from the brush, raising both hands high.

“Hold your fire! Stop shooting!”

The platoon leader’s voice crackled from the surviving Unit 5’s speaker.

—*Open fire.*

*Click. Click. Click.*

Kanya squeezed her eyes shut. But instead of gunfire, all she heard was the metallic *clang* of bullets ricocheting off steel.

“What… what?”

She blinked, disoriented. The world around her seemed to warp, the air itself distorting like heat over stone. At the center of the distortion, two colossal cogwheels turned in perfect mesh.

Sein’s *Equilibrium* had neutralized the chemical triggers in the ammunition.

“Misfire! It’s a dud round, Platoon Commander!”

“Same here! Check your spark plugs!”

“Nothing’s wrong! This—this… *Kuck!*”

*Cooong!*

Quan and Etela struck—one of the remaining Kuroi crumpled under their assault. It took longer than expected, but the result was undeniable: two warriors trained in anti-biological combat had felled a war machine with sheer physical force.

The cockpit glass shattered. The pilot slumped unconscious, still clutching the synchro controls. Beside him stood Armand, silent and watchful.

Space-time mages like Armand could appear anywhere, even inside sealed machinery, as long as the coordinates were known.

The rest of the team made quick work of the remaining Kuroi. Soon, only one aircraft remained—the platoon commander’s personal unit.

Gaold stepped forward, studying the machine with the curiosity of a scholar encountering a new species. He didn’t extend a hand in greeting. Instead, he tilted his head up toward the pilot’s purple-lit face and said:

“Get out. Unless you want to die.”

*Perong!*

The cockpit launched upward like a cannon shell. The platoon leader leapt down, Piper in hand, and lunged at Gaold.

“Die!”

“No—Platoon Commander!” Kanya screamed.

But it was too late. The commander’s Cigna blade was already descending toward Gaold’s skull.

“Canya?” Gaold glanced over at Shirone—then, as if the movement were separate from thought, his fist snapped forward and smashed into the commander’s jaw.

“Agh!”

The man dropped like a sack of stones, rolling across the ground with wide, unseeing eyes. Had Gaold not canceled the press magic at the last instant, the blow would have erased the commander’s face from existence.

Gaold turned to Shirone. “Someone you know?”

Shirone squinted, still disbelieving. “Is that… Kanya? You’re Kanya, right?”

Relief flooded her face. The tension in her body melted away. Without another thought, she tore off her Piper, stripped down in the middle of the battlefield, and ran barefoot across the dirt—straight into Shirone’s arms.

“Shirone! Shirone! *Aaaaaang!*”

The rebels froze. A moment ago, they’d been locked in mortal combat. Now, a tender reunion unfolded in the middle of the wreckage.

“…Really? *Shirone?*”

Among the rebels, there was no one who didn’t know the name.

He was the first Nephilim to stop Ilhwa’s drinking.

The rebellion had begun with the riots in Sharmaine District 73—and Shirone had become their symbol, their beacon. They called him *the Light of District 73*.

Kanya wiped her tears, laughing through her embarrassment. “Sorry… I didn’t recognize you at first. You’re wearing such strange gear.”

Shirone waved it off. Anyone would have mistaken him for an enemy under the circumstances.

“It’s fine. How’s Lena?”

“She’s safe. Back at the main unit. She’ll go absolutely black with joy when she sees you.”

Shirone chuckled. “And your parents?”

“Dad still scolds me.”

“…I see.”

He didn’t ask about her mother.

Back then, her time had already been short. Kanya’s silence now told him everything.

There was no such thing as a good death.

But Shirone hoped—truly hoped—that she had passed peacefully, surrounded by those who loved her.

“How long has it been since I left?”

“Exactly 432 days.”

Kanya didn’t hesitate. That day—the day Shirone stopped Ilhwa’s drinking—was etched into rebel history. No calculation was needed.

*432 days.*

Not so different from the time that had passed in Shirone’s own world.

The Guffin’s Door warped space, eliminating temporal distortions caused by distance. Yet time here still moved slightly faster—likely due to the planet’s gravitational field.

Shirone turned to the question that had been burning in his mind.

“I heard you’re at war with Heaven. But since I arrived, I haven’t seen a single angel—not even Mara. Why?”

Kanya shook her head. “We don’t know either. We fought hard—almost got wiped out—but then, one day, the angels just… vanished. Even on the full moon, when they always patrolled.”

Sein, who had been listening while maintaining her mental link, stepped forward, eyes alight.

“When exactly did this happen?”

“Um… about 130 days ago?”

*130 days.*

Roughly three months in the original world’s time zone.

Sein’s mind raced. That was around the time she’d lost contact with Gaold’s bodyguards who had infiltrated Heaven.

*Something must have happened up there. But what? Is it connected to the maze… or not?*

The platoon leader glared at the ruined Kuroi.

Shirone—the Light of District 73—hadn’t needed to fight. But the cost had been high.

“This is serious,” he muttered. “The damage to the aircraft is extensive.”

Shirone approached, guilt in his eyes. The machines were beyond field repair—window maintenance would be required.

“I’m sorry.”

“There’s nothing to apologize for. *We* fired first.”

The female squad leader, clearly bearing some responsibility, raised her palms in a gesture of fairness. “But that man—Gaold—gave me a grotesque vision! If you’d seen it, you’d understand. Anyone would’ve seen him as a threat.”

Fleur scoffed. “Before that, *you* aimed at the arc first.”

“Before that, *he* started the fight!”

“You misunderstood *before* you started fighting.”

The woman snapped. “How could I *not* misunderstand? He came at me like he was going to crush me!”

Silence fell.

Eventually, someone muttered, “Well… Gaold’s face *is* kind of a sin.”

The platoon commander gave up. “Enough. Let’s stop this. Gather what’s left of the aircraft and rejoin the main unit. They’re in no position to relax either.”

Sein asked, “Where’s the main unit?”

“The Plains of the Dead. We’re conducting an operation to eliminate the giants.”

The commander strode toward his surviving Kuroi. “This is the only unit still operational. I’ll lead. Follow me—”

He stopped.

Gaold was standing directly behind him.

The commander, one foot already on the machine, stared in disbelief. Gaold simply tilted his chin toward the side.

“Move. I’m taking it.”

“What? You’re *riding* this?”

Kang Nan sighed and stepped forward. “Why the hell do you want to ride it? It’s synced to Piper. It won’t move unless you have the link.”

Gaold’s answer was simple. “Then give me Piper.”

Kang Nan turned to Sein, silent question in his eyes. *Can we trust this man with our equipment?*

Sein nodded.

Shirone had shattered Heaven’s defenses. But only Gaold could stand against an army of angels. If a fight came, he was their best chance.

“For now, let him do as he pleases.”

Five minutes later.

*Chunk. Chunk.*

Gaold, now equipped with Piper, climbed into the Kuroi.

The platoon commander, stripped of his command and machine, stood staring at the distant mountains, hollow-eyed.

Shirone watched with concern and turned to Fleur. “Are we really doing the right thing? The mood’s light, but… stealing their equipment feels a bit much.”

Fleur raised an eyebrow. “What’s wrong with you? Since when do you care about propriety when something interests you?”

Shirone closed his mouth.

Fleur sighed, then relented. “Honestly… I don’t know either. But everyone here follows the Association President. I trust her. That’s enough for me.”

Gaold connected Piper to the Synchro Machine.

As he raised his arms inside the cockpit, the Kuroi mirrored the motion.

“How do you feel?” someone asked.

With no tempered glass, Gaold leaned forward, scanning the terrain with his own eyes. “Hmm. Let’s see.”

He twisted the drive joint at the waist to its limit.

The Kuroi’s waist spun like a top.

“Quack. Kang Nan, look at this.”

Gaold swung the machine’s arm in a playful jab. Kang Nan leaned back effortlessly—but then yelped.

“Oh, seriously! I’m afraid I’ll break it!”

Kanya watched, wide-eyed. These were the same people who had just destroyed four Kuroi without breaking a sweat.

“Please, be careful,” she pleaded. “The Kuroi are extremely valuable.”

“Whoa, okay. I’ll die of stress because of *him*.”

Only then did Gaold begin to assess the machine properly.

It wasn’t on par with the Spirit Zone’s synesthesia, but the Multivision offered an exceptional field of view. And the firepower? Comparable to a heavy air cannon.

“How many of these do the rebels have?”

“That’s confidential. I can’t tell you.”

The Kuroi’s gun barrel swiveled—*click*—and pointed directly at the platoon commander.

It was unclear how much was a joke, how much was threat.

“It’s confidential,” Gaold said, “but I don’t care. At best, you only know your battalion’s inventory. A platoon commander wouldn’t know the rebels’ total production.”

He licked his lips, then powered down the machine.

Jumping down, he tossed aside the Piper and rejoined Shirone’s group without a word.

Kang Nan crossed his arms, glaring. “Why? You acted like you were going to ride it all day.”

“I get motion sickness. Worse than you can imagine.”

Kang Nan gasped—then snorted in disbelief.

Meanwhile, Gaold’s mind churned.

*I wouldn’t even need a chin to crush that thing. At least two thousand units? No… more than that.*

That was the number of Kuroi he estimated would be needed to take down a single ordinary angel.

* * *

As the sun dipped below the horizon, Shirone and the others emerged from the forest.

From the hilltop, they looked out over the Plains of the Dead.

Below, the 23rd Battalion of the Rebel 1st HQ clashed with a procession of giants, the battle stretching across the horizon like a scar.

The battalion had thirty Kuroi in total.

But with Kanya’s unit sent as reinforcements, only twenty-five remained in active combat.

Divided into five squads, the Kuroi units worked in tandem with infantry, luring the giants at least a kilometer from the main column before unleashing concentrated fire.

Each time a giant fell, Shirone’s expression darkened.

Giants were once human.

If the Mecca members had been subjects, they would know this truth better than anyone. Kanya’s face, too, was shadowed with sorrow.

“It’s standard procedure,” the platoon commander said, almost apologetically. “Eliminate giants on sight. If they reach Jotunheim and achieve mental unity, their combined schema becomes as powerful as a master swordsman. By then, even a full battalion couldn’t stop them.”

The group descended into the plains and quickly rejoined the main unit.

The company commander stared in disbelief at the returning squad—only one Kuroi remained.

Then his face twisted with rage.

“What happened?! I sent *five* units to handle this! I expected results, not this disaster!”

“…Sorry,” the platoon commander muttered.

He had no defense. As leader, he bore the blame—ten mouths wouldn’t save him.

But Kanya wasn’t waiting.

*Shirone is back.*

And he’d brought someone stronger than anyone they’d ever seen.

This news outweighed the loss of machines.

“Squadron Commander! I have a report!”

“What are you doing?! You’re bypassing the chain of command! Show some respect!”

Kuroi pilots were infantry squad leaders. Reporting directly to the company commander, skipping the platoon leader, was a grave breach of protocol.

But Kanya couldn’t wait.

This was urgent—critical. The giants’ advance was being contained, but the real breakthrough was here.

“The Light of District 73!” she shouted.

“What nonsense is this?!”

Kanya screamed it again, raw and fierce:

“SHIRONE IS HERE!”
