Second Purgatory (4)

"Shirone?"

Kanya's voice trembled as she stared at the Multivision display. It was definitely him — but how could he possibly be here?

No. That didn't matter right now. If the figure on the screen really was Shirone, then he could bring new hope to rebels who had all but given up.

*—Unit Five is down! Someone cover them!*

The radio chatter finally snapped Kanya back to her senses, and she turned to look at the Multivision feed again. Most of the enemy aircraft had already been disabled, but a few of the Kuroi units were still armed and capable of firing — and every one of them had its guns trained on Shirone.

"Wait, no!"

Kanya slammed her hand down on the radio.

"Don't shoot! He's not an enemy!"

No one answered. A cold dread settled over her, and she turned back to check on Shirone. He stood there completely unconcerned, openly displaying his strength as though daring them to try something.

"This—!"

She slapped the emergency escape switch. The cockpit's tempered glass blew forward with a crack. Kanya tore her Piper free from the Kuroi's synchro machine, scrambled out of the cockpit, and raised her hand into the air.

"Wait! Stop shooting!"

The platoon leader's voice crackled out of the speakers of Unit Five, which had just scrambled back onto its feet.

*—Open fire.*

Tick! Tick! Tick!

Kanya squeezed her eyes shut, bracing for the worst — but all she heard was the sound of rounds bouncing harmlessly off metal.

"What... what's happening?"

She opened her eyes, looking around in confusion. The air itself seemed to be warping. At the center of the distortion, a pair of enormous cogwheels turned against each other, meshing in perfect rhythm.

It was Sein's Equilibrium — a spell that had quietly rebalanced the chemical reactions inside the ammunition, turning every shot into a dud.

"Misfire! It's a dud round, Platoon Commander!"

"Same here! Check your spark plugs!"

"Nothing's wrong with mine! This is—! Gah!"

Craang!

Two of the intruders — Quan and Etela — brought down one of the Kuroi with nothing but their bare hands. It took them longer than it should have, but there was something almost poetic about two anti-biological warfare specialists tearing a machine apart through sheer physical force.

Another Kuroi's canopy burst open. Its pilot slumped unconscious over the controls — with Armin already standing calmly beside him inside the cockpit. As long as he had fixed coordinates to work with, a space-time wizard could slip through even a sealed hull without trouble.

The rest of the party made short work of the remaining machines. Soon, the only Kuroi still standing was the platoon leader's own.

Gaold strolled up to it, stroking his chin with idle curiosity, studying it the way one might study some unfamiliar creature. Then, instead of offering any kind of greeting, he looked up at the machine's purple-tinted canopy and said flatly:

"Get out. Unless you want to die."

The canopy blew open and rocketed skyward. The platoon leader lunged at Gaold, Piper cables still trailing from him, screaming, "Die!"

"No, Platoon Commander!" Kanya shouted — too late.

"Kanya?" Gaold's eyes flicked toward Shirone for the barest instant — and in that same moment, as if the motion belonged to some entirely separate part of him, his fist snapped out and caught the platoon leader square across the jaw.

"Guh!"

The man crumpled, rolling across the ground, eyes wide open in shock. If Gaold hadn't held back the killing force woven into that punch — the press magic layered beneath his knuckles — the platoon leader's head would have simply ceased to exist.

"Anyone you know, Shirone?" Gaold asked.

Shirone narrowed his eyes, studying the woman standing off to the side more closely.

"Is that... Kanya? You're Kanya, right?"

Whatever composure Kanya had left finally gave out. Sobbing, she tore the last straps of her Piper gear away and ran — bare-handed, stumbling — straight into Shirone's arms.

"Shirone! Shirone! Aaaaah!"

Everyone froze. It was a strange, tender scene to erupt in the middle of what had, moments ago, been a fight to the death.

"It's really... Shirone?" someone murmured.

There wasn't a single rebel who didn't know his name. He was the first Nephilim to ever put a stop to Ilhwa's Drinking. The rebel movement itself had grown out of the uprising that began in Sharmaine District 73, and out of respect, they had taken to calling him "the Light of District 73."

Kanya finally wiped her tears away and managed a sheepish smile.

"Sorry. I didn't recognize you — you were dressed so strangely."

Shirone didn't hold it against her. Anyone would have mistaken him for an enemy under the circumstances.

"It's fine. How's Lena?"

"She's with the main unit. She's going to lose her mind with joy when she sees you."

"Ha! And your parents?"

"Dad's still doing corrections work."

"...I see."

Shirone didn't ask about her mother. Even back then, her life had been running short, and Kanya's silence told him everything he needed to know. There was no such thing as a good death in this world — but he hoped, at least, that she'd gone peacefully, surrounded by her family.

"How long has it been since I left?"

"Exactly four hundred and thirty-two days."

Kanya didn't even need to think about it. The day Shirone had stopped Ilhwa's Drinking was etched into rebel memory as a landmark date — no one had to calculate it.

*Four hundred and thirty-two days.*

That wasn't too far off from the time that had passed in Shirone's own world. Guffin's Door distorted space, not time, so there shouldn't have been any real discrepancy — the slight difference was probably just the influence of this world's gravitational field.

Shirone asked the question that had been nagging at him the most.

"I heard you were at war with Heaven. But I haven't seen so much as a single Mara since I got here, let alone an angel. What happened?"

Kanya shook her head.

"We don't really know either. We were fighting them tooth and nail — honestly, we were losing — and then one day the angels just started disappearing. Even during the full moon, when they always used to patrol."

Sein, who had been quietly listening in through a mental link, cut in, her eyes suddenly sharp with interest.

"When exactly did this start?"

"Um... maybe a hundred and thirty days ago?"

*A hundred and thirty days.*

Roughly three months, by the calendar of the original world. And that timing lined up exactly with when contact had been lost with the spies Gaold had planted inside Heaven.

*Something happened up there. But what? Does it have anything to do with the labyrinth — or not?*

The platoon leader stared at his ruined Kuroi with a bitter expression. Shirone — the Light of District 73 — hadn't needed to fight them at all, and yet the cost had been steep.

"This is a disaster. The damage to the aircraft is severe."

Shirone approached, looking apologetic. The unit was wrecked badly enough that it couldn't be salvaged without a full depot overhaul.

"Sorry about that."

"There's nothing to apologize for. We attacked first."

The squad leader — the woman pilot who bore some of the responsibility — threw her hands up defensively.

"But that man gave me some kind of grotesque vision the moment I saw him! Anyone would've taken that as hostile intent!"

She lost that exchange badly.

"Before that, though — you were the one who aimed at him first, weren't you?"

"He started it first!"

"You misunderstood the situation first, and that's what started it."

The woman couldn't take it anymore.

"How was I *not* supposed to misunderstand, looking like that?! He walked up to me like he was about to hit me!"

A brief, awkward silence followed. In the end, once someone floated the theory that Gaold's face alone was reason enough to assume hostility, the platoon leader decided he didn't want to argue anymore.

"Fine. Enough. Let's collect the wreckage and get back to the main unit — they're not exactly having an easy time of it either."

Sein asked, "Where's the main unit?"

"The Plains of the Dead. We're in the middle of an operation to wipe out the giants."

The platoon leader started walking toward his own Kuroi.

"This is the only unit still combat-capable. I'll take the lead — follow me and— ...yes?"

He stopped short. Gaold was standing directly behind him.

The platoon leader, one foot already braced on the boarding step, looked back in disbelief. Gaold merely tilted his chin toward the cockpit.

"Move. I'm riding."

"What? You want to ride *this*?"

Kang Nan sighed and stepped closer.

"Why on earth would you want to ride that thing? It's synced to his Piper — it won't even move for you."

Gaold's solution was simple.

"Then give me the Piper."

Kang Nan glanced toward Sein — a silent question directed at the group's actual authority: was it really all right to hand a total stranger this kind of gear?

Sein nodded. Shirone was going to be the one dealing with Heaven itself, but Gaold was the one who could hold his own against a horde of angels long enough to buy them time. When it came to actual combat, it made sense to let him do as he pleased.

"For now, let him have his way."

Five minutes later, Gaold — Piper properly attached — climbed into the cockpit of the last remaining Kuroi. The platoon leader, stripped of both his mecha and his gear, stood off to one side, staring blankly at the distant mountains.

Shirone watched him with some guilt and turned to Fleur.

"Is this really okay? I get that things settled down, but just... taking the man's equipment feels a little much."

"What are you even worried about? Since when have you ever stopped doing something interesting just because it felt wrong?"

Shirone had nothing to say to that.

Fleur seemed to feel a flicker of guilt herself, and softened a little.

"Honestly, I don't really know either. But everyone here trusts the guild president enough to have followed her all the way out to this world. I'm just trusting her judgment too."

Meanwhile, Gaold linked the Piper into the synchro machine. As he folded his arms inside the cockpit, the Kuroi mirrored the motion perfectly.

"Feels about right," he muttered. With no canopy glass in the way, he could see the surrounding terrain clearly with his own eyes as he tested the machine's movement.

"Hmm, how about this?"

He cranked the drive joint at the waist all the way around, and the Kuroi's midsection began spinning like a top.

"Ha! Kang Nan, look at this!"

He swung the Kuroi's arm at her playfully. She dodged it easily, leaning back out of the way, but she still snapped at him.

"Are you serious?! You're going to break the thing!"

Coming from someone who had already personally destroyed four of these machines, the complaint didn't land the way she probably wanted. Kanya, meanwhile, was practically pleading, her eyes wide and sincere.

"Please — be careful with it. These units are genuinely valuable."

"Ugh, fine. That man is going to be the death of me."

Only then did Gaold actually take a proper look at the equipment. It wasn't quite on the level of Spirit Zone's synesthesia interfaces, but the Multivision system offered an excellent field of view, and the firepower rivaled a proper autocannon.

"How many of these does the rebellion actually have?"

"That's classified. I can't tell you."

Click. The Kuroi's gun barrel swung around and pointed directly at the platoon leader. It was impossible to say how much of that was a joke.

"It's classified, but also I genuinely don't know. I might know the equipment count for my own battalion, at best. There's no way a platoon leader has visibility into total rebel production numbers."

Gaold clicked his tongue and brought the machine to a stop. He climbed out, tossed the Piper aside, and wandered back over to rejoin Shirone's group without ceremony.

Kang Nan crossed her arms and glared at him.

"Why? You acted like you were going to ride that thing all day."

"Motion sickness. Worse than you'd believe."

Kang Nan let out an exasperated snort.

Privately, though, Gaold's mind was working through something far less trivial. *That man wouldn't stand a chance against even one of those things. To bring down a single ordinary angel, you'd need... two thousand of these units, easily. Probably more.*

That was the number Gaold had calculated it would take to properly restrain even a low-ranking angel.

* * *

By the time the sun began to set, Shirone and the others had left the forest behind. From the hilltop overlooking the Plains of the Dead, they could see it stretching to the horizon — a column of giants locked in battle with the Rebel First Headquarters' 23rd Battalion.

The battalion had thirty Kuroi units in total, but since Kanya's squad had been sent out as reinforcements elsewhere, only twenty-five remained actively deployed on the plains. Split into five groups, the Kuroi worked in tandem with infantry, luring small clusters of giants at least a kilometer away from the main marching column before cutting them down with concentrated fire.

Every time one of the giants collapsed in pieces, Shirone's expression darkened. Giants had once been human. If the Mecca had once ruled over these very people, they understood that better than anyone — which was why Kanya's face had clouded over too.

"It's standard procedure to eliminate giants the moment they're spotted," the platoon leader said, sounding almost apologetic. "If they reach Jotunheim and achieve unity of mind, they can draw on the full power of a true giant."

It wasn't wrong. If eight egos merged into one, the resulting construct became powerful enough to rival an elite swordsman — and at that point, not even a full battalion could handle it easily.

Once they crossed into the plains proper, the group made their way quickly back to the main force. The company commander, seeing the party return with only a single surviving Kuroi, stared at them in disbelief before his temper snapped.

"What happened out there?! It was the Kergoin operation! I sent five units to deal with it cleanly — is *this* what 'cleanly' looks like to you?!"

"I'm sorry, sir," the platoon leader said again. As the one in command of the squad, there was nothing he could say in his own defense, no matter how many excuses he might have offered.

But Kanya felt differently. Shirone had returned — and he'd brought back allies far stronger than before. That was news that outweighed the loss of a few tanks by any measure.

"Company Commander! I have a report!"

"What is this? Can't you see I'm speaking with the platoon leader? Are you ignoring the chain of command?"

A Kuroi pilot was, by rank, just an infantry squad leader — reporting directly over the platoon leader's head like this was a serious breach of protocol. But Kanya couldn't hold it in any longer. This was something the commander needed to know right now, especially with the giants' advance line already tightening around them.

"The Light of District 73!"

"What? What are you babbling about?"

Kanya threw her head back and shouted at the top of her lungs.

"Shirone is here!"
