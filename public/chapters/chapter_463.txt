Chapter 463

Opportunity for Revenge (5)

The ice sheet of Niflheim trembled.

From above, one might have seen ripples spreading outward across the frozen surface like concentric circles on water—except these were not ripples of water, but of pure, concentrated magical energy vibrating through the ice itself.

At the center stood Gaold.

Alone. Still. Utterly motionless.

“Big,” he muttered, grinding his teeth like a cornered beast.

Veins writhed along his neck and temples, pulsing like worms beneath his skin. The air around him shimmered with suppressed force.

Nearby, the guide knelt on the fractured ice, staring at the spot where the Grim Reapers had vanished. His face was pale, hollow with shock.

“This… this is impossible.”

As if in answer, the ice beneath them groaned. Cracks spiderwebbed outward in all directions, thick fissures splitting the ancient glacier. One jagged line curved unnaturally toward the guide, as though the ice itself sought to claim him.

“Hey!”

Nan Nan lunged, seized the man by the collar, and yanked him aside just as a four-meter-wide chasm opened where he’d been kneeling.

The cracks ceased. Silence fell—but not peace. The air was scorched, heavy with residual energy. It was the mark Gaold had carved into Niflheim itself: a testament of power too vast to contain.

Then, from the sky, the Grim Reapers ascended.

And as they rose, they vomited streams of black essence—life force, soul, memory—spilling into the air like ink from broken veins.

“Huh? Wh-what…?”

The guide’s eyes widened, trembling in their sockets. He had never seen anything like it. Ten black elixirs—ten concentrated cores of a Grim Reaper’s existence—soaring into the heavens at once.

Just witnessing it sent a jolt of ecstasy through his body, so intense it nearly stopped his heart. The sheer *pleasure* of it overwhelmed him.

Below, the elixirs rolled across the ice with dull thuds. The guide scrambled forward, limbs flailing like a madman, scooping them up without thought. He didn’t feel the cuts on his palms from the jagged ice. He didn’t care.

When he finally held one in both hands, the weight surprised him.

It was heavy. Unnaturally so.

But more than that—it *felt* heavy. As if he were carrying the weight of a lifetime.

‘If this were all mine…’

His breath hitched. His eyes burned with greed.

Then he saw Kang Nan’s cold, unreadable gaze.

His spirit shattered.

Words spilled out before he could stop them.

“Oh—no, I mean, half of it is mine… right?”

“Pick it up,” Kang Nan said, voice flat. “All of it.”

Only after the guide scrambled to obey did she turn her attention to Gaold.

‘He’s an idiot,’ she thought.

Pain—one hundred thousand times amplified.

To an ordinary human, such a concept was unthinkable. But for Gaold, it was routine. A daily reality.

Pain, to him, was just a signal—neural noise transmitted from body to brain. Nothing more.

Yet the curse he bore forced him to live with that signal turned to a scream, every second of every day. A lifetime of agony, unrelenting, inescapable.

And yet, he stood there—whole. Unbroken.

“Is it okay?” Gaold asked, wrinkling his nose with a mischievous grin.

Kang Nan snorted. “What if it’s not? Will you kiss me?”

“Why is this?” she muttered. “When we first met, he couldn’t even look me in the eye.”

*Nothing* like that had happened.

Yet she remembered that time—remembered it clearly. A pang of sadness touched her chest.

‘Was I… really like that?’

She was relieved to see him so bold now. But with that relief came something else—an ember glowing deep inside. A fire she hadn’t felt in years.

Her pride surged.

A normal human would scream and writhe on the ground if the pain of a paper cut were amplified a hundredfold.

Gaold lived with a thousandfold amplification—every second, every breath.

She knew, without needing to ask, that he couldn’t possibly be “okay.”

“Listen carefully,” she began. “From now on, when you’re amplifying pain—”

“I can see a little now,” Gaold interrupted. “Let’s go.”

He turned and walked toward the guide.

The man flinched as Gaold approached. His face twisted in pure terror.

At the start of this journey, he’d thought he could handle his share. That he could survive.

But now? Now he knew exactly where he stood.

This wasn’t about betrayal. Not anymore.

In Niflheim—a wasteland of ghosts and ice—Gaold was a god. And the guide was nothing but a mortal in the shadow of divinity.

‘He’ll kill me. I know he will.’

The agreement had been simple: fifty percent of the harvested elixirs. Half for him, half for them.

In theory, fair.

But in practice? Two black elixirs meant one for him. That was the rule.

And now, with more elixirs than ever, that fifty percent felt heavier than ever.

Why stay alive if you had to pay such a price?

“How many did you collect?” Gaold asked.

The guide winced, then slowly opened his pouch.

“Eh… well…”

Fourteen this time. Added to the previous twenty-four.

Thirty-eight black elixirs.

The number made his head spin. Thirty-eight lifetimes of power, wealth, freedom—all in his hands.

“As you can see,” the guide stammered, “there are thirty-eight.”

“I don’t have such big ambitions,” he added quickly. “Please, I just—”

“Go back.”

“Huh?”

The guide froze. He had been about to beg for mercy, to plead for more time.

Now, he looked up, stunned.

“Leave half. Take half. From now on, you don’t guide me.”

Gaold’s voice was calm. Final.

The closer they got to Hell, the more likely they were to encounter stronger enemies. Forty Grim Reapers had fallen in an instant. Who knew what waited ahead?

Letting the guide go now was smart. For both sides.

The man’s mind reeled. The very thing he feared most—being abandoned in this frozen hell—had just been reversed.

He could return to the mainland. With *nineteen* black elixirs.

Nineteen lifetimes.

He could live freely for thirty-eight years. Do whatever he wanted.

‘No,’ he thought. ‘Better. I’ll use ten to build a community. Then I’ll live in luxury, served by subordinates, for the rest of my life.’

But his heart didn’t settle.

He had nineteen. He kept nine for himself.

Yet he felt like he’d *lost* ten.

The human heart was strange. The moment he stopped chasing everything, he began to mourn what he’d given up.

‘Ai-san… if only there were one more. Just one. Then I wouldn’t regret it. Ten or nine—what’s the difference? It’s embarrassing to hesitate.’

The joy of holding one black elixir—of owning the world—had vanished the moment he grasped a greater greed.

‘Yes. Just one more. Even if I turn back now, the danger’s the same.’

He’d memorized the safest routes, the low-occurrence zones for Grim Reapers. But the data wasn’t perfect.

“What if I break the contract?” Gaold said, stepping closer. “I can’t let you walk away like this. Guide me to Hell.”

The guide looked into Gaold’s eyes.

Desperation. Resolve. The last chance of a man clinging to life.

“Cheuk,” the guide whispered. Then, louder: “Yes. If you’re a man, you take the big risks. I’ll guide you.”

“Your word,” Gaold said.

The guide stood, shoulders straightening. He tapped the pouch at his side—full of black elixir—and remembered his parents’ old saying:

*Paddle when the water comes in.*

“It is done. Wake up.”

Shirone and Fleur removed their visors and neural interface devices, sitting up from the isolation beds.

They had been quarantined for seven days. Daily physicals. Brainwave scans. Today was the final evaluation.

“In my assessment, there’s no abnormality,” the examiner said. “Quarantine measures are lifted. You may return to your accommodations.”

Shirone exhaled in relief.

Seven days of being watched, tested, unable to even use the bathroom without permission—finally over.

As they walked side by side toward the dormitory, Shirone finally spoke.

“No matter how you look at it, it’s strange, right? Why are they examining us so intensely?”

She had suspected from the beginning. The “unknown pathogen” was just an excuse.

But she hadn’t protested. Not when her hands were tied. Not when even a whisper of resistance could cost her everything.

“I doubt it too,” Fleur said. “The promised integration of the rebels is stalled. Now that we’re free, something’s changed. I’ll investigate quietly while you focus on your duties abroad.”

“Is it really necessary? Maybe it has nothing to do with us.”

Team Shirone’s mission was clear: unite the rebels.

Even if Reysis had secrets, if they were internal matters of headquarters, why dig deeper?

‘No,’ Shirone thought. ‘There must be something.’

During quarantine, all contact with the outside world had been severed. But small things—glances in the hallway, shifts in routine—had unsettled her.

‘I’ll have to search. Carefully. Without being caught.’

An old man passed by, glancing briefly at Fleur.

Reysis entered the secret laboratory beneath the Second Command.

As always, she wore her crimson cloak. Her expression was distant, lost in memory.

“You sent the specimens requested by Night Blind?”

“Yes. They’re being dismantled now. Please verify.”

On the examination table, chunks of raw meat—thick with fat—lay piled within a layer of gray, slime-coated leather.

Across from it, the creature’s head rested on a pedestal.

Reysis stared at the long, serpentine snout. The jagged teeth arranged in a circular maw.

Her breath caught.

‘It is him. I finally have him.’

Park Yo-jong Jeremong.

A Purgatory Hunt, Rank A. Possessed the ability known as *Antithesis*—a power that bound all beings except its own kind.

In essence, a law that rejected other laws. A mechanism unique to Izermont’s home world.

“I saw Night Blind,” Reysis said, smiling. “Nowremon. You acquired him faster than I expected.”

“I was surprised too,” came the voice from the shadows. “Even our elite soldiers were helpless against Antithesis.”

“Either you found a way to bypass it… or you hired someone exceptional. It could be both. Either way—” Reysis turned toward the machine at the center of the room—“activate the Fusex Machine.”

Before her stood the *Biological Mixer Fusex Machine*—a device with a four-meter glass column at its center, surrounded by eight vertices, each capped with a glowing sphere.

It was designed to fuse multiple organisms, creating a new being with combined traits.

‘I must strengthen Izermont,’ Reysis thought.

Park Yo-jong Jeremong was powerful alone. But fused with other traits—enhanced by the power of Antithesis—he could become the perfect weapon.

‘An Antithesis strong enough to suppress angels. Without that, he’s useless in war.’

Technicians poured water into the central column, then added a series of elixirs in precise proportions. Finally, the black elixir was introduced.

A long, inky liquid flowed through the hoses and into the chamber.

*Plasm*—the same substance used in Anecdote’s creation.

Plasm reacted only to organic matter, dissolving living tissue and absorbing genetic data. When mixed with other plasm and stimulated electrically, it created hybrid organisms—exactly as Anecdote had been made.

But the more species involved, the higher the risk of failure.

Which was why Reysis had been watching Shirone.

The Nephilim’s spirit was a rare variable—one that could stabilize the fusion process.

“Commander,” a technician called. “Izermont’s Plasm is complete.”

Reysis stepped forward.

Inside the chamber, the plasm pulsed, dark and viscous.

It reeked—rotten meat and ozone—but to her, it was the sweetest scent in the world.

Her eyes gleamed.

‘I will evolve endlessly.’

This was the water of life.

The path to godhood.
