Chapter 448  
Second Purgatory (5)  
~*~

Shirone's party pushed through the battlefield, where bullets rained down around them, and made their way to the command headquarters where the battalion commander was stationed.

Even inside the command post, they were close enough to the front lines that no one could hear a word without shouting. It was only after stepping into the barracks tent that the noise dropped to a bearable level.

The company commander, having already finished delivering his report, was resting in a corner of the tent. Kanya stood beside him, there to help smooth the conversation on Shirone's behalf.

The battalion commander did not look up from the strategic map even after Shirone and the others entered.

"I heard about you."

He was a cold-looking man, gray hair combed back, with a thick, stubborn-looking mustache.

"You're a Nephilim?"

Shirone didn't deny it. Whatever the truth of the matter, it seemed more useful, for the rebels' sake, to let the claim stand.

The battalion commander showed no particular interest and moved straight to his next question.

"I'll ask you plainly. Why did you come back?"

Shirone didn't understand what he meant.

"To the rebels, you're something like a myth. But myths are most beautiful while they remain myths. Some of my men consider you an irresponsible deserter. You left the battlefield once already — why come back now?"

Shirone had a feeling that the battalion commander himself was one of those who thought him a deserter.

"There's no grand reason. As before, I fight according to my own convictions, as one mage among many."

"Convictions… "

The battalion commander walked to the tent's window flap and looked out over the battlefield.

"The light you lit over District 73 has become the sun for seventy thousand rebels. I don't want to be the one who puts it out. So if you've only come back for your own beliefs, I'd rather pretend we never met."

Shirone realized the man would not be moved. What burned in those cold eyes wasn't fighting spirit — it was the grim certainty of a man who believed, from the very start, that he had chosen the wrong path.

"Even a small conviction can serve the same purpose. I'd like to speak with your commander directly. Please let me through to rebel headquarters."

"Refused."

There was an undisguised hostility in the battalion commander's eyes.

"Fine, I'll be honest with you. This war is already lost. No — it's on the brink of total defeat. If Heaven's offensive hadn't suddenly stalled, it would already be over. The gap in strength was that overwhelming. Countless soldiers have already died. The only card left to us is negotiation with Heaven. If you go stirring things up again, the light of District 73 will only get in the way."

"But the war is still going on. And right now, Heaven isn't moving. That could be an opportunity. If we can find out exactly what's happening on their side, we might have a real chance."

"You — the light of District 73."

The battalion commander pressed his palm against his face.

"Don't judge the Meccans with a Norse mindset. Yes, this rebellion started with the Norse. But now? It's only us Meccans who keep dying. Why has Heaven stopped its offensive? Who could possibly figure that out?"

"I can figure it out."

The battalion commander said nothing. His jaw worked behind closed lips, teeth grinding.

"Listen here, boy. Do you think I don't know what you're really after? No — I don't know the whole truth. But I already know you intend to use us, one way or another. People call you a myth, but to me you're nothing but a seed of disaster — at best, a legend who couldn't even bring down a single fairy manager. Do you have any idea how hard we fought while you were gone? And now, after running off at the worst possible moment, what exactly do you think you're going to change?"

"I intend to meet Ikael."

"Puh—"

A breath of laughter escaped the battalion commander's mouth.

"Puhahahaha! Fuhahahaha!"

It was almost embarrassing to hear. The Archangel Ikael — the one seated at the very top of Heaven's law. Meeting him? If it had been that simple, hundreds of thousands of his men would not already be dead.

"Now I know for certain. You're no myth. You're a slayer who seduces countless souls with a silver tongue and leads them straight to their deaths. Company Commander! Arrest this man. Don't say a word of this to the soldiers."

The company commander had no choice but to move to comply — but at that moment the tent flap burst open and an adjutant rushed in.

"Captain! Emergency! The giants have broken through the last line of defense!"

"What? Why, damn it? What idiot lured a giant here? Why?"

"Unconfirmed, sir. After the third Kuroi unit lost contact, the line kept collapsing—"

The battalion commander slammed his fist on the tactics table.

"Damn it. Why?"

Variables almost never occurred in giant elimination operations — the creatures, stripped of their identities, reacted the same way every time. But now the last line of deterrence had been broken. He had no idea where such a deviation in the law could have come from, but living through today, no different from yesterday, left him only one conclusion.

The battalion commander seized Shirone by the collar.

"You saw it, didn't you? You're the cause of all of this. You're the one who destroyed the Kuroi unit and tore a hole straight through our tactics. Now you're about to wipe out my entire battalion!"

Kanya shouted, as if ready to die on the spot.

"Captain! That's only a guess! Shirone—!"

"Shut up! Does a pilot who couldn't even protect her own machine have any right to speak right now?"

The adjutant pressed in.

"Captain, please, your orders! If we do nothing, we won't survive even thirty minutes — we'll be wiped out!"

The battalion commander grabbed a piper rifle hanging on the tent wall, slid the Ark into his pocket, took up Cigna and Xd, and turned to glare at Shirone as he left the tent.

"Get out of here. Now. I don't want to look at your face again."

Kanya called out.

"Captain! They can help us — their strength could still—"

"Didn't you hear me? Shirone is the very reason the giants' law has changed! Our job right now is to fight the enemy, not hide behind a deserter!"

When the battalion commander stormed out, Kanya had no choice but to follow. As a soldier, she couldn't simply stand by while her comrades died.

"Shirone… I'm sorry."

As she left the barracks, she gave him a sad smile.

"But it was good to see you again."

She had no idea, in that moment, that it was a goodbye.

Still, Shirone refused to let go of the thread of hope he was holding.

*A change in the law. They said the law could be altered by an expression of will. Maybe… maybe it really is possible.*

Kang Nan spoke up.

"The situation is worse than we thought. Can we really still move the rebels like this?"

Sein answered.

"There are still cards left to play. It's the same with Tagis. The problem is the chain of command. From what I've heard about the military, the location of headquarters is being kept hidden — which means there's a good chance we won't be able to get any information from the mainland side. We need to act now."

Zulu spoke.

"I think the change in the giants' law is the key issue. I don't understand why creatures whose only law is to march toward Jotunheim are suddenly acting in concert like this. Does anyone have an answer?"

No one did.

In Heaven, where the Akashic Records exist as manifest reality, the law functions as an artificial system. But in the world where Shirone and the others lived, it was simply the underlying principle of the universe itself. Just as the flap of a butterfly's wings could cause a storm somewhere else entirely, it was pointless to search for a single point where the law had been changed — countless minor variables could all be involved at once.

Gaold offered his own solution.

"First, let's wipe out all the giants. If we save enough lives here, maybe that'll be enough to get us in front of the commander, or whoever's in charge."

Shirone raised his hand.

"Leave that to me."

"No. It's too dangerous. You have a special mission to complete. Don't act as though your life belongs only to you."

"No — I think this actually concerns the law itself."

Sein asked, "You have a theory?"

"Yes. But think of it as an experiment rather than a guess. There's something I need to verify in Heaven."

* * *

"All units, attack! Do not fall back!"

Seven giants seized a single Kuroi unit, hurled it to the ground, and began stomping it beneath their feet in small, deliberate steps. The cockpit shattered. The pilot's scream cut off after barely a second.

"Shit! Aaaaaa!"

The battalion commander, arriving late to the front, fired the arc cannon in rapid succession — a barrage with no real aim, just desperation. Still, most of the shots landed true on the giant's body.

And still, the creature didn't so much as flinch. That fact alone was enough to shatter what was left of the battalion commander's composure.

"Damn it! Damn it all!"

This had never been something humans could handle, not from the very beginning. What use was it to talk of legends now? What did a fixed lifespan even mean anymore? Because of humanity's original sin against God, they had lost the path to eternal life — and now the species itself stood on the edge of extinction.

"Aaaaaaa!"

The battalion commander drove Cigna's blade down into the giant's leg. The giant slowly turned its head, registering the pain. The moment their eyes met — that dilated, empty pupil — gooseflesh crawled up his arms.

"Heresy… must be punished."

His legs trembled. He couldn't hold back the moan that leaked through his clenched teeth.

"Huh — uh — uhhhhh."

As the giant closed the distance, the battalion commander's face crumpled into something close to tears. He had seen countless deaths in this war, but the prospect of his own death was something else entirely — nothing could prepare a man for that.

"Heresy… must be punished."

"Oh, no — I am the heresy—"

As the giant's fist came crashing down, the battalion commander let out a scream with no sound left in it and squeezed his eyes shut.

*Kurrrrr…*

At the low, scraping growl, he slowly opened his eyes — and found a beast he had never seen before standing between him and the giant's fist like a wall of black.

It had sleek, jet-black fur, a slender body like a leopard's, six legs, a flattened face, and fangs jutting out well over a meter long. Cracks spread through the ground beneath its curled claws as it absorbed the full weight of the giant's blow.

"Wh-what is that thing?"

"What is it? A Tier 3 monster — a cougar."

Gaold and the others had arrived without him even noticing.

Zulu stepped forward and flicked a finger. The cougar let out an odd, almost cute noise — "*Gyareung?*" — that didn't suit its size at all, then spun around.

"Go. Play."

*Aaaaaaang!*

The cougar kicked off the ground and launched itself forward, and the battlefield seemed to open up, as if a black curtain had been torn away. Every swipe of its claws sheared straight through the giant's body. In raw physical power alone, the cougar stood at the top of all Tier 3 creatures.

The battalion commander, who had been staring at the scene as if in a trance, slowly turned his head.

"Who are you?"

Gaold pressed his lips together and answered.

"Human. Same as you."

* * *

"Lena! Lena!"

"Sister!"

Kanya threw herself at Lena, wrapping her in her arms as the younger girl fought her way between the towering giants. The sole of a giant's foot slammed down exactly where the two of them had been standing a moment before, and the ground shuddered beneath them like a trampoline.

Lena, terrified by a law of nature she had never before witnessed with her own eyes, clung to her sister and felt her over, checking for injuries.

"Sister! Are you hurt anywhere? Where's your Kuroi?"

Tears welled up in Kanya's eyes before she could even answer. *My poor little sister.* She had never done anything to deserve this, and it broke Kanya's heart that she might have to leave the world like this.

Lena smiled, as though she had already guessed something.

"Sister, I'm all right."

Kanya pressed her cheek against Lena's. Her sister was clever — of course she already understood. The corpses of their comrades lay scattered everywhere around them. There wasn't much time left to say what needed to be said.

She had wanted to say these words before the end came, because Lena had waited for her, all this time, just as much as she had waited for Lena.

"Lena… Shirone…"

"Brother Shirone?"

As Lena lifted her eyes toward the sky, Kanya's own eyes went wide with shock as she followed her sister's gaze upward.

Shirone hung in the air above them, golden wings spread wide.

*Is this what a war in Heaven looks like?*

The view of the battlefield from up high was devastating. Giants slaughtering humans looked no different from humans crushing ants underfoot.

*But I can end this. If this works…*

Shirone cast Shining Impact. A brightness equal to sunlight burst across the field, blinding everyone below. Against the darkness of the battlefield, the effect was even more overwhelming.

"Gaaaaa!"

"Damn it! What is this, all of a sudden?!"

It took a while before anyone's sight returned. Even the giants, blinded, were seized by a fear intense enough to send them into a screaming frenzy.

Then something flashed across the sky, and colorful light spilled over the night like a wave. Giants and humans alike lifted their heads as ribbons of rainbow color rippled across the darkened plain.

"Wh-what is—"

Shirone, having completed a massive ataraxia above his own head, hovered there, wings beating slowly, looking down over everyone below.

The Meccans stood frozen. In that instant, Shirone's appearance matched — precisely — the being that the rebels had learned to fear above all else.

"Ughhhh. Uhhhhh."

Hundreds of giants shook the ground as they dropped to their knees, mouths gaping like idiots, and began to weep. Giants, too, existed within the law. Even stripped of their identity, Ikael's magic circle still carried enough weight to command something deep in their instincts.

"The law… the adjuster… "

As if kneeling alone weren't enough, the giants lowered themselves further, pressing their foreheads to the ground.

The human figure among the light was finally visible to all. The soldiers below glanced at one another for a moment, then lifted their eyes back to the sky.

"The Nephilim…"

A word long buried in their minds rose unbidden from the mouths of men who had been branded heretics.

"Sister — Brother Shirone is here."

In Kanya's arms, Lena burst into tears. Even after being called a deserter, even after being accused of leading them into sin against God, she had never once doubted Shirone — and now, here he was. It felt like a miracle.

"Yes, Lena. Shirone… he really must be a descendant of angels."

Shirone had returned, wings more beautiful than any angel's, and above his head floated a colorful halo — the very symbol of an archangel. From the light of District 73, he had become the light of Heaven itself.

"Nephilim. The Nephilim."

"O adjuster of the law…"

Everyone knelt at Shirone's feet.
