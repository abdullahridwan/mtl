Chapter 448

Second Purgatory (5)

Shirone’s party pushed through the battlefield, bullets whistling past them like angry hornets, until they reached the command headquarters—a makeshift barracks where the battalion commander was stationed.

Even inside, the roar of war was deafening. Only when they stepped fully into the structure did the noise dull enough to allow conversation.

The company commander, having already delivered his report, sat slumped in a corner. Kanya stood beside him, ready to assist Shirone in navigating the tense exchange.

The battalion commander didn’t look up as they entered. His eyes remained fixed on the tactical map spread across the table.

“I’ve heard about you,” he said, voice flat.

He was a grizzled man—gray hair swept back, a thick mustache framing a mouth set in a permanent scowl. Cold, calculating.

“Are you a Nephilim?”

Shirone didn’t deny it.

It didn’t matter whether it was true. If the rebels believed it, then the label had power.

The commander didn’t seem to care either way. He moved on.

“I’ll ask directly. Why did you come back?”

Shirone hesitated. The question caught him off guard.

“You’re a legend to the rebels,” the commander continued. “And legends are most beautiful when they stay legends. To some, you’re not a hero—you’re a deserter. Why return now, after abandoning the battlefield?”

Shirone suspected the commander was one of those who saw him that way.

“There’s no grand reason,” Shirone replied. “Like before, I fight as a mage. With everything I believe in.”

“Belief…” the commander muttered.

He turned and walked to the tent’s opening, gazing out at the war-torn field.

“The light you brought to District 73—it’s become the sun for seventy thousand rebels. I don’t want to extinguish that light. But if you’ve come only for ideals, then I’d rather pretend we never met.”

Shirone understood. The man wouldn’t budge.

The coldness in his eyes wasn’t the absence of courage—it was the certainty of a man who believed he’d chosen the right path from the start, no matter how dark it had become.

“Even small convictions can lead to the same goal,” Shirone said. “I’d like to speak directly with the Commander. Please allow me to go to rebel headquarters.”

“Denied.”

Hostility flared in the commander’s gaze.

“Fine. Let’s be honest. The war is already lost. No—worse. We’re on the brink of annihilation. If Heaven hadn’t suddenly halted their offensive, it would’ve ended already. The difference in power was overwhelming. Countless soldiers died. Our only remaining card is negotiation. If you reignite the flames, you’ll only make the light in District 73 a target.”

“But the war *is* still ongoing,” Shirone countered. “And Heaven isn’t moving. That’s an opportunity. If we can understand *why*, we might still turn this around.”

“Listen, *sweetheart*,” the commander sneered, stepping closer. “Don’t judge Mecha with a Norse mindset. This rebellion was started by the Norse, yes. But who’s dying now? Only Mecha. Why did Heaven stop? Who knows? *I* don’t.”

“I can find out.”

The commander’s jaw clenched. His teeth ground together in silence.

“You think I don’t see what you’re doing? No, I don’t know the truth. But I *know* you’re using us. People call you *Shinhwa*, the myth. To me, you’re just a seed of chaos. At best, a legend who’s never beaten a fairy manager. While you were gone, we fought. We bled. And now, when you show up at the worst possible moment, what do you expect to change?”

“I will meet Ikael.”

A dry puff of air escaped the commander’s lips.

Then he laughed.

“Puhahahaha! Fuhahahaha!”

It was almost pitiful.

*Archangel Ikael?* The supreme authority of Heaven’s law?

If it were that simple, hundreds of thousands wouldn’t have died trying.

“Now I’m sure,” the commander spat. “You’re no myth. You’re a predator—charming with three tongues, leading countless souls to slaughter. Company commander! Arrest him. And don’t let the soldiers see him.”

The company commander moved to obey—but just then, the barracks flap burst open. An adjutant rushed in, breathless.

“Captain! Emergency! The giants are breaking through the final defense line!”

“What? Who the hell—? Who lured them here?!”

“It’s not confirmed. After Kuroi Group 3 lost contact, the defense collapsed…”

The commander slammed his fist onto the table.

“Damn it! Why *now*?!”

Giants were predictable. Mindless, driven by instinct. Their behavior never changed.

Yet now, the last line had fallen.

He didn’t know why. But one thing was clear—someone had altered the rules.

And his eyes snapped to Shirone.

“Did you see it? You’re the cause of this! You destroyed Kuroi, left a gap in our defenses—and now you’ll get us all killed!”

Kanya stepped forward, voice sharp.

“Commander! That’s speculation! Shirone wouldn’t—”

“Shut up!” the commander roared. “A pilot who couldn’t even protect her own Kuroi has no right to speak here!”

The adjutant pressed again.

“Captain! We need orders! If we don’t act, we’ll be wiped out in thirty minutes!”

The commander snatched a piper rifle from the wall, shoved an Ark device into his pocket, and grabbed Cigna and Xd. As he stormed out, he shot Shirone one final glare.

“Get out. I never want to see you again.”

Kanya hesitated, torn.

“Captain! They can help! Their power—”

“Did you not hear me? Shirone changed the giants’ law! Our job is to fight, not hide behind a fugitive!”

With no choice, Kanya followed.

As a soldier, she couldn’t abandon her comrades.

“Shirone,” she said, pausing at the entrance. Her voice was soft. “I’m sorry.”

She smiled sadly.

“But it was good to meet you.”

She didn’t realize it was goodbye.

But Shirone hadn’t given up.

A thread of hope still held.

*‘A change in the law… They said the law can shift with the expression of will. Maybe… maybe it’s really possible.’*

Kang Nan stepped forward.

“The situation’s worse than we thought. Can you really move the rebels like this?”

Sein replied, “There are still cards to play. Tagis, for one. The real problem is command. The military’s hiding the Commander’s location—if that’s true, we’re cut off from mainland intelligence. We need to act now.”

Zocre frowned.

“The change in the Giants’ Law is critical. Why are they acting collectively? Their only instinct is to return to Jotunheim. Who can explain this?”

No one answered.

In Heaven, the Akashic Records made the law tangible—an artificial system. But in the world Shirone lived in, the law was the universe’s fundamental principle.

Like a butterfly’s wings stirring a storm, every tiny variable mattered. To demand a single cause was futile.

Gaold offered his own solution.

“First, wipe out the giants. Save my life, and I’ll help you meet the Commander—or whoever you need.”

Shirone raised his hand.

“Leave that to me.”

“No,” Gaold snapped. “It’s too dangerous. You have a mission. Your life isn’t yours alone.”

Shirone shook his head.

“It’s about the law. I need to test something.”

Sein studied him.

“You have a theory?”

“Yes. But think of it as an experiment, not a guess. There are things I need to confirm—in Heaven.”

* * *

“All units—attack! Do not retreat!”

Seven giants surrounded a single Kuroi, slammed it to the ground, and began stomping. The cockpit shattered. The pilot screamed—then fell silent.

“Shit! Aaaaaa!”

The battalion commander arrived too late. He fired the arc in rapid succession—wild, unaimed shots.

One giant staggered.

But it didn’t fall.

It didn’t even flinch.

And that was what broke him.

“Damn it! Damn it!”

This was beyond human strength.

What was the point of stories? Of legacy? Of a lifespan fixed by divine punishment?

Because of their sin, humanity had lost eternity. Now, they faced extinction.

“Aaaaaaa!”

The commander fired Cigna at the giant’s leg. The impact made it turn—slowly.

Its pupil dilated.

The commander froze.

Goosebumps crawled up his spine.

“Heresy… Punish it.”

His teeth chattered. His legs trembled.

“Huh… uhhhhhhh…”

As the giant advanced, the commander’s face twisted—tears welled in his eyes.

He’d seen death countless times.

But *his* death? That was new.

“Heresy… Punish it.”

“No… I’m not heresy… I’m not…”

The giant raised its fist.

The commander closed his eyes, waiting for the end.

*kurrrrr…*

A low, guttural sound—like claws scraping stone.

He opened his eyes.

A beast stood before him—black as shadow, a living wall.

Silky fur. A leopard’s sleek form. Six legs. A flat, alien face. Canine fangs longer than a meter. The ground cracked beneath it, claws digging in as it blocked the giant’s strike.

“What… what is this?”

“Tier 3 monster cougar,” Gaold said, stepping forward with the others.

Zocre flicked a finger.

The cougar turned. “Gyareung?” It made a sound almost playful.

“Go play.”

*AAAAAAANG!*

The beast launched forward—like a black storm.

Every swipe of its claws tore through the giants.

Athletically, it was the peak of Tier 3—unmatched.

The battalion commander stared, dazed.

Slowly, he turned to Gaold.

“Who… are you?”

Gaold smirked.

“Human. Same as you.”

* * *

“Lena! Lena!”

“Sister!”

Kanya lunged through the chaos, tackling Lena just as a giant’s foot crashed down where they’d stood. The ground shuddered like rubber.

Lena trembled—not from injury, but from the law. Something in her had shattered.

Then she saw Kanya—alive—and threw her arms around her.

“Sister! Are you hurt? What about your Kuroi?”

Kanya couldn’t speak. Tears spilled over.

*Poor little sister.* Born into war. Never given a chance. And now, this.

Lena smiled, as if she already knew.

“Sister… I’m fine.”

Kanya pressed her cheek to Lena’s.

She was smart. She *knew*. The battlefield was littered with corpses.

There was only one thing left to say.

One thing she’d wanted to say for years.

“Lena… Shirone…”

“Shirone brother?”

Lena looked up.

And Kanya followed her gaze.

Shirone soared above them—golden wings spread, cutting through the smoke-choked sky.

*Is this a war in Heaven?*

From above, the battlefield looked like an ant colony under a boot.

Giants crushing humans. Humans screaming.

But Shirone saw a way to end it.

*If this works…*

He cast *Shining Impact*.

Light—bright as the sun—erupted.

In the darkness, it was blinding.

“Gaaaaa!”

“Damn it! What the hell is that?!”

For a moment, the world went white.

When sight returned, the giants were disoriented. Fear twisted their faces.

Then—something flashed in the sky.

Colorful lights bloomed across the night.

Rainbow streaks danced over the plain as shadows deepened.

“I—that…”

Shirone hovered above, wings beating slowly. Above his head, a massive *Ataraxia* shimmered into place—a symbol of divine calm.

The Meccans froze.

Because now, Shirone looked exactly like the being they feared.

“Ughhhh… uhhhhh…”

Hundreds of giants dropped to their knees. Their mouths hung open. Tears streamed down their faces.

The law still lived in them.

Ikael’s magic circle—etched into their instincts—still resonated.

“The Law… Coordinator…”

As if kneeling wasn’t enough, they lowered themselves further—foreheads pressed to the earth.

The human form was revealed.

Yet, after a moment, they looked at each other—then back to the sky.

“The Nephilim…”

The word, long buried, rose from the throats of heretics.

“Sister,” Lena whispered, tears streaming. “Shirone brother is here.”

In Kanya’s arms, she wept.

Even if others called him a deserter, even if they said he’d betrayed God—she’d never doubted him.

And now, this—this was her miracle.

“Yes, Lena,” Kanya murmured. “Shirone… he truly must be an angel’s descendant.”

He returned—golden wings brighter than any angel’s, a radiant halo above his head, the mark of an archangel.

From the light of District 73… to the light of Heaven itself.

“Nephilim. Nephilim.”

“O Adjuster of the Law…”

One by one, they knelt.

At Shirone’s feet, the battlefield fell silent.

All bowed.
