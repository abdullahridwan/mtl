Opportunity for Revenge (2)

Upon arriving at the Second Headquarters, Shirone and Fleur were ushered into a room and told to wait.

Of course, “waiting” was Cho Won’s polite way of putting it. Shirone knew better—it was quarantine.

Fleur, who had been silently observing the activity outside through the narrow window, finally sat down beside him.

“Now talk,” she said. “What happened?”

Shirone exhaled and began recounting the events inside Babel’s cockpit. He explained how the Ultima System unified all signals and transmitted them, how it had accessed Babel’s historical records, and what he had discovered.

Fleur listened intently, then took a slow breath. “I’ve heard fragments about the history of Heaven,” she said, “but this… this is something entirely different.”

“Don’t you think the person whose records were meant to be erased in the Babel Project might be connected to the Reset?” Shirone asked.

McLean Guffin.

He was almost certain.

More than anything, it was the revelation that Guffin had been involved in Ikael’s great sin that shook him.

“Now that I think about it,” Shirone continued, “Guffin must have had a reason to leave—something unrelated to the affairs of this world. That’s why he chose such a complicated method: initialization followed by reconstruction.”

“That makes sense,” Fleur agreed. “But I wonder if it’s tied to the Ultima System itself. What exactly *is* that system?”

“It’s less like language and more like sensation,” Shirone explained. “Just as you feel heat when you touch fire, or pain when pricked by a needle—those signals come through with infinite clarity. The Ultima System doesn’t interpret; it *experiences*.”

“Hmm… I think I understand,” Fleur murmured. “I can grasp it intellectually, but unless you’ve felt it yourself, you can’t truly know.”

“In that sense,” Shirone said, “the Ultima System is the opposite of the Akashic Records. The Akashic Records preserve the whole even if the parts change. But the Ultima System distinguishes every single part with perfect precision. It’s no exaggeration to say that if all Gaia’s people were linked through it, we could have stood against Ankera.”

“But wouldn’t its power weaken as the number of participants decreases?”

“Yes,” Shirone admitted. “Right now, I can only interpret the signals. I can’t generate or amplify them.”

Fleur shook her head. “That’s still an incredible ability. It’s already useful here, where we don’t even speak the language. And in the future? The applications are endless.”

Shirone gave a dry, humorless chuckle. “Assuming we survive long enough to use it.”

Fleur smiled faintly at the grim joke.

“But anyway,” she said, shifting gears, “where did Babel go? If it’s a weapon against Heaven, did it head there?”

“It’s possible,” Shirone said. “Either way, I should report this to Mr. Sein. This could be a major variable.”

“Good idea. And while we’re at it, let’s ask Mr. Gardlock for help. Sounds like you’re not too fond of staying here.”

“Neither is Clove,” Shirone replied.

They both laughed—briefly, bitterly—before the silence returned.

* * *

Heaven’s Sixth Heaven, Jebul.

Since Arius had entered Dremo, Kariel had not left the Great World War system.

Miro remained unconscious, showing no sign of waking.

It wouldn’t be easy.

Even for someone as skilled in the intricacies of the human mind as Kariel, extracting a single consciousness from the dense maze of Drimo—where mental energy coiled like storm clouds—was like casting a fish into the ocean and expecting to catch it again ten years later.

‘If it were Brahma, he’d handle himself,’ Kariel thought.

Mara had the sharpest insight among the Mara, and even Kariel trusted her judgment.

Still, a shadow lingered on Kariel’s beautiful face as she stared at the central processing unit.

Then, suddenly, a long-dormant data category flickered on the screen.

The timestamp was ancient—so old that even angelic memory blurred around it.

Kariel’s eyes lit up with curiosity.

“This?”

She studied the screen for a long moment, then frowned.

Babel had descended.

Tracking attempts confirmed it: a red dot flashed, moving at high speed over Purgatory.

“But how?”

During the war with Gaia, Kariel herself had sabotaged Babel’s production.

As the Archangel of Creation, she had despised Uriel’s methods—destruction was counterproductive, irreversible, foolish.

Instead, she had subtly altered the algorithms embedded in Babel’s core programming, redefining its perceived enemies: Heaven was no longer a target, but a subject to be preserved.

That single act had been the primary reason the Second Gaia War had failed.

So who had reactivated it?

After Guffin’s erasure, there were no pure Nephilim left in Purgatory. And no angel would defy Ankera’s will to awaken Babel.

Curious, Kariel pulled up Babel’s transmitted data and displayed it on the main screen.

As the program activated, the view shifted to a first-person perspective.

Then a face appeared—one Kariel knew all too well.

As Babel closed in, the image zoomed in sharply.

And then, decisively, Babel ceased its attack protocol.

If the boy on the screen was who Kariel thought he was, the decision made perfect sense.

“Quack.”

A thrill surged through Kariel’s soul. A laugh burst from deep within her chest—wild, unrestrained.

“Ha ha ha ha! Ha ha ha ha!”

Finally. Her chance had come.

All beings with human blood deserved death.

But among them, Shirone was special.

The human Ikael loved most.

“Will you still hold your head high when you see this?” Kariel whispered, her voice trembling with dark delight.

For the first time in ages, she stepped away from the Great World War.

The first image that came to mind was Ikael’s pale, serene face.

* * *

Heaven’s Seventh Heaven, Araboth.

In the sacred realm where Ankera resided, there was a secluded chamber—once the dwelling of Archangel Ikael, now a place of rest for the fallen sovereign.

Kariel strode down the long, luminous hallway, a triumphant smile playing on her lips.

She had no intention of reporting Shirone’s location.

Though Ikael’s power was sealed, the name still carried weight—a symbol of glory among the angels.

Too soon, perhaps, to act.

But the mere thought of what was to come sent a shiver of pleasure through her.

‘Where can I see that pretty face now? I’m in charge, after all.’

Just before reaching Ikael’s chamber, space itself cracked open before her.

Hundreds of glass panels shimmered into existence, glowing with varying intensities.

As the blurred silhouette coalesced, the figure of a man materialized—a signal encoded within a specific type of panel.

Ashur, Ikael’s three-legged Mara, blocked her path, his eyes as deep and dark as his ink-black hair.

“What business do you have in Araboth, Angel of Creation?”

“I’ve come to see Ikael,” Kariel said smoothly. “Are you going to let me pass?”

She moved forward as if he hadn’t spoken.

Ashur stepped in again, blocking her way.

Kariel’s face twisted in fury.

“Are you seriously standing in my way? A mere Mara?”

She remembered clearly—Ashur had done the same before, appearing with sword drawn just as she was about to capture Shirone.

Back then, she had obeyed Ikael’s order and let him go.

This time, she would not be stopped.

“Ikael does not wish to see anyone,” Ashur said firmly.

“*No one*?” Kariel sneered. “An angel on probation dares to dream so boldly? Step aside, insignificant creature.”

She shoved Ashur aside—hard.

Before he could recover, she threw open the door.

Inside, Ikael sat on the floor, bathed in soft white light. Pure. Still. Waiting.

“It’s been a while, Kariel.”

Kariel forced a smile, though it strained her face.

Even now, standing before Ikael, her celestial form instinctively shrank—but she refused to show weakness today.

“Hmph. Sitting on the ground like a beggar. Where is the authority of the archangel you once were?”

Ikael smiled gently. “Your presence has weakened considerably. Then again, you’ve never known how to focus. Always chasing after the next distraction.”

It was true. Kariel’s form was visibly deteriorated. Her once-sharp eyes—those brilliant, gun-metal irises that had conceived a thousand wonders—were now clouded.

“What do *you* know?” she snapped. “Ra has forbidden angelic activity. You’re powerless now. You must be dying to know what I’m planning. No—*crazy* with curiosity. But what can you do? You’re just a puppet, clinging to a name you no longer deserve.”

“Your anxiety is childish,” Ikael said softly. “Like a child crying for milk. What are you so afraid of?”

“Afraid?” Kariel’s eyes widened. “Ha ha ha! I fear nothing! I am greater than you ever were! Who do you think I—!”

Her voice cut off abruptly.

Ikael began to untie the fastenings of his pure white robes.

The collar loosened. The fabric slipped from one shoulder, then the other, revealing the smooth, flawless skin beneath.

Kariel’s breath caught.

She saw the straight line of his neck, the lean strength in his arms, the perfect symmetry of his eight-headed form—no flaw, no imperfection.

“Come here, Kariel,” Ikael said, his voice tender. “You were always a special child. Hold me, like you used to. Tell me your stories. Then rest. Sleep well. And when you wake, everything will be as it should.”

“…uh…”

Kariel took an involuntary step forward, dazed.

Then she stopped herself.

Even now, even after everything, Ikael was beautiful.

The most perfect form in existence—pure white, untouched, absolute.

The ultimate purity, free of even the smallest impurity.

Just as before, she wanted to embrace him. To press her cheek against his shoulder. To whisper all her secrets.

Yes. That was all.

A perfect ataraxia—complete peace, found in simply being near him.

‘She was like that… her… her…!’

Only one being had defiled it.

“*Human…!*”

Kariel’s planetary body began to spin, expanding into a radiant halo.

The room trembled. Ikael’s golden hair fell over his face, hiding it in sorrow.

Kariel lunged forward, screaming.

“Shut up! How dare you say that?! You—You—! *Keugh!*”

The psychic backlash overwhelmed her. She staggered, clutching her head.

Her already-fragile presence teetered on the edge of collapse.

‘Damn it! Why…?’

Never—*never*—had she desired to possess Ikael.

Her awe had been pure, untouched by human greed or jealousy.

There was a time when simply *seeing* Ikael had filled her with absolute happiness.

‘Stupid humans! Ugly, grasping things!’

Why couldn’t they leave beauty untouched?

Did they have to *own* it? Couldn’t they just *admire*?

Everyone wanted Ikael’s love. Was that why they had to *take* her from those who truly understood her?

To Kariel, Ikael was no longer sacred.

She was a withered flower—ripped from its stem by the hands of an insignificant human.

“I won’t forgive you,” Kariel hissed, pressing close to Ikael’s bowed form. “I *never* forget. So I’ll do the one thing you hate most. Because you did the one thing *I* hate most.”

With that, she turned and left.

Behind her, Ikael lifted his head slightly, his expression sorrowful.

“Ashur.”

At the sound of his voice, the Mara prostrated himself instantly.

His face was twisted with shame—his master had been insulted, and he had been powerless to stop it.

“Your eternal servant answers the call.”

“Find out what Kariel is planning.”

Ashur hesitated. Though his power had diminished alongside Ikael’s sealed strength, he would never refuse a command.

But…

“The atmosphere in Heaven is unstable,” he said carefully. “If another incident like this occurs…”

Ikael silenced him with a gentle, radiant smile.

“It won’t. Please.”

No matter the sins, no matter the fall—*Ikael was Ikael*.

There were countless angels and Mara who would die for that smile.

Ashur bowed his head in silence.

Then his body fragmented into hundreds of glass panels—and vanished in an instant.
