Chapter 444  
Second Purgatory (1)  
~*~

The Arun Mountains, in the northeast of the Tormia Kingdom, were steeped in an ancient, oppressive aura. Deep beneath their peaks lay the long-sealed prison of Belkyr, a demon god bound by mages of old—those who once upheld the natural law, even when the world had forgotten what magic truly meant.

But millennia had passed. The will of righteousness had weakened, and through the cracks in the seal, Belkyr’s malevolence seeped into the world once more.

Now, resurrection had begun.

With a roar that shattered bedrock, Belkyr burst through twenty meters of underground stone, emerging into the hollow heart of the mountain range. Towering at fifteen meters and weighing thirty-two tons, the demon resembled a grotesque fusion of octopus and serpent. Its skin was blackened, as though rotted by time itself. Two humanoid arms—each a writhing mass of colorful, living snakes—extended from its torso. Its face was reptilian, monstrous, with eye sockets filled only with flickering red flames.

On dozens of undulating limbs, Belkyr surged forward like a tidal wave.

“I will destroy the world!” it bellowed. “I will crush all life beneath my feet!”

That was its only creed. Its only law.

Yet the grandeur of ages past met the harsh reality of the present. Humanity had evolved. Civilization no longer crumbled at the mere sight of a demon.

The Mage Sect, having traced Belkyr’s location through ancient texts, had formally requested cooperation from the Kingdom’s Ministry of Defense. With royal approval, a Cage-level extermination party was formed—twenty-one elite mages descending into the Arun caves.

Eight hours and thirty-seven minutes later, the battle neared its end.

Cage B Team—composed of official 4th to 6th class mages—had severed all of Belkyr’s legs and now pressed the final assault.

“QUAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAA!”

Belkyr writhed, dragging itself forward with its serpentine arms. Even with its regenerative powers, the sheer magical output of twenty-one high-tier mages overwhelmed the ancient demon.

“Insignificant worms!” it shrieked. “How dare you raise your hands against me? AAAAAAGH!”

A massive X-shaped gash split its torso open, flesh tearing as thick, slimy fluid gushed out. Into the wound leapt a fat, bald man, dual tanning knives flashing in a blur.

“Is the X just decoration? Huh? Decoration? Huh? Decoration?” he cackled, hacking deeper.

Twenty-eight powerful buffs surged through his body. His arms moved without pause, carving into Belkyr’s core, tearing toward the heart.

When he finally reached it—a pulsing mass of light purple muscle—his face flushed with ecstasy.

And he bit into it.

With a savage yank, he tore the heart free, slicing through the last tendons with a razor-sharp blade. The organ dropped into his waiting mouth.

The moment it touched his tongue, raw, electric flavor exploded through his skull like fireworks.

“Oooh! Oohhhh!”

**Meat Addict Benifis**

Certified 5th Class Battle Mage

*Amplify omnipotence through the joy of eating game meat*

Special Note: Operates a butcher’s shop

“End it… now…” Belkyr gasped, clinging to existence.

Any ordinary being would have perished long ago. But demons were born of obsession—of relentless, unyielding will.

“I… cannot vanish like this…”

“Sorry,” came a soft voice. “I’m so sorry.”

A woman with waist-length gray hair and a deathly pale face cradled the dying demon. Each time her hand brushed its body, the writhing flesh stilled.

“How much does it hurt?” she whispered, giggling. “Hehehe… Sorry. I can’t help it… Hee hee hee…”

Tears streamed from her wide eyes, even as her mouth twisted into a grotesque, manic grin. The contrast was chilling.

**Saint Moriac**

Certified 6th Class Healing Mage

*Wide-area healing magic reaching 100 meters in diameter, long-lasting*

Peculiarity: Manic Severe

Once, she had been revered—nearly divine. In the human world, she was called a saint. In heaven, she might have been seen as a two-legged mara. Now, she was merely a demon, weeping over another demon’s end.

Belkyr finally understood. It was not just wounded. It was *defeated*.

For eight hours, the mages had fought with abilities that mirrored its own—sealed, refined, and turned against it.

As the demon faltered, Rose, leader of Cage B Team, turned to the man who had been lounging in the corner, idly smoking.

“Hey! Aren’t you going to finish this? Do you have any idea how much this operation costs per hour? Does the Association just hand out money like dirt?”

“Ah, that witch,” Tarvan muttered, flicking his cigarette aside.

He stepped forward. Belkyr was already broken—compact, vulnerable.

*Stop wasting time, you jingling bastard.*

Tarvan raised his hands. From his palms erupted a high-frequency sound that pierced the air like a blade.

**Kaiser Blast.**

At the mere mention of the spell, every mage scattered.

As the sound waves struck, Belkyr’s massive body began to vibrate violently.

“Remember, humans! I—!”

**WOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOH!**

The explosion consumed everything. Belkyr vanished—its form erased, its flesh vaporized by the heat.

“Mission complete.”

Exactly eight hours and forty-nine minutes after their arrival.

At that moment, the communication mage—slumped against the cave wall, half-asleep—opened his eyes.

A crystal ball before him flickered to life, displaying a stream of data—over four hundred lines of code, all to deliver a single message.

“Manager. Manager.”

Rose turned. The mage lifted the crystal ball into the air.

“It’s an Association call. Gather all of Team B in the cage.”

**Magic Association – 18th Floor**

The hallway outside the president’s office buzzed with tension. Wizards of Cage B Team stood scattered, restless, their expressions sour.

These were the kingdom’s finest—selected not for teamwork, but for individual excellence. Most were already stretched thin by royal duties, research, or private contracts. Being summoned *after* a grueling mission was an insult.

“I need to get home,” one muttered. “My baby’s sick. Are they seriously asking for more?”

“Where’s the team leader?” another asked, turning to Horkin. “You’re the oldest. You know something, right?”

Horkin, the de facto senior of Cage B and keeper of protocol, offered only a cryptic smile.

Then the office door opened.

Rose stepped out.

The wizards surged forward.

Her expression told them everything.

“Team leader,” someone asked, “what did the president say?”

Rufist currently held the title of Association President, but few outside the inner circle cared. To most, the title meant nothing—only the settlement terms mattered.

“It’s an urgent mission,” Rose said. “You’re being redeployed.”

Silence. Then, a collective chill. Cold stares swept the hallway.

Saint Moriac stepped forward. “Why us? There’s Cage A Team, isn’t there?”

There was little difference in raw skill between A and B. Tormia wasn’t so weak that it couldn’t field forty elite mages.

But Cage A was a cohesive unit—long-standing, synchronized. Cage B was assembled case by case, a temporary coalition of specialists.

Logically, A should have been chosen.

Horkin spoke. “I suggested it. But Cage A is currently unavailable.”

“Because of Gaold,” Tarvan added.

“Exactly. Team A has someone presumed to be Gaold’s personal guard. Until the investigation concludes, we’re the only active Cage-level unit in the kingdom.”

Still, murmurs rose.

“This is ridiculous,” one mage snapped. “Didn’t the team leader request the contract in the first place? And Roche’s daughter—she’s barely a toddler. She’s being beaten by Jesu’s men.”

“Please, Chief,” another pleaded. “Let me out. I’ll pay the penalty.”

Rose’s eyes narrowed.

“Don’t misunderstand,” she said, voice icy. “This isn’t a guild. Settlements are just formalities at the Association level. A first-class mobilization order has been issued by the Kingdom. Refusal means disqualification from the Red Line—and punishment under royal law. Do you have any idea what the political climate is like right now? You’d better learn to crawl.”

The wizards fell silent.

The Red Line was everything. To lose it was to lose status, privilege, protection. There was no alternative.

Rose’s tone softened slightly.

“As your leader, I apologize. But upon mission completion, the Association has promised substantial rewards. Triple the standard down payment. And reputation points equivalent to a special promotion.”

Money was one thing. But *reputation points*—those were power. For 6th-class mages, promotion required an immense accumulation. This mission could secure a lifetime of advancement.

Horkin exhaled. “So… what are we being sent to do?”

Rose hesitated. This was the hard part.

“The target… is human.”

A beat. Then disbelief.

“Human? Who?”

Rose’s gaze sharpened.

“Eliminate Mikea Gaold—the former president of the Tormia Magic Association.”

**Purgatory, Outside Heaven – Forest of the Vulnerable**

Beyond the tunnel, where light flowed like a rushing river, Shirone emerged into a vast, open expanse.

Above, the concentric rings of Heaven shimmered through a sea of clouds. In one distant sector, drones swarmed like gnats.

Shirone took a deep breath and prepared to descend.

As expected, gravity shifted abruptly. He dropped vertically.

The sky vanished. The sea below gave way to forest.

He kept his eyes wide open until the ground rushed up—then twisted his body midair.

“Whoa.”

The landing was far smoother than before. But no one praised him.

The nine others who had passed through Guffin’s Door first were scattered, gazing in different directions, lost in thought.

Sein spoke.

“This is the Forest of the Profane. Home of the monopods.”

“Single ears,” someone murmured. “Quiet place. Shall we go?”

*Secluded,* Shirone thought.

He remembered being surrounded by poisonous ears—how it had led him to Kanya and Lena, how it had opened the path to Heaven. But it had never been safe.

*Well, maybe it *is* secluded… for them.*

As they walked, Sein explained.

Purgatory was divided into three main regions: the cults’ habitat, the mainland, and the outskirts of the community. The outskirts were where non-subjects lived—those outside the system. They were known as borderline people.

Sein’s destination was the mainland—a neutral zone where heretics and vigilantes gathered. The only place to get real information.

Midway through the forest, the ground trembled.

The party froze, scanning for threats. The shaking faded. They exchanged glances and pressed on.

Then—

“Oh my god…”

Below, on the plains beyond the mountain, hundreds of giants marched in a single file.

Created from Anecdotal Sake.

Back then, their existence had been shocking—so much so that they hadn’t stopped to question it. But now, Sein explained: they were en route to Jotunheim, the land of giants.

There, they would reclaim their true identities and return to Heaven to uphold the law.

Fail, and they would be cast into Muspel—the land of fire.

“Giants are powerful,” Sein said. “But they’re just monsters. Best to ignore them and move on.”

Without a sound, the ten figures slipped deeper into the forest, vanishing into the shadows.
