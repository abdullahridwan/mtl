Chapter 445  
Second Purgatory (2)  
~*~

Shirone and the others advanced toward the mainland.

Traveling by Zulu’s summoned beast would have been faster, but it wasn’t a viable option—not with the unknown dangers lurking in Purgatory. The skies above were patrolled by angels, Maras, and drones, and Shirone had no intention of being ambushed beyond the reach of Etela’s Spirit Zone. Staying within its protective radius was essential.

Yet, contrary to their expectations, the Profane Forest was eerily quiet.

They had anticipated constant aerial patrols, divine enforcers hunting heretics and rebels across the land. Instead, the forest lay still—unnaturally so.

Kang Nan frowned. “How should we interpret this? Is it an unexpected shift… or proof that the rebels have already been wiped out?”

“Either is possible,” Sein replied.

Fleur tilted her head. “Is there really a chance the forest could be this quiet *without* the rebels being destroyed?”

“By common sense, no,” Sein said. “But then again, the maze vanished too. Nothing follows logic here anymore. We can’t assume anything.”

A chill ran through her. The distortion she’d felt in the maze’s spacetime had returned—subtle, but undeniable. And now, Purgatory itself defied expectations. That only deepened her unease.

*Twenty years I’ve planned this. Twenty years of waiting, watching, preparing. So why is everything unraveling before it even begins?*

Can I trust my own senses?

*Maybe it’s all coincidence. People always find patterns after the fact, shaping causes to fit outcomes.*

She pushed the doubts aside.

*Stop overthinking. Both possibilities must be accounted for. I can handle it.*

“There’s someone nearby.”

Ethella halted, turning sharply to the right.

Shirone hadn’t sensed anything—but then, he wasn’t relying on his own senses. Etela’s Spirit Zone, spanning two kilometers in every direction, fed him a flood of data.

Twenty figures, moving with unnatural speed.

The clash of steel. Explosions. The sounds reached him not through his ears, but through synesthesia—filtered through the Spirit Zone’s expanded perception.

“It’s a battle,” Shirone said. “About 800 meters ahead.”

Fleur glanced at Gaold. “Are you sure we should move?”

It was a request for permission. In this unpredictable environment, caution would have been justified. But Gaold had never been one for hesitation.

“Check it out,” he ordered.

In an instant, the entire team blurred into motion—vanishing like shadows swept away by wind.

Quan and Kang Nan were carried by Zulu’s magic, but the rest were seasoned operatives. Navigating the dense, tangled forest posed no challenge. Even Shirone, who had mastered movement control at the senior level, felt the gap in skill. The leaders—Gaold, Sein, Zulu, and Armin—moved like phantoms, their steps defying terrain as if gravity itself bowed to them.

*How long does it take to reach that level?* Shirone wondered.

They arrived just as the silence shattered.

The forest erupted with noise.

“Follow me! Rip out their hearts!”

“Kyayayayayaya!”

Black-armored Kergos charged through the trees, pursuing a group of Mechas whose bodies were augmented with mechanical exoskeletons. The Kergos moved like a storm, their hooves tearing through undergrowth—but the Mechas were faster.

Shirone’s eyes narrowed.

The Mecha warriors’ mobility came from a skeletal framework fused to their backs. Metal spines ran from neck to legs, branching into arms, joints clicking with each motion—*Ching! Ching!*—amplifying every step. Each leap covered five, six meters, as if springs were built into their bones.

“Piper,” Shirone murmured. “A strength amplifier. These are Mecha fighters.”

They darted between trees like coiled springs released, but the Kergos were relentless hunters.

One warrior burst from the foliage, cutting off a Mecha’s escape.

“Hah!”

The Mecha raised an Xd—a holographic shield flared into existence, repelling the blow with a shockwave. But the Kergo’s raw strength overwhelmed the defense. The Mecha staggered, then collapsed.

Fellow soldiers rushed to his side, scanning the trees.

Too late.

Kergo warriors emerged from all sides, sealing off the retreat. They had been herded.

The short-haired woman leading the Mechas whispered into a drone mounted on her wrist.

“This is the Second Reconnaissance Team. We’re surrounded by Kergos. Request immediate support.”

No response.

Radio silence.

*Please… please let this get through…*

Twenty meters away, Shirone and the others watched, hidden.

The Kergos prided themselves on tracking and pursuit—but against Sein’s *Equilibrium*, their senses were useless. Within the Spirit Zone, even the sound of breathing was crushed into silence. The five senses meant nothing.

Kang Nan turned to Gaold. “Who do we back? Kergos or Mechas?”

This was their first real decision in Heaven.

The three dominant races—Kergo, Mecha, and Nor—each had distinct cultures, combat styles, and ideologies. Choosing which side to support would shape their path to the maze. One wrong move could close off vital routes.

*Rustle.*

Bushes parted. A Kergo scout patrolled the perimeter.

He froze.

Ten people stood where moments before there had been nothing—not even a flicker on his sensors.

Heart pounding, he turned to flee.

“Fem—”

Gaold moved.

“—ale…”

A single strike. The scout’s head exploded like a crushed melon.

Shirone and Fleur stared, stunned, at the twitching corpse—faceless, collapsing like a puppet with cut strings.

Then they understood.

Gaold hadn’t hesitated. No warning. No negotiation. Just elimination.

*If you judge that killing is necessary, then kill. I’ll handle the consequences. Even if it was a mistake, the only outcome worse than a wrong choice is the death of a teammate.*

It was an unspoken rule. A license to kill.

Cold. Efficient. Ruthless.

And that was precisely why Gaold was their commander.

Kang Nan glanced at him. “Now that we’ve done this… we’re siding with the Mechas?”

Humans valued many things above taking a life. But the Kergos were bound by divine law. Once blood was spilled, compromise became nearly impossible. The Mechas, though wary, were more pragmatic.

“For Anchera!” the Mecha squad leader shouted.

Gaold’s expression darkened. “We’re going with the Mechas.”

Quan was already moving.

A blur. A shadow.

Perfect for this kind of work.

“Squad leader! If we don’t break out now, we’re finished!” a Mecha cried.

They were barely holding on. The Piper enhanced muscle strength, but only in major limbs. Against Kergos—entire bodies honed into weapons—close combat was suicide.

“Fall back! I’ll hold them!” the Mecha leader yelled, leaping to the front.

She knew what awaited her if captured. But she had to buy time.

“Cut off her limbs! Bring back the torso!” a Kergo roared.

The words sent ice through her veins.

She wanted to run—but Kergo blades were already descending.

*Whoa!*

A flash.

A streak.

The lead Kergo warrior’s head *popped*—severed clean, flying like a cork from a bottle.

‘Seven left,’ Quan assessed mid-air, already spinning.

Six more fell in a single arc. No one saw the twelve micro-adjustments in his blade’s path—each cut precise, lethal.

“Enemies! Kill them!” the remaining Kergos screamed.

Only now, after losing half their number, did they realize the true threat.

Quan twisted through the air, threads of external gravity pulling him in three directions at once. He swung like a pendulum, then lunged—blood spraying from four throats.

‘Three left.’

Another head flew.

‘Two.’

The Kergo unit commander’s eyes widened in fury.

They used external gravity too—but only to enhance slashes. Quan’s movements were something else entirely. Unpredictable. Mocking.

“Don’t mock Kergo techniques!” the vice-captain snarled, swinging his crescent blade.

Quan’s path bent—repelled like a magnet with matching poles—sliding past the strike.

“Ha! Got you!” the vice-captain grinned.

A trap.

A second warrior, hidden in the trees, dropped down—sword aimed at Quan’s exposed back.

“Got you now—”

The blade passed through empty air.

Quan hovered above, motionless.

He shot the warrior a look of mild annoyance—then turned away.

His body spun like a top. An invisible thread yanked him forward—straight to the unit commander.

“Captain! Behind—!”

Too late.

Quan’s sword slit the commander’s throat.

Gravity released. Quan landed lightly, spun, and hurled his blade.

It flew faster than an arrow—piercing the ponytailed warrior through the gut and pinning him to a tree.

Silence.

The Kergo unit was gone.

Annihilated in less than five seconds.

Only then did Shirone and the others step into the clearing.

Shirone stared, awed. He’d heard legends of Quan’s skill—but seeing it firsthand was something else.

Yet Quan’s face was twisted in frustration.

*Damn it… How far have I fallen?*

In his prime, he would have slaughtered them all before the first breath of external gravity faded.

Now? He’d needed a secret technique just to finish the last one.

“Quan-san,” Shirone said, stepping forward. “Are you hurt? Even if you are—”

Quan wiped the scowl from his face. “Sorry. My balance isn’t perfect yet.”

Shirone tilted her head. As a sorceress, she couldn’t sense the flaws in his form.

After a pause, she offered what she could. “It was incredible. I don’t see swordsmanship often, but… today broadened my understanding.”

Quan looked away, embarrassed.

As she walked past, Shirone glanced back.

*Did I say something wrong?*

The Mechas, meanwhile, tensed as Gaold approached.

They owed their lives to these strangers—but in Purgatory, trust was rare.

And one swordsman had just wiped out an entire Kergo unit.

They were clearly outmatched.

“Who are you?” the Mecha leader demanded. “Heretics? Rebels?”

Gaold glanced at Sein.

Only some of the team understood Heaven’s language. The rest relied on Sein’s spirit magic for translation.

Once the words were conveyed, Gaold stepped forward.

The Mechas flinched.

Their Piper systems whirred, joints tightening defensively.

But Gaold didn’t attack.

He simply waited.

And the forest held its breath.
