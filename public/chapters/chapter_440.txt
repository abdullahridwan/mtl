Chapter 440  
Task Force (2)  
~*~

A two-hour carriage ride from the city of Creas brought one to the small port town of San Ferros. Outside the holiday season, Galliant—normally a world-class resort—was nothing more than a quiet fishing village nestled by the sea.

Shirone arrived around lunchtime and bought a handful of fish fillets from a roadside stall. The local specialty, fish pho, was said to be best when slightly spoiled. It had a jelly-like chewiness and a deep, briny flavor that lingered on the tongue.

There were no passenger liners running today, so hiring a private captain was the only option. But the fare was steep—more than the money he’d managed to save. When he asked the villagers, they told him the fishing boats that had left at dawn would return in about an hour.

With nowhere else to go, Shirone walked to the breakwater and gazed out at the distant horizon. It wasn’t the first time he’d traveled alone, but standing there, he felt utterly isolated. His teammates were already waiting for him on the island, yet he couldn’t shake the sensation of being left behind.

‘School must be at lunchtime by now,’ he thought.

But he didn’t linger on the memory. There was no place to return to. There never had been.

“Are you Arian Shirone?”

The voice came from behind. A woman stood there, dressed in attire unlike anything seen in the Tormia Kingdom. Even in the warm weather, her sleeves extended to her wrists, and a thick hood hung down her back—garments heavier than winter wear. Her face was partially obscured, but her eyes were deep and enigmatic, radiating an aura that made it impossible to stare too long.

Shirone’s attention shifted to the creature floating above her head. It was soft and translucent, a spherical being spinning slowly within, generating a faint electric field. An auto prism—used to scatter light and create invisibility in a localized area. Though it had little combat value, it was classified as a Tier 4 summon, prized for its utility in stealth and reconnaissance.

Shirone tensed, hand instinctively moving toward Armand at his side. If this woman could summon a Tier 4 entity, she was no ordinary mage. In terms of raw ability, he would be at a disadvantage.

“Who are you?” he asked. “And how do you know my name?”

“My name is Zulu,” the woman replied. “Gaold sent me to bring you.”

“…Zulu?” Shirone blinked, momentarily thrown by the strange phrasing. He looked her in the eye. “Zulu?”

There was no way he hadn’t heard of her. The only summoning mage in the world capable of matching a Tier 1 lich—legendary, unmatched. Disbelief flickered across his face as he pointed at her.

“…Zulu?”

“Yes. Zulu.” She nodded, unfazed. “I don’t have much time. We need to cross the border before dark. Follow me.”

“The head of the Association—where is he?”

“They’ve already arrived at Galliant and are preparing for the Gangran. Are you coming or not?”

“I’ll go.”

Shirone abandoned his search and stepped forward. Zulu dismissed the auto prism and cast a mass teleportation spell.

The world twisted, folding inward as if pulled toward a single point—then snapped open into a new landscape. They stood halfway up a mountain behind the port. The breakwater where they had just been was now a tiny speck in the distance, and below, the fishing village clung to the shore.

From the air, Zulu summoned Kaidra.

The creature was grotesque—smooth, hairless skin stretched over a long, pointed snout, massive claws gripping the earth like anchors. Despite its monstrous appearance, Kaidra nuzzled its beak against Zulu like a child seeking affection, then lowered one wing in invitation.

Shirone climbed onto the wing behind Zulu. A ridge of bone protruded along Kaidra’s spine like a shark’s fin, cutting through the wind and creating a stable hollow where riders could sit securely.

“Hold on,” Zulu said. “We’re going high.”

Kaidra flapped its enormous wings and launched into the sky, climbing rapidly. Shirone gripped the spine, his pale face turned upward as the ground fell away beneath them. Soon, the village was a speck, the coastline a thin line.

“Kaidra is a lift-based rogue,” Zulu explained over the wind. “They require less oxygen, so they can fly at altitudes beyond the reach of most creatures. This reduces the chance of detection by Joner’s search algorithm.”

Scanning the skies wasn’t difficult for those with a spirit zone—especially one with a base diameter of two kilometers. But as altitude increased, maintaining that range required shifting the zone into a narrow, targeted mode, drastically reducing coverage.

Only then did it fully sink in.

This woman—Zulu—was real.

“Thank you for coming to get me,” Shirone said, voice tight with awe. “I never imagined someone of your stature would come in person.”

“Gaold said the Association would move soon,” Zulu replied. “There’s stillness inside—something’s brewing. I was sent ahead to secure the path. Transport duty fell to me. Right now, I’m on my way to pick up a woman named Shiina.”

“I see.”

“Of course, I’d have come regardless. Sein said we should use every resource available to achieve results. I’ll be handling support and logistics for the team. If you need anything for the mission, tell me.”

Her words made sense. From here on, they had to function as a single unit. Rank and seniority didn’t matter—only duty.

“Yes,” Shirone said, bowing his head. “I appreciate it.”

* * *

Kentra City.

Famous for its pastoral lands, Kentra produced meat so high in quality it was designated royal tribute. The city’s streets teemed with restaurants, and the air was thick with the scent of grilled meat. Cowboys in wide-brimmed hats strolled past shops selling leather goods and whips. The frontier spirit thrived.

But Shiina hadn’t come for the food or the souvenirs.

Kentra was the midpoint between Creas and Baska—the perfect neutral ground.

She checked the restaurant sign and stepped inside. The atmosphere was rustic, more subdued than Creas. Despite the hour, the place was nearly empty, even though reservations had been made.

She drained the glass of water the waiter poured and waited.

Twenty minutes until the meeting. And still, she wasn’t sure he’d come.

No—any sane person wouldn’t.

After their last encounter, she hadn’t contacted him once. Then, out of nowhere, she’d sent a certified 5th-class congratulatory letter—followed by a rapid telegram summoning him to Kentra for lunch.

Who would receive a dawn telegram, rush to prepare, and travel all this way just to eat meat?

‘Because it’s worth the bottom line,’ she thought.

The door chimed. A man entered.

Parka Quan, instructor at the Kaizen Swordsmanship School.

His cold, assessing gaze swept the room—unchanged from the last time she’d seen him. But his clothes were different. He moved carefully, with a limp, and every noble in the room turned to stare.

Quan didn’t hide his injury. But the weight of their eyes—the pity, the judgment—made him feel like a broken man.

And when their gazes shifted to Shiina, a familiar fury rose in her chest. She wanted to cut them all down.

She stood and raised her hand.

Quan approached. True to his nature, he offered no smile—only a stiff nod. He didn’t care about the stares. Not anymore.

Not after Armin had sacrificed both his eyes for her.

Quan’s legs were damaged. But that didn’t matter to Shiina.

And Quan, for the first time, felt it—her sincerity. No pretense. No pity. Just truth.

Maybe that was why he’d come this far, despite everything.

“You came,” Shiina said. “Sorry for the abrupt request.”

“This was an urgent telegram,” Quan replied. “I assumed it was important. Please, sit.”

The food arrived, but neither touched it. They sat in silence, focused only on cutting meat, chewing, swallowing.

The tension was suffocating.

Finally, Quan—the man—spoke first.

“Congratulations. Certified 5th-class mage. You’re impressive for someone so young.”

Shiina couldn’t delay any longer. Time was short. If he refused, she had to leave immediately for Galliant.

“I sent the telegram because I have a request.”

The words hung in the air, jarring after a compliment. Quan paused, utensils in hand, and blinked.

Then he nodded. “Go on.”

Shiina explained the mission. She withheld dangerous details—information that could compromise the team—but the core was clear. The world’s notice would end tomorrow. The stakes were absolute.

“…So I need a hitman on the team,” she said. “And I believe you’re the right person.”

Quan remained still until the end. Then, with a clatter, he set down his utensils. He reached for his water and drank deeply, as if to steady himself.

Shiina waited.

It was no different than asking someone to die. The stigma would follow his name forever.

Persuasion was impossible.

The choice was his alone.

Quan stared at the floor, lost in thought. Then he looked up.

“Can I ask one question?”

“Anything. Any answer I can give.”

“Why me?”

Shiina’s gaze hardened.

“You can see my leg. It’s not whole. And for a swordsman, the legs are everything. Why choose me?”

“Because of your skill,” Shiina began—then stopped. She swallowed the praise.

She knew exactly how Quan had gotten here. No man would travel this far for a woman he didn’t care about—unless driven by something deeper.

Armin had sent him off with his heart. But she wouldn’t use that.

She wanted only the truth.

“Quan-san,” she said, voice steady. “It doesn’t matter when you die.”

Quan stared at her, expressionless.

“I’ve heard you have no parents, no siblings, no ties. If you die, there’s no one to demand answers. No one to complicate things. I thought… it would be clean.”

Quan looked at her—long and hard.

And then, unexpectedly, he laughed.

“Kuk… kuk. Whoop, whoop, whoop.”

He lowered his head, shoulders shaking. Shiina tensed, unsure.

“Even when I die,” Quan said, lifting his eyes, “I won’t leave a burden? If you truly believe that…”

He bit his lip.

“Then you’ve come to the right man.”

Now he understood.

This woman—she didn’t pity him.

And in that absence of pity, he found something deeper.

To a man weary of false kindness, of hollow respect, her honesty was a revelation.

He retracted his doubts.

Perhaps, after all, God hadn’t abandoned him.

This was it.

Could there be a more fitting place for a sword demon’s tomb?

Quan rose from his seat.

“Let’s go. It’s urgent. There’s nothing left to prepare. My affairs were settled ten years ago.”

“Thank you,” Shiina said. “But at least leave a message—”

“No need.” Quan cut her off. “This is my decision, mine alone. If you wait until the moment of death to prepare, it’s already too late. On the path to battle, there’s no looking back.”

It was an extreme resolve. But for the role of a hitman—there was no one more suitable.

Regardless of motive, he was a suicide commando—willing to carry the fate of humanity into the fire.

Shiina bowed, deeply, sincerely.

“Thank you.”

Quan, in turn, bowed—like a swordsman swearing an oath.

“I am.”
