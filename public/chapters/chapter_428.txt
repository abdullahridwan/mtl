Chapter 428  
The Rapidly Changing Situation (2)  
~*~

Shiina staggered into the inn, still reeling from the ceremony that officially promoted her to a fifth-grade wizard. Though she’d received countless bouquets of congratulatory flowers, the only ones she kept were the ones Shirone had given her.

She kicked off her shoes and glanced down at the floor, where dozens of congratulatory letters from various organizations were scattered. Shiina sat at her desk, flipping her hair back as she began sorting through the pile of correspondence. The letters poured in from everywhere: the Tormia Defense Magic Research Institute, the Royal Magic Museum, the Climate Association, the Wizard Pension Corporation—there was no end to them.

“Well, looks like I made it,” she murmured. She shrugged off her coat and stood up. A hangover wasn’t something she worried about, but she was drenched in sweat and desperately wanted to wash up first. She tossed the coat onto the desk, her eyes catching a distinctive institutional seal among the stack of letters.

“Kaizen Swordsmanship Academy?”

Shiina frowned. Born into the Olifer family, she had spent her entire life as a wizard and had no particular connection to swordsmanship. *Is this from the person I met at the teachers’ meeting?* She broke the seal and scanned the name.

*Parka Quan.*

“Ah… Mr. Quan.”

She remembered him clearly. He was the man she’d encountered last year when she’d gone out to stand in line, pressured by her family. It hadn’t been a terrible encounter. Though his tone and demeanor were cold, he had never been rude.

*How did he even find out?* she wondered. He must have heard through the teachers’ association. Still, swordsmanship and magic were entirely different fields; unless she’d been paying close attention, she wouldn’t have known he was keeping track. *I should reply.*

Shiina sank back into her chair, wearing only her underwear. She opened her desk drawer, pulled out her stationery, dipped her fountain pen into the inkwell, and hovered it over the paper. *Tap.* The nib touched the page, leaving a single dot that refused to move.

*Brother Armin…*

If there was one person she truly wanted to congratulate, it was him. The man who had sacrificed his eyes for her when they were children. Without him, she never would have reached this point.

She closed the drawer, leaving the paper untouched. She felt bad for Mr. Quan, but her heart was too crowded with memories of Armin, who had already left. *How are you doing?* she thought. *It seems your latest work hasn’t even been released yet.* Since he’d departed while working at Creas, not a single letter had reached her. She’d worried something had happened, especially since he used to write at least once every three months. *But he’s so much smarter than me. Who needs to worry about whom?*

Even now that she was a certified fifth-class wizard, Armin still saw her as nothing more than the crybaby little sister from their childhood. Shiina stared up at the ceiling and offered a sad, quiet smile.

“Did I do well, Brother?”

* * *

It was the worst nightmare of his life.

Countless Maras descended from a rift in the sky the size of a city. Beings that humans called demons, fiends, and gods tore through the earth, obliterating all living things. Everyone was lost.

Amidst the corpses strewn across the land like a harvest field, every person Shirone loved lay dead.

*No! No!*

Shirone sobbed over Amy’s lifeless body. His eyes, unable to close, stared up at the dozens of Maras hovering in the sky with a hollow, pitying gaze.

*Aaaaaaa!*

Ataraxia accumulated at a terrifying rate. As Shirone’s hair stood on end, a blinding flash of light shook the heavens. The Maras were buried in the radiance and vanished.

But the instant the world was swallowed by light, darkness rushed in from beyond the edges, quickly smothering the glow.

Shirone was left alone in the void. The word “solitude” didn’t even begin to describe the isolation of the last human on Earth.

He fell to his knees and wept, crying out into the darkness in a desperate attempt to summon back what was lost.

“S-ro-yes-yeo.”

A muffled voice pierced through his eardrums.

A thin, jagged flash split the darkness vertically, revealing a pair of enormous pupils.

Shirone’s eyes widened. Reflected in the center of those massive eyes was his own image, his face twisted into something demonic.

“...! ...!”

Shirone screamed, coughing up blood. It was the first time in his life he had felt pure, unadulterated hatred.

Yet the voice sounded muffled, as if he were speaking underwater.

The colossal eyes, previously indifferent and fixed in the dark, continued to speak in a thick, muddy tone.

*“You have the right and the duty to answer the questions left to you.”*

“...! ...!”

Tears of blood streamed from his eyes. A torrent of curses and vitriol shot toward the pupils.

Losing control even within his dream, he pounded the darkness with both fists. Resentment, fury, and longing weighed heavily on his trembling shoulders.

The giant eyes finally focused on Shirone. The pupils didn’t move, but they had to be watching.

As if he’d suddenly gained hearing, Shirone could finally hear his own voice echoing in the dream.

“What? What question am I supposed to answer?”

“...”

The pupil seemed to reply.

“Huh!”

Startled, Shirone jerked his upper body upright. His vision cleared, landing on the drafting board on his desk, covered with sticky notes.

*My room. This is my room...*

Shirone collapsed back onto the desk. A cold sweat broke out across his forehead, and his body felt utterly drained, as if he’d run a marathon.

“*What the hell was that? Why did I have such a bizarre dream...*” he muttered.

He felt the crumpled paper clinging to his cheek and pushed himself up. Among the densely scrawled notes, one phrase caught his eye: *destruction of heaven*.

He’d fallen asleep after studying magic all night. It felt less like scholarly research and more like he was drafting a spell for genocide. He wondered if the stress of having no solution was finally manifesting as a nightmare.

It was already 5:00 a.m.

Shirone neatly organized his notes. Ten thick notebooks were already filled with concepts related to heaven-destroying magic. His understanding of God Particles had deepened, yet the probability of meeting Gaold’s proposed criteria remained stubbornly at zero percent.

*No, there has to be a way. Maybe...*

If completed, it would be the most destructive magic in existence. Still, he couldn’t give up. A faint, persistent echo in the back of his mind kept whispering that he was close to the right answer.

After washing his face with warm water, Shirone left his quarters, a notebook and pen secured in his crossbody bag. The graduation term that had begun in winter was already sliding toward summer. The nights had grown shorter, and dawn had just broken. The air felt crisp and refreshing, like carbonated water, and the forest rang with the clear chirping of birds.

*Can I really reach heaven?* he wondered.

When he’d first met Gaold, it felt like a year had passed. But now, buried under the grueling schedule of his final year, every promise he’d made to him felt like it was unraveling like a dream.

“Huh? Shirone?”

Shirone turned his head. Maya was sitting on a garden bench, blinking sleepily. In her hands, she held a mug of steaming coffee.

“Maya, what are you doing out here at this hour?”

“Oh? I always get up now. I have to play the weather music. You?”

Shirone had grown accustomed to waking up to her music every morning, but he’d never considered the trouble it might cause her.

“Oh, I see. I just woke up early and have some free time, so I’m going for a walk.”

“I see.”

Though their relationship had grown closer since they’d secured their positions, an awkward tension still lingered. They weren’t meeting by choice; circumstances had simply forced them together.

Maya lowered her gaze, using her thumb to nudge the mug slightly forward. Gathering her courage, she gestured to the empty space on the bench beside her. “Do you want to sit down?”

Shirone didn’t refuse. He’d only stepped out to clear his head, but keeping Maya company wasn’t a bad idea.

Where a wizard typically drew strict boundaries, Maya stepped right past them, gently soothing the pains of others. Initially, as a magician, Shirone had felt embarrassed by her approach, unwilling to offload his own burdens onto someone else. But he had to admit it honestly now. Her kindness was an oasis that quietly nourished his heart, which had been growing desolate under the crushing weight of his graduation schedule.

“Would you like a drink? It’s still warm.”

Maya handed him the mug, now mixed with sugar and milk. Shirone, stressed and exhausted, had no reason to refuse something sweet. Besides, it was Maya, so he accepted it without hesitation.

“Yeah, thank you.”

As Shirone took a sip, Maya’s cheeks flushed, and she offered a shy smile.

“Is something wrong? You look pretty down.”

Shirone sighed and leaned back against the bench. “I’ve been stuck on something lately and can’t seem to crack it. I haven’t slept properly in days. On top of that, I had a terrible nightmare this morning. That’s probably why I feel so off.”

Even through Maya’s eyes, Shirone looked painfully thin. She knew he was thriving during his senior year, but reaching the fourth grade clearly hadn’t been easy.

“Don’t push yourself too hard,” Maya said softly. “It’s like when I sing. If you try too hard to control it, it gets strained and falls apart.”

Shirone knew that feeling. He’d learned that if you didn’t clear your mind, inspiration wouldn’t flow. But simply relaxing wasn’t enough. True breakthroughs only came when pressure built to the breaking point, like a dam finally giving way, turning the mind into a torrent that carried genius ideas.

“Hmm, I don’t know,” Shirone muttered. “Whether it’s easy or hard, it has to make logical sense. If I keep forcing it, I might actually lose my mind.”

He tilted his head. Diligent birds darted across the morning sky, searching for breakfast.

Maya’s expression softened into pity. How much stress must he be under to have dark circles so pronounced?

“Shirone...”

She reached out and covered his hand with hers. Even as she did, Shirone’s gaze remained fixed on the sky. She couldn’t tell if he was too lost in thought to notice, or if he was simply tolerating it. Either way, the contact made her heart pound.

“Shirone, can you tell me what’s going on? No matter how brilliant you are, there are times when you miss the smallest details. Sometimes, asking for help is the only way to find a solution.”

Shirone blinked slowly.

*Seek help? That’s actually a good approach. But this is a state secret... so who could I possibly tell...?*

His eyelids snapped open. His usual stoic, almost lifeless expression shifted into something sharp and focused.

Maya didn’t notice the sudden shift in his demeanor. She just wanted to comfort him. *Not with words. With myself.* She had become his woman, after all. She felt her body lean toward him. *We can still be like this,* she thought. *If he didn’t want it, he would’ve pulled away the moment I touched his hand.* Maybe Shirone was waiting for her to make the next move?

Emboldened by her own thoughts, Maya gently parted her lips and leaned in toward his.

“Shirone, maybe I…”

“God Particles.”

“Huh?”

The words tumbled out of Shirone’s mouth before he even realized he’d spoken them.

*Get help. There are limits to being alone.*

The faint, flickering light in his mind suddenly flared, amplifying at a tremendous speed. Goosebumps rose across his skin. Finally, with a sensation like ice water pouring through his skull, his head cleared completely.

“Maya!”

“M-Maya! I’m sorry! I just…”

Maya scrambled back, nearly crouching in place. It took a moment for her to realize what she’d almost done. It would have been the perfect setup to be mistaken for a creep—or worse.

Shirone set the mug down with a sharp *clink* and stared at her.

“Hey, Shirone, so you know, why did I do that…”

Before she could finish, he pulled her into a tight hug.

Maya’s eyes widened. As her mind went pleasantly hazy, a wave of joy washed over her.

“Thank you, thank you so much! I finally figured it out!”

Shirone, still gripping her shoulders, gave her a vigorous shake before springing to his feet. He snatched his crossbody bag, looking like a man possessed, and bolted.

“Sorry! I have work to do! See you later!”

He ran with everything he had, stumbling once but quickly regaining his balance as he disappeared around the far side of the park.

Maya heard her own heart hammering against her ribs. Today hadn’t been a lucky day—it had been a windfall.

She stood up, a bright, happy smile spreading across her face.

“Shirone! Work hard! Go for it!”

Instead of turning back, Shirone simply raised a fist in the air.
