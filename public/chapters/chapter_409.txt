Chapter 409  
The Meaning of Survival (1)  
~*~

For the senior class, holidays were precious beyond compare.

Even so, Shirone had to wake before dawn, rubbing sleep from his eyes.

After the fourth week’s evaluation, today was the day of the comprehensive evaluation, which was held six times a year. It was the closest opportunity the students had to experience the regular events of the graduation exam firsthand, so it was an evaluation where no one could afford to hold back.

The winning side gained 20 points, and the losing side lost 20 points.

With a total swing of 40 points at stake, even students who had spent the last few weeks locked in quiet psychological warfare would be forced to give it everything they had. In terms of interpersonal combat, it was the equivalent of winning thirteen matches in a row. In terms of strengthening one’s main specialty, it was equal to passing four master-difficulty categories.

The rankings could change drastically today.

No—there was no way they would not change.

Today’s comprehensive evaluation was survival.

Because the rules inevitably produced losers, there was a real chance for some students to make a dramatic leap in the rankings. And for Shirone in particular, this was an event he could not afford to miss. Survival tested the same abilities required for the graduation exam: cognition, judgment, and mental endurance.

The students would enter an enormous Virtual Zone as a group, and they would be evaluated on how long they could endure under extreme conditions.

Judgment would be important, but mental stamina was essential. If Shirone failed to earn points here, he would be forced into a disadvantage for the rest of the schedule.

After meeting Nade and Iruki, Shirone left the dormitory building and headed for the training ground that housed the large Virtual Zone.

Many students had already gathered.

They were still competitors, but the initial awkwardness between them had faded. By now, everyone had at least some grasp of one another. Shirone still found it difficult to get along with Screamer, Pony, and Luman, but he had become fairly comfortable with Maya and Eider. His relationship with Dorothy, Suabi, and Fishaw remained neutral, while the students of Class One still did not so much as glance at Class Three.

Once the senior class had assembled, Collie lined the students up by class and looked over each of them in turn. Survival had a reputation for being the most dangerous and grueling of all the evaluations, and even his expression carried a solemn weight.

“Today’s comprehensive evaluation category is survival. Before we begin, I’ll explain the rules. First, you will divide into teams. Based on the line drawn over there, the right side will be the red team, and the left side will be the blue team.”

A line had been drawn across the wide open space leading into the Virtual Zone.

“Once the teams are decided, everyone will enter the Virtual Zone. The survival program consists of stages one through seven and lasts a total of fifty minutes. The moment the final survivor is determined, the result is decided, regardless of how much time remains. In other words, the team with the final survivor takes the overall victory, and the losing team loses 20 points.”

Boyle raised his hand.

“What are the criteria for choosing teams?”

“You may join whichever team you want.”

Pandora asked hesitantly, “Um… doesn’t it have to be fifteen against fifteen?”

“It doesn’t matter. If all thirty of you choose the same team, that is also allowed.”

A chill settled over Class Three. Some students even glanced left and right, as if to confirm they had heard correctly.

It was impossible to have a competition if all thirty students formed one team. What kind of rule was that?

Of course, no one was going to suggest that all thirty of them join one side, live together, and die together. This was the senior class, where every student was desperate to rise above the others.

But they still had to consider the meaning behind the rule.

Looking back at how the senior class had been managed so far, the system was not simply about earning points and climbing the rankings. It was structured so that advantages and disadvantages changed depending on the position one took within the competition—and on how accurately one understood the abilities of the other students.

Iruki said, “A mage’s position in a real party is absolute. It seems the comprehensive evaluation is no exception.”

If the mage faltered, the party collapsed. That was common knowledge even among third-rate travelers.

Simply being able to unleash powerful magic or maintain an excellent Spirit Zone did not mean someone could pass the graduation exam. The ability to judge one’s place among competitors and establish the best position for oneself was also an invisible part of the evaluation.

“We will now decide the teams,” Collie said. “You have ten minutes. Anyone who fails to arrive on time will be considered to have forfeited. Choose the team you want.”

At Collie’s words, all thirty students flinched as if startled.

Someone must have moved first, but in the end, everyone reacted at nearly the same time, making it impossible to tell who had begun it.

They had to think carefully.

As long as even one member of a team survived to the end, everyone on that team would receive the 20-point victory bonus. That meant students who lacked confidence in survival needed to determine who was most likely to remain until the end and join that person’s team.

In a sense, that too was a real survival ability.

For that reason, the students confident in their mental endurance should have been the first to move. Some were already considering taking the lead.

But even then, they hesitated over whether to choose red or blue, though the colors themselves had no meaning.

What had the win rates been for the blue team and the red team so far?

It was nothing more than a jinx, but jinxes were hard to ignore.

“I’ll choose first, then.”

Shirone was the first to move.

Since he intended to become the final survivor himself, he had no need to worry about anyone else’s choice.

When Shirone walked to the red team’s side, the crowd began to stir. Naturally, Nade and Iruki were the second to move.

Shirone and his group were on the red team.

Once that crucial piece of information was established, the remaining twenty-seven students began thinking furiously.

“Hmm. Then I suppose I’ll go blue.”

At the sound of Fermi’s voice, everyone turned toward him. He walked forward with confidence, and his companions followed behind him.

After reaching the center of the blue team’s side, Fermi glanced back at Shirone and smiled mischievously.

Shirone did not smile back. He could not tell what thoughts were hiding behind those narrow eyes.

At last, the moment the rest of the students had been waiting for arrived.

On one side were the formidable newcomers of Class Three. On the other were Fermi and his group, who had never once lost first place in the final evaluation.

Which side was more likely to win?

From this point on, it was a matter of choice.
