Opportunity for Revenge (4)

Heaven’s Fifth Thousand, Matei.

The colossal fortress stood as a monument to ancient power, birthed not from stone or steel, but from the very essence of legend. Here, the giants—born of sacred rites performed in Jotunheim—had returned after realizing the Law. As "those who keep the Law," their sacred duty was to preserve the peace of Heaven and stand against any force that threatened it.

Kariel, flanked by Uriel, gazed up at the towering citadel. At two meters thirty, he was tall by mortal standards—but dwarfed beside the fifteen-meter giants who now knelt in reverence. Their massive forms bent low, yet even at full bow, their heads barely reached Kariel’s waist.

Kariel snorted and strode past the entrance without a word.

Even so, his chest burned with frustration.

Ymir, the king of the giants, had claimed he could not intervene—having destroyed the "Part" and now lying in hibernation. But at the very least, the corps commander should have come out barefoot, head bowed in submission.

“These are real…?”

The state of Heaven was chaos.

Ever since Shirone, the Light of District 73, shattered the Anecdote, the subjects had begun to question Ankera. Everything had changed after that.

Kariel burned with the urge to descend into Purgatory and crush the rebels where they stood. But he couldn’t even protect himself without Uriel at his side.

*Let’s see. I’ll put an end to it sooner or later.*

If Jebul was a perfect system, Matei’s castle was its rival in sheer grandeur. No artificial alloys—no fusion of Mecha, Nord, or Kergo. Only raw, natural materials, stacked by the hands of giants. Even the colossal pillars, impossible to move without titanic strength, spoke of their philosophy: power unassisted, unaltered.

After crossing the great hall and ascending the central stairway—walls rising like cliffs—Kariel and Uriel reached a line of giant guards, their heads brushing the vaulted ceiling.

Each one radiated power enough to shatter worlds.

Yet they did not move. Not until Ymir gave the order.

“Why does the great archangel deign to visit such a humble place?”

A figure approached—smaller than the others, yet still three meters tall. He dragged a black cloak behind him, each step vibrating the floor like a war drum.

Girshin, the giant corps commander, a master of the Seventh Level of Anecdotal Art.

Where Ymir was like unshaped bedrock, Girshin was a blade forged a thousand times—refined, lethal, unyielding.

“Meet the corps commander, Guru-sin,” Uriel said, stepping forward.

Girshin dropped to one knee before Kariel, bowing his head deeply. Even a warrior capable of splitting mountains could not defy the hierarchy ordained by Law.

Kariel sneered.

Among the eight archangels, he had no rival in raw force. Yet he could see the truth behind the gesture—the grudging respect, the quiet defiance beneath the surface.

“Has your body grown sluggish at Level 7? I’ve walked a hundred meters, and you’ve only just arrived.”

Girshin lifted one corner of his mouth in a cold smile.

“A giant does not possess an angel’s infinite patience. Had you sent word, I would have met you.”

His tone was different from Ymir’s—sharper, more calculating.

To a being with centuries of memory, dwelling on the past was a waste.

“The reason I came myself,” Kariel said, “is to give you orders.”

“Orders?”

“Lead the giant army. Wipe out the rebels—now.”

Girshin’s brow furrowed.

He had heard the rumors—discord among the angels in Jebul. But to ignore Ra’s decree forbidding angelic intervention was itself an act of treason.

“Haona, Ra…” Kariel spat the name like poison. “That’s precisely why I’m here. Ra’s command applies only to angels. The rebels are purging giants born from the Anecdote the moment they’re found. Don’t you have enough reason to act?”

Girshin remained silent.

He was caught—between duty and loyalty.

But he had no intention of moving the army.

They followed only Ymir.

And Anchera obeyed only because Ymir did.

“I’m sorry,” Girshin said, voice steady. “I cannot follow your will.”

Kariel’s face twisted in rage.

Behind him, the guards growled, saliva dripping like thunder in their throats. But Girshin did not flinch.

“You *dare* refuse? You understand what you’re saying?”

Kariel snapped open the *Daebeopjeon*—a sacred artifact unique to the Archangel of Birth.

It was a direct threat. A challenge to life itself.

“Don’t you know, Kariel?” Girshin said, unshaken. “Without King Ymir’s command, the giants—”

“AAAAAAH! Insignificant wretch!”

Kariel slammed the *Daebeopjeon* down—striking Girshin’s head with full force.

There was no dignity in his face now—only madness, fury, the flailing of a man losing control.

*Bang! Bang! Bang! Bang!*

Even weakened, an archangel’s strike was devastating. Blood trickled from Girshin’s forehead as he stood motionless, jaw clenched.

Uriel, arms crossed, watched in silence.

This wasn’t the Kariel he knew.

The Archangel of Birth—once serene, purposeful—was now lashing out like a cornered beast.

*This isn’t like you, Kariel.*

*Are you truly this consumed by hatred for humans? No… you should know better by now. You look… like him.*

Uriel cut off the thought.

From the ranks of guards, killing intent erupted.

Though their status was lower, their pride was no less than an angel’s. To see their commander humiliated before them was unbearable.

*Thud.*

One guard stepped forward, ready to charge.

Uriel’s starlight flared—expanding into a radiant Halo.

Girshin snapped his head back, eyes wide with warning.

“Ugh…”

The guard froze. Then, as if realizing the folly of his action, he retreated.

With each strike of the *Daebeopjeon*, cracks spiderwebbed across the floor. The ground buckled. Yet Girshin did not raise a hand.

*Now is not the time. Even if we attack as one, we cannot defeat Uriel—the Angel of Destruction.*

An incarnation of ruin, said to rival even Ikael in combat power.

*I must endure. Until our king, Ymir, awakens.*

Kariel staggered back, hands trembling, breath ragged.

His vision blurred. His thoughts felt molten, dissolving.

“Haha…”

Girshin said nothing, only stared at the blood pooling at his feet.

To Kariel, who revered creation, this silence—this defiance—was an abomination.

“Let’s go!”

Kariel turned sharply and stormed away.

Only then did Uriel allow emotion to show. He glanced back at the giants—his gaze lingering on Girshin.

The presence of the archangels, bound by Law, made the giants’ legs tremble.

“Be careful, Girshin,” Uriel said softly. “You are not Ymir.”

Then, light unfurled from his back—wings of pure radiance. He rose, a streak of brilliance, and vanished through the entrance like a comet.

* * *

Gaold’s hair had turned white—completely. His face was twisted, demonic, as if carved from rage and pain.

A storm of compressed air erupted from his body, tearing through all forty Grim Reapers in an instant.

Then—

The ice trembled.

The figures of the Grim Reapers dissolved into smoke, writhing like flames.

*Ooh oh oh oh!*

Hoods fell away, revealing hollow skulls beneath.

Their shrieks pierced the air—so maddening that the guide clutched his head and collapsed, blood streaming from his ears.

“Aaaaaagh!”

He hadn’t wanted to come here in the first place.

Black Elixir, luxury, power—none of it mattered now. It had all been illusion.

“I… please… AAAAAAN!”

At the guide’s scream, Kang Nan sprang forward.

Her expression was tight—haunted. The past surged back—ten years ago, the accident, the failure.

*That fool!*

The Grim Reaper, wreathed in vivid purple aura, swung its scythe in a wide arc.

Each strike moved beyond Mach speed.

Though they were made of the same "material," their mechanics defied the physical world entirely.

Kang Nan dodged with the agility of a bouncing ball, darting in and kicking the dark horse’s leg.

The beast’s thick limb snapped. Black smoke erupted like fire.

*Hey hey hey hey!*

The horse’s screams tore through her ears, but Kang Nan gritted her teeth and endured.

She scanned the battlefield—searching for Gaold.

But the rampaging dark horses had turned the world into a black curtain of chaos.

“I really can’t live!”

As Kang Nan crouched, the fabric of her stocking split at the thigh—waterdrop-shaped holes torn by the scythe’s edge.

The moment the blade plunged vertically, she launched twenty meters into the air—escaping gravity itself.

There—buried among the Grim Reapers—was Gaold.

Each time a Grim Reaper dissolved into smoke, a flash of iron-colored light lashed out, carving through the space where Gaold stood.

Yet he remained untouched.

The magic shielding him was nothing more than a basic air barrier—something even a novice could conjure.

But none of the attacks pierced it.

“Big!”

As Gaold’s face contorted—twisted like a goblin’s—Kang Nan’s heart sank.

“No!”

Atmospheric pressure surged around Gaold—ten thousand times normal.

*Pain 100,000 times – Air Pressing.*

*Koo goo goo goo goo!*

The ice shook as if struck by an earthquake. The Grim Reapers’ smoky forms flattened, crushed like rubber.

“Whoa!”

Gaold puffed his cheeks and took a deep breath.

But already, the crushed figures were crawling back from the ground—returning to their original forms.

Niflheim’s curse: instant regeneration, so long as they had the slightest space to escape.

Kang Nan charged into the fray, shouting as she ran.

“Crazy! I really want to die…!”

Gaold, eyes wide, barely parted his lips.

Seeing that face, Kang Nan couldn’t scold him.

It made no sense to expect laughter.

“I told you not to come,” Gaold said, voice faint. “I couldn’t finish it because of you.”

“Don’t talk nonsense. Don’t you know everything ends if you die here?”

“Don’t worry. It’s different now. It’s not like ten years ago.”

Kang Nan screamed, unable to bear it.

“It was still dangerous!”

*Autoreciprocal Mutation.*

The disease Gaold had contracted on the Day of Judgment of the Twenty.

Organic limitations were bound by environment. Humans burned because they were born without fire. Salamanders did not, because they were born within it.

But Gaold had severed that link—through unimaginable penance.

With no environment, there were no limits to mutation at the stem cell level.

His world—where his nervous system was a thousand times more sensitive than any human’s—was pure hell.

Even breathing felt like inhaling glass dust. The slightest breeze threatened to tear his skin.

But in return, he had gained self-denial and mental strength beyond imitation.

“You know it becomes lethal beyond Pain 10,000 times! Even now, my hair is falling out, my memories slipping—but if I go further, I *will* die!”

“I will not die.”

Gaold turned coldly and walked away.

“As for pain—I will never die from it.”

Kang Nan clenched her fists, biting her lip.

But in the end, she lowered her head—resigned.

She knew the truth.

You don’t die from pain.

You suffer because you *couldn’t* die.

She didn’t know why Gaold had been cursed like this.

But one thing was certain:

No one else could annihilate forty of the strongest demons in an instant.

Gaold’s eyes slowly returned to normal.

A smile crept back onto his face. He wiggled his fingers, taunting the Grim Reapers.

“Come on, you fallen skeleton gourds.”

*Ooh oh oh oh!*

They likely didn’t understand human speech—but as if triggered, all forty expanded their auras.

The screams of countless ghosts shook the snowy wasteland. A whirlpool of purple light swirled above.

Then—thousands of skulls formed in the vortex, hurtling toward Gaold like bombs.

The sun vanished. Darkness swallowed the sky.

Skull after skull rained down upon him.

“Haha. Kkkkkkkkk!”

Gaold bared his teeth, stance lowering.

His pupils vanished—only blackness remained, his mouth twisted into a grotesque grin.

*Pain 100,000 times – Air Pressing.*

*Woooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooo!*

Everything collapsed. The darkness roared—and was crushed beneath it.
