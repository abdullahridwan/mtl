Chapter 438  
Final Decision (8)  
~*~

It was a gloomy day.

Shirone rolled up his spare clothes and packed dry rations and a first aid kit—just in case. With his right hand, he hoisted the full military backpack, then stored it inside Kubrick’s second slot.

“Loaded weight: 38.7 kg,” he noted quietly. It was optimal—efficiently packed, yet still leaving room for essentials.

He glanced around the dorm room one last time, checking for anything left behind. Then he checked the time.

Past midnight.

From the drawer of his desk, he took out Maya’s letter—folded neatly, preserved like a relic—and read it again.

It had been the day of the Supernatural Psychic Research Society’s presentation when Maya first fell for Shirone.

He remembered standing before her at the senior year welcome party. When he’d jokingly—half-seriously—asked which girl he’d wanted to kiss, she’d taken it as a sign. As if destiny had whispered in her ear that he might choose her.

The letter spanned four pages, each filled with raw, unembellished emotion. No poetic flourishes, no pretense—just her heart, laid bare.

*If you accept my feelings,* she had written, *please come to the park two nights from now, at midnight.*

*Even if you don’t come, I won’t resent you. I won’t hate you.*

*I just want you to be honest this time. I love you.*

Shirone refolded the letter along the original creases, slipped it back into its envelope, and tucked it carefully into his inner pocket. He pressed his palm gently against it, as if safeguarding something sacred.

“Maya…”

He left the dorm earlier than necessary. Would it be cruel to make her wait?

Ten minutes ago, he’d arrived at the park.

Beneath the dim glow of a single streetlamp, he waited.

Deng. Deng. Deng.

The midnight bell tolled.

Almost immediately, the sky darkened. Clouds thickened, wind howled, and rain began to fall. Thunder rumbled in the distance. Cold drops pelted his skin, soaking through his hair and clothes, weighing them down.

Rain poured in relentless sheets, drumming against the pavement like a thousand impatient fingers.

Shirone stood motionless, eyes fixed on the path Maya would take.

Deng.

The bell marking one o’clock echoed through the night.

She hadn’t come.

Then—splash.

The sound of footsteps in puddles. Someone approaching from behind.

Only when the rain stopped falling on his head did Shirone finally turn.

Amy stood there, holding an umbrella.

They stared at each other in silence.

Amy waited for him to speak first. Shirone had nothing to say.

Her suppressed emotions erupted—resentment, anger, confusion, betrayal, and beneath it all, a desperate ache.

She clenched her teeth, voice trembling.

“Why are you here?”

Shirone didn’t answer.

“Say something! Why did you come? Was it for Maya…?”

“It has nothing to do with you.”

Amy’s heart sank.

She *knew* Maya was going to confess.

And she had agreed to it—not out of confidence, but because she thought she’d seen the truth in Shirone’s eyes. She’d believed, foolishly, that he cared for *her*.

But now, seeing him wait in the rain for another woman…

She’d paced her room, restless, suffocating, until she couldn’t bear it anymore. She’d run here, desperate to stop something she couldn’t name.

And now he looked at her with cold, distant eyes.

“No… It can’t be.” Her voice cracked.

“What’s going on? You’ve been acting so strange lately. Tell me! Say something! If you don’t, I’ll never speak to you again.”

“Yes,” Shirone said flatly. “I don’t care.”

Amy couldn’t believe it.

Why was he treating her like this?

This wasn’t the Shirone she knew. She was afraid.

For the first time, she felt utterly exposed—naked before him.

“Why? Why won’t you say anything? Is it because I’m not like Maya? Fool… I love you too… I love you too…”

Shirone pulled her into a tight embrace.

It was the only way to hide the pain twisting his face.

Hot tears mixed with cold rain, streaming down his cheeks.

“Amy… thank you. Thank you so much.”

Amy stiffened, then broke.

She grabbed his collar, trying to push him away, but her strength failed. Her arms slipped uselessly.

“No… don’t hug me! Say it! Tell me why! You bastard!”

But her hands were weak.

Time pressed her forward, and ahead loomed a cliff. For the first time, she wished she could turn back.

Shirone smiled sadly.

“Goodbye, Amy…”

The umbrella slipped from Amy’s hand and clattered to the ground. The roar of the rain swallowed everything.

Even after Shirone stepped away, Amy stood frozen, dazed.

Tears blurred her vision. She saw nothing.

“Ugh… ugh…”

When the sob finally tore from her throat, the sky seemed to weep with her.

* * *

Thump! Thump! Thump!

Collie, the senior class homeroom teacher, woke to frantic knocking at the door of his annex.

He quickly threw on his robe, rubbed his eyes, and opened the door.

Maya stood there, drenched.

“Maya? What are you doing here at this hour?”

“Teacher…”

She collapsed into his arms, sobbing uncontrollably.

“Aaaaaang!”

Collie was stunned, but quickly took in her state. First, calm her down.

He led her inside, stoked the fireplace, and handed her a towel.

After boiling cocoa, he set it before her, but Maya just stared into the flames, entranced.

*It must be about him,* Collie thought.

He’d known today was the day Maya had mentioned—the night she’d planned to confess.

He hadn’t shown it, but he cared deeply for her. He was the one who’d personally recommended her to the magic school, who’d taken her under his wing.

*A sensitive child. Too concerned with others to tend to her own heart.*

He remembered the first time he saw her—fourteen years old, singing at an outdoor concert by the famous singer Canary.

Outside the barricade, commoners gathered to listen. The event planners, seeking to highlight Canary’s brilliance, had arranged for a commoner girl to open the performance.

That girl was Maya.

But the plan backfired.

Her voice—pure, haunting, beautiful—gave even the nobles goosebumps.

Enraged by the humiliation, they booed and jeered when she finished.

Worse—some nobles demanded the tribal chief bring Maya to them, offering obscene sums for her.

The chief fought back, was beaten to death, and cast out.

That day, Maya gave up on her dream of becoming a singer.

Collie had stepped in.

She had a gentle spirit, but undeniable talent. Within two years, she’d broken into the Spirit Zone.

He’d never wanted her to become some legendary wizard.

Wouldn’t it be enough, he’d thought, if she could become a mage, lift her tribe, and sing the songs she truly loved?

“Did Shirone-kun not come?” Collie asked gently.

Maya lifted the corner of her mouth in a fragile smile.

“No. He didn’t come. Like an idiot, I told him not to wait if he didn’t want to. But I came anyway. I waited over an hour.”

She covered her face with both hands.

“It hurts… because Shirone is so kind. Because I want to be the one he chooses.”

“Shouldn’t you have given up? Could it be that you truly loved him?”

Maya shook her head.

“I knew from the start I couldn’t win his heart. But I had to confess. I love him too much to stay silent. I walked in knowing it would be hell.”

She looked at Collie, eyes wide with fear.

“But what do I do now? It hurts so much… I feel sick. I think I’ll die. Can I forget Shirone? Can I go back to who I was before I met him?”

Collie spoke plainly.

“Maya, you can’t erase the past. These memories will stay with you for life.”

“I don’t think I can bear it… I’m so broken…”

Maya finally broke down, sobbing.

Collie pulled her into his arms, patting her back gently.

“You don’t need to be afraid. Scars don’t disappear—but as life goes on, new ones form. Eventually, you won’t even remember which ones came from Shirone. And then, you’ll be okay.”

Maya pressed her face into his chest.

“This isn’t living. Without hope… how can I go on?”

“Maya, we don’t live *for* hope. We live *by creating* hope. You won’t forget him, but you *will* be alright. You can build another hope.”

“I hate that! I want *him* to come to me. I want him to love me with poetry.”

Being cut by a knife doesn’t hurt at first. The pain comes when you realize you’ve been wounded.

That was the emotion Maya felt now—not pain, but fear. Fear of the pain to come.

The more tender the heart, the deeper the wound.

“But Maya,” Collie said softly, “an artist is someone who speaks for humanity. Even when they stand above, they never look down on people. So remember today’s pain. Sing from the lowest place. One day, your song will heal others—and in time, it will heal you too.”

He stroked her back as her shoulders trembled, fragile as a storm-shattered bird.

* * *

3 a.m.

Shirone had never intended to sleep.

He returned to the inn, washed off the rain, and changed into dry clothes.

Sitting at his desk, he tried to quiet his thoughts—but the echo of weeping, real or imagined, seeped through the silence.

“Is everything settled now?”

He had resigned from the Supernatural Psychic Research Society. Nade had been furious, but in the end, she’d hugged him and whispered, “No matter what, come back someday.”

And today, he had said his final goodbyes—to Maya, to Amy.

He needed to sever every tie before ascending to Heaven.

No—*had* to.

If he carried regrets, he would hesitate at the decisive moment. And hesitation, in a fight where death was certain, meant failure.

In a short time, he had cut every bond he could.

Yet still, the faces of those he cherished lingered.

Lian. Tess. And… his parents, who had always wanted only his happiness.

He had been lucky—he’d told them “I love you” before leaving home.

It had been, truly, the greatest moment of his life.

He opened a drawer and took out a clean sheet of stationery. Words he couldn’t say aloud would have to be written.

Spinning the pen between his fingers, Shirone finally began to write.

*History.*

*“Black. Sobbing.”*

Tears fell as sadness overwhelmed him.

He had wanted to graduate from magic school. He had wanted to travel the world with his friends. He had wanted to feel again the electric thrill of holding Amy’s hand.

“Mom…”

His only parents were Vincent and Olina. That would never change.

He should have known before going to Heaven.

No—he *hadn’t* met Ikael from the beginning.

But now he knew.

She had never told anyone, but deep inside, she had already found the answer to the question that had haunted her for a lifetime.

*I’m sorry. I’m sorry.*

Shirone wiped his tears with his sleeve and continued writing the letter—his will.

Gratitude to those who had helped him. Stories of happiness born from love. Respect for his parents.

Everything he had carried for nineteen years was pouring out onto the page.

Emptying.

Preparing.

For what came next.
