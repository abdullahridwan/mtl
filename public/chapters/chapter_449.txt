Heaven, the sixth thousand jebul. The Great World War.

Among the angels was one whose face was as transparent and beautiful as light itself — a being born with a slender, two-meter-thirty-centimeter frame and a mind that grasped every principle of the world, the very pinnacle of intelligence. Among the countless angels, only eight held the rank of archangel, and of those eight, Kariel, the angel of birth, was renowned as the most beautiful of all.

But now his face looked drawn and sickly, his eyes sunken, his expression taut with strain. The benevolent, intelligent lines that had once defined his features had grown distorted of late — in truth, he was in a state that could only be called illness.

If an angel's power stemmed from clarity of concept, then Kariel's current clarity had fallen to a mere tenth of what it had once been. All of it was the price of the labor he had endured to bring a single human here.

"Kariel, are you truly all right?"

Uriel, the angel of destruction, watched Kariel's faded presence with concern. Standing three meters tall — the tallest of all the archangels — with a breastplate so thick it nearly brushed his chin, a golden halo spun above his head as though seething with anger.

"I can still hold on. I have to endure it. This is only the beginning."

By human standards, the fatigue Kariel felt was equivalent to forty years without sleep. Any ordinary creature struck with exhaustion on that scale would have had every cell in its body wither and dry up. And yet his eyes still burned with feeling.

Uriel followed Kariel's gaze. Beneath the galactic mirror that governed the workings of the world sat a woman, cross-legged — beautiful, by human standards.

Adrias Miro.

To the denizens of Heaven, could there be a more dangerous existence than this? She was humanity's last bastion, the one who, by raising an impenetrable barrier, kept even Ankera, the living incarnation of the Akashic Records, from conquering the universe.

Yet even she had finally been broken by Kariel's relentless persistence.

"So you finally did it," Uriel said.

Right before the rebels could be annihilated, Ankera had abruptly called off the war and forbidden any further investigation of humanity. Every angel had obeyed that decree — except Kariel, who refused to give up and quietly pressed on with his own scheme, one aimed, in the end, at humanity itself. What he had ultimately obtained through it was what now sat before them: the physical body of the labyrinth itself.

"How did you manage it?" Uriel asked. "Didn't I tell you the labyrinth's space-time was indestructible?"

A faint smile touched Kariel's exhausted face.

"When I struck the labyrinth's space-time with a cloth bomb, Optrus slipped through the rift it opened. Information about the labyrinth's time-space had been embedded in its electromagnetic patterns — I analyzed it."

"But the labyrinth's space-time healed itself instantly. Surely you couldn't simply break through her dimensional barrier and drag her out?"

"The dimensional barrier itself is, in fact, insubstantial. The real reason Heaven's armies cannot reach the human world is that its protocol is entirely different from ours. It's an independent world — one that doesn't even share quantum entanglement with us."

Uriel made no attempt to follow the explanation. Those born for destruction were under no obligation to understand what they destroyed.

"So?"

"I deciphered the protocol using the information Optrus retrieved. Then, through quantum superposition, I transposed the labyrinth into the Great World War."

"Hm. And?"

Kariel realized Uriel still hadn't grasped it.

"Simply put — imagine reaching into a barrel and pulling things out at random until you find the one you want. It's no exaggeration to say that barrel was the size of the entire universe."

Only then did Uriel understand why Kariel had grown so weak.

"So you searched the whole universe... and dragged her out of it."

"...I suppose that's right."

Kariel chose not to dwell on it.

"Hm," Uriel murmured, studying Miro's face as she sat lost in her own trance. It was the same youthful face she had worn the moment she alone tore open a dimensional wall against the armies of Heaven, right before the final war broke out.

"The human spirit, in a sense, is formidable," Uriel said.

Even now, her mind — the very thing that gave shape to the labyrinth's space-time — was being endlessly dragged into the dimension of cowardice. It was a mental state, a concept, that an angel born perfect in every way could never comprehend.

"Hmph. That's simply proof of an imperfect being," Kariel said. "Don't waste sentiment on insignificant humans."

Uriel did not answer. For him, it was enough merely to destroy what he could not understand. But would Kariel be able to do the same?

Birth, as a concept, presupposed that everything was understood. That Kariel — who governed the art of life itself — could not understand humanity was, perhaps, the greatest contradiction in the universe.

"Human beings are very strange creatures," Uriel said. "Even now, isn't a single human blocking all of Heaven's power?"

Kariel's expression crumpled.

Humans. Humans. He could not fathom why everyone, beginning with Ankera, applied a different standard to humans alone.

"It's nothing like that. I will destroy her."

Uriel simply turned away. He had no wish to antagonize the other angel — they might be twins born of the same day, but their concepts stood as opposites, and pressing the point rarely helped.

"All right — what now?" Uriel asked. "Even if you kill the labyrinth, its space-time won't simply vanish. Why go to the trouble of dragging her here and torturing her mind to the very brink of extinction?"

"Hmph, obviously it's to destroy the labyrinth's space-time," Kariel said. "I intend to pull her out of the dimension of cowardice. If she's killed after regaining her senses, the labyrinth's space-time will vanish along with her."

It was the right approach — no, the only one.

"But we can't understand human imperfection," Uriel said. "I doubt there's anyone among our subjects capable of understanding Miro's spirit — not since the Nephilim vanished after the erasure of the Guffin."

"Not in Heaven, no. But there is, in the kingdom of Earth."

Uriel tilted his head. It was true that in the kingdom of Earth, some still awakened spontaneously as Nephilim. But as long as the labyrinth's space-time stood as a barrier, bringing anyone out of that world should have been impossible.

"Are you going to search the whole universe again?" Uriel asked. "Do that, and you might vanish completely this time."

"That won't be necessary. I don't have the strength left for it anyway."

Kariel flew up to the pillar-shaped central system and accessed the Akashic Records. Uriel, who flew up after him, looked over his shoulder at the screen.

"I was searching through the Akashic Records," Kariel said, "and found something interesting."

As Kariel stepped aside, a human's name appeared on the screen. Reading the explanation beneath it, Uriel nodded.

"Certainly, interesting."

Fittingly, this was someone who understood the human spirit better than anyone. No — it would be more accurate to say he had become what he now was precisely because he understood the human mind so well.

"I will summon him to the Great World War."

When Kariel opened his hand, a celestial light flashed, and a code of law took shape upon his palm. The law code, bound in a cover of high-strength metal and holding within it the principles of the universe, began to turn its pages of woven light. As the central computing system roared to full operation, bluish electricity rippled across the floor like waves, gathering into the arched mechanism above.

Uriel watched the scene for a moment, then turned back to the screen of the central processing unit.

"A grave robber..." he murmured.

On the screen, blinking, was the name of someone who until now had existed only as a concept.

Morrigan Arius.

Rebel 1st Headquarters.

Shirone's party was met with enthusiastic cheers from the rebels. The tale of Shirone subduing hundreds of giants with the power of an archangel had already spread through the camp via drone broadcasts, becoming something of a new legend among the rebels.

Amid the roar of it all, Shirone's expression only grew more troubled.

It seemed strange that this base carried the designation "1st," but the sheer size of it — far larger than he had expected — put any doubts to rest. Dozens of buildings, presumably munitions warehouses, ringed the site, and a massive structure over thirty meters tall rose from the forest at its center.

That's incredible power, Shirone thought. If they mobilized all of it—

But Gaold's thoughts ran in a different direction.

There wouldn't even be seeds left, he thought grimly.

The headquarters' strength was, without question, formidable. And yet there was no mistaking the battalion commander's words — the rebels stood on the very brink of annihilation. This wasn't simply a matter of power imbalance. From the very beginning, the beings of Heaven had been something fundamentally different from humankind. A human bitten by thousands of ants might well die — but no human would ever let it come to that in the first place. The angels were the same way.

"Huh? Is that— Shirone! Hey, Shirone! It's me, it's me!"

A familiar face burst out from the middle of the crowd. It was Clove and Gadlock, who had guided Shirone and his friends to the Nord shelter a year ago in Heaven. The memory of how Clove had once brushed Amy off still lingered, but even so, it was good to see a familiar face at a time like this.

Still, Shirone couldn't bring himself to go over. The huge crowd, having overheard Clove's shout, looked ready to swarm him at any moment.

"Whoa! It's really him! The real Shirone is here!"

In moments, Clove and Gadlock were swallowed by the crowd and vanished from sight. It happened because so few people outside District 73 had ever seen Shirone's face in person.

Shirone widened the distance between himself and the crowd, his expression cold. If people pressed any closer, someone was bound to get hurt.

Watching Shirone walk away, Clove tore at her hair, lamenting.

"Oh, I never imagined that kid would turn into someone this important. If I'd known it would come to this, I'd have gotten close to him ahead of time."

Gadlock rapped his disciple on the head.

"Is that really what matters right now? We're talking about whether we get annihilated by the angels or not."

Clove replied glumly. "But Master said the same thing — that once Shirone arrived, we'd finally be treated better here too."

Gadlock had nothing to say to that.

Shirone had returned — and at exactly the right moment, too. The suspension of Heaven's assault was certainly a boon for the rebels, but it alone couldn't mend the rift between Mecca and Nord, a divide that had only deepened through their losing streak. Unable to reach any consensus, the command had finally split into 1st and 2nd Headquarters. No actual battle had been fought, yet the sense of defeat already ran deep. Just as everyone braced for the rebellion to collapse under its own weight, the light of District 73 had begun to shine on them once more.

Why now, of all times? Had he come back knowing all this? Gadlock wondered. The chain of events, linked by threads too fine to see, made it impossible to write off Shirone's return as mere coincidence.

"Hehe, it's so nice getting to walk together like this," Lena said, her arm looped through Shirone's, refusing to let go for even a moment as they made their way toward the command center.

Fleur, walking stiffly behind them, watched Shirone's back and clicked her tongue.

"What is she doing? Did you two come to Heaven to fall in love or something?"

Gaold didn't seem bothered. "Ah, leave them be. A little of that can only help with the show." A miracle named Shirone had arrived among rebels standing on the edge of annihilation — a little theater to go with it couldn't hurt.

Kanya, though, was mortified by her sister's shamelessness. Unable to stand it any longer, she went over and hissed into Lena's ear.

"What are you doing? Let go of him already. Don't you know what Shirone means to the rebels right now? What if people get the wrong idea?"

"What wrong idea? Shirone and I aren't even close," Lena said. "We're meeting again for the first time in a year — can't I even loop my arm through his?"

Lena turned to Shirone with a grin. "You should let my sister walk arm in arm with you too, then."

Shirone had no idea how to respond. His heart was already pounding as it was — how was he supposed to walk down the road with two women hanging off his arms?

Kanya's face flushed scarlet.

"W-what are you talking about? Why would I be arm in arm with Shirone?"

"Oh, right," Lena said airily. "My sister's too delicate for this sort of thing. We need to look close with Shirone so we can secure good positions at headquarters. Besides, my sister's Kuroi got wrecked — I need to get her a new one so she can fly again."

Kanya, struck speechless, could only clamp her mouth shut. Young as she was, Lena thought of her older sister more carefully than Kanya had given her credit for. No — perhaps clever was the better word for it, Kanya thought.

Shirone tried to reassure her.

"Kanya, don't worry too much about it. It was my fault your Kuroi got destroyed — I'll make sure to bring it up when I see the Commander."

Kanya managed a smile for Shirone, then turned back to Lena with narrowed eyes.

Ugh, that little fox..., she thought.

Lena, whether or not she understood her sister's true feelings, simply wrapped her arm tighter around Shirone's and smiled, perfectly content.
