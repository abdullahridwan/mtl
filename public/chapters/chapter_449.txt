In Heaven (1)  
~*~

The Sixth Thousand Jebul of Heaven. The Great World War.

An angel stood, his face luminous and translucent, as if carved from pure light. He was the apex of intellect—a being born with a slender, two-meter-thirty frame and an innate understanding of all cosmic principles. Among the countless angels, only eight were archangels. Of them, Kariel, the Angel of Birth, was said to possess the most exquisite beauty.

Yet now, his face was gaunt, his eyes sunken, his expression strained. The serene, intelligent features that once radiated calm wisdom had grown distorted, twisted by exhaustion. He looked ill—pale, trembling, barely holding himself together.

An angel’s power stemmed from the clarity of their concept. Kariel’s was now diminished to a mere 10% of its former brilliance.

All of this—his deterioration—was the price he had paid to bring one human into Heaven.

“Kariel,” said Uriel, the Angel of Destruction, his voice low with concern. “Are you really all right?”

At three meters tall, Uriel was the tallest of the archangels. A thick breastplate encased his torso, rising to meet the base of his jaw, where a golden halo spun violently, as if mirroring his inner agitation.

Kariel didn’t look at him. His hollow eyes remained fixed on the figure below.

“I can still hold on,” he whispered. “I have to. This is only the beginning.”

By human standards, the fatigue Kariel carried was equivalent to forty years without sleep. Any mortal creature subjected to such strain would have withered to dust, every cell collapsing. Yet Kariel stood—barely. And still, a flicker of fire burned in his gaze.

Uriel followed his line of sight.

Beneath the galactic mirror that governed the mechanics of the world, a woman sat cross-legged in deep meditation. By human standards, she was breathtakingly beautiful.

Adrias Miro.

The very idea of her existence was an affront to Heaven.

She was humanity’s final bulwark—the one who had erected an impenetrable barrier, preventing even Ankera, the living incarnation of the Akashic Records, from conquering the universe.

And yet, even she had fallen.

Defeated not by force, but by Kariel’s relentless will.

“You did it,” Uriel said quietly.

Just before the rebels were to be eradicated, Ankera had abruptly halted the war and forbidden all investigations into humanity. Every angel obeyed.

Except Kariel.

While the rest of Heaven turned away, he had continued his silent campaign—methodically, obsessively—until he succeeded.

What he now possessed was the physical vessel of the Labyrinth itself: Miro’s body.

“How?” Uriel asked, frowning. “Didn’t you say the Labyrinth’s space-time was indestructible?”

A faint, weary smile touched Kariel’s lips.

“When I attacked the Labyrinth’s space-time with a cloth bomb, Optrus slipped through the resulting rift. Embedded in its electromagnetic patterns was residual data—fragments of the Labyrinth’s own time-space structure. I analyzed it.”

“But the space-time healed instantly. Couldn’t you have broken through her dimensional barrier and brought her here directly?”

“The dimensional barrier isn’t physical,” Kariel replied. “It’s conceptual. The Kingdom of Earth operates under a different protocol—entirely independent, with no quantum entanglement to this realm. We couldn’t cross it… until now.”

Uriel didn’t bother to grasp the full meaning. Angels of destruction were not meant to understand the intricacies of what they shattered.

“So?”

“I deciphered the protocol using the data from Optrus. Then, through quantum superposition, I pulled the Labyrinth into the Great World War.”

Uriel grunted. “And?”

Kariel sighed. He knew Uriel wouldn’t understand.

“Imagine reaching into a barrel the size of the universe,” he said, “and pulling out the one item you wanted. That’s what I did.”

Uriel finally understood why Kariel was on the verge of collapse.

“So you scoured the entire cosmos… and kidnapped her.”

Kariel closed his eyes.

“Yes.”

He didn’t correct him.

Uriel turned his gaze back to Miro.

She sat motionless, her consciousness immersed in a state of deep trance. Just before the final war, she had been the lone figure who stood against Heaven’s army, tearing open the dimensional wall with sheer will.

“The human spirit,” Uriel mused, “is formidable in its own way.”

Even now, Miro’s mind—the very core that sustained the Labyrinth’s space-time—was being drawn into the dimension of cowardice. A realm of fear, doubt, and psychological erosion.

It was a state incomprehensible to an angel. Beings born perfect, with no flaws, no uncertainty—how could they understand such weakness?

“Hmph. That’s the mark of imperfection,” Kariel said coldly. “Don’t sentimentalize insignificant beings.”

Uriel didn’t respond.

To him, destruction was enough. Understanding was irrelevant.

But Kariel was different.

Birth—the concept he embodied—was founded on comprehension. To create life, one must understand it.

And yet, Kariel could not understand humans.

That was the greatest contradiction in the universe.

“Strange creatures,” Kariel murmured. “Even now, one human resists the full might of Heaven.”

His voice cracked.

*Human. Human.*

He still couldn’t understand why everyone—Ankera included—treated humans differently. Why they were granted exceptions, protections, special attention.

“It means nothing,” he said, clenching his fists. “I will destroy it.”

Uriel turned away. He had no desire to provoke the twin archangels—born on the same day, yet embodying opposing concepts.

“Fine,” he said. “But what now? Even if you kill Miro, the Labyrinth’s space-time won’t vanish. Why bring her here and torture her mind to the brink of extinction?”

“Because I’m going to pull the Labyrinth out of the fear dimension,” Kariel said. “Once she regains consciousness, I’ll kill her. And when she dies *here*, in this realm, the Labyrinth’s space-time will collapse.”

It was the only way. The *right* way.

“But we can’t understand human imperfection,” Uriel said. “There’s no one in Heaven who can grasp Miro’s spirit. The Nephilim are gone—erased with the Guffin.”

“There is one,” Kariel said. “Not in Heaven. In the Kingdom of Earth.”

Uriel tilted his head.

Yes, Nephilim were known to awaken spontaneously in the mortal realm.

But as long as the Labyrinth’s space-time blocked access, no one could be brought here.

“Are you going to search the universe again?” Uriel asked. “You barely survived the last time. And if you wait too long, she might vanish completely.”

“I don’t have the strength,” Kariel admitted. “But I don’t need to search.”

He floated toward the central pillar—a towering system interface—and placed his hand upon it, accessing the Akashic Records.

Uriel followed and stood behind him, watching the screen.

“I was reviewing the Records,” Kariel said, stepping aside.

A name appeared on the display.

As Uriel read the accompanying data, he nodded slowly.

“Interesting.”

More than that—*perfect*.

This human understood the human spirit better than any other.

No—*because* he understood it, his current state was all the more revealing.

“I will summon Lee to the Great World War,” Kariel said.

He raised his hand. A celestial light flared, and a Law Code manifested in his palm—a tome forged from the principles of the universe. Its cover was a dense, metallic alloy, its pages composed of shifting light.

As the central system activated, arcs of blue electricity surged across the floor, converging into the arched mechanism above.

Uriel watched in silence. Then, his eyes flicked back to the screen.

One name pulsed faintly in the data stream—

Morrigan Arius.

A being who existed only as a concept.

A grave robber of fate.

Rebel 1st Command

Shirone’s arrival was met with thunderous cheers.

The tale of how he had felled hundreds of giants with the power of an archangel had spread like wildfire through the rebel network, relayed by drones and whispered in awe. It had already become legend.

Yet amid the roaring celebration, Shirone’s expression remained solemn.

The headquarters bore the number “1,” but its scale far exceeded expectations. Dozens of structures—likely munitions depots—surrounded the central complex, and at its heart stood a colossal building, over thirty meters tall, rising like a fortress from the forest.

*Incredible power,* Gaold thought. *If they ever mobilize…*

*Not even the seeds will survive,* he finished silently.

Yes, the base was formidable.

But the battalion commander’s words rang true—this force was on the brink of annihilation.

It wasn’t about numbers.

Heavenly beings were in a different category entirely.

It was like ants swarming a human—technically, they could kill. But the human wouldn’t even register them as a threat.

“Hey! There he is! Shirone! Over here!”

A familiar voice cut through the noise.

From the crowd emerged Clove and Gadlock—the two who had guided Shirone and his companions to Nord’s shelter in Heaven a year ago.

The memory of Clove ignoring Amy still stung, but seeing a familiar face in this moment was a relief.

Still, Shirone hesitated to approach.

The moment Clove called out, the entire crowd surged forward, a wave of bodies threatening to crush anyone in their path.

“No way! It’s really him! Shirone is here!”

Clove and Gadlock were instantly swallowed by the mob, vanishing from sight.

Few outside District 73 had ever seen Shirone’s face—this was the first time many had laid eyes on the legend.

Shirone stepped back, his expression cold.

This wasn’t safe. People could get hurt.

Watching him retreat, Clove tugged at her hair in frustration.

“I can’t believe that kid became *this* important! If I’d known, I would’ve treated her better back then!”

Gadlock smacked her on the head.

“Does it matter now? We’re fighting for survival!”

Clove pouted. “But the Master said it too—when Shirone comes, things will get better here.”

Gadlock fell silent.

Shirone had returned.

And at the perfect moment.

Heaven’s assault had paused—undoubtedly a blessing.

But that alone couldn’t close the widening gap between Mecca and the Norse after a string of defeats.

The command had fractured, splitting into First and Second Headquarters. No battle had been fought, yet the loss was already absolute.

Just as despair had settled over them, the light of District 73 returned.

*Why now? Did he come back knowing?*

The pieces connected too neatly to dismiss as mere coincidence.

“Hehe, it’s so nice walking with you like this,” Lena said, clinging to Shirone’s arm as they headed toward the command center.

Fleur, watching from behind with a stiff back, clicked her tongue.

“What’s she doing? Did she come to Heaven just to flirt?”

Gaold shrugged. “Leave her be. A little showmanship won’t hurt.”

The rebels were on the verge of collapse. A miracle like Shirone’s return deserved a little spectacle.

But Kanya was mortified by her sister’s behavior.

Flushed with embarrassment, she hurried over and whispered fiercely into Lena’s ear.

“What are you doing? Let go! Don’t you understand what Shirone means to the rebels? What if people get the wrong idea?”

“What idea?” Lena shot back. “Shirone *oppa* and I aren’t even close. We haven’t seen each other in a year. Can’t I just walk with him?”

She turned to Shirone.

“Brother and sister, stand arm-in-arm with me, okay?”

Shirone opened his mouth, but no words came.

His heart was pounding. Two women on his arms? In front of *everyone*?

Kanya’s face turned crimson.

“You—what are you implying? Why would *I* be arm-in-arm with Shirone?”

“Oh, right,” Lena said sweetly. “My sister’s too shy. We need to look close to Shirone *brother* so we can get positions at headquarters. Besides, her Kuroi was damaged. She needs a new one to become a pilot again.”

Kanya choked on her words, stunned into silence.

Though young, Lena was thinking ahead.

Clever? Or just shameless?

Shirone placed a hand on Kanya’s shoulder.

“Don’t worry, Kanya. I’m the one who destroyed your Kuroi. I’ll make sure the Commander knows.”

Relief flooded Kanya’s eyes. She glanced back at Lena, her expression dark.

*That fox…*

Lena, oblivious—or perhaps delighting in it—wrapped her arm tighter around Shirone and smiled.
