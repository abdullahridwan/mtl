Final Decision (2)

The Akkadian Desert, the world’s largest expanse of sand, lay west of the Paras Kingdom on the map, its dunes stained a deep ocher. It was an endless sea of sand, a place where life struggled to survive under eons of relentless heat. Yet, human footprints marred the smooth, cream-like surface of the dunes, leading toward a colossal pyramid that rose from the sands like a mirage. Though all the oases in the heart of the Akkadian Desert had long since dried up, and even seasoned travelers could barely reach it, few knew that this ruin was actually a dungeon—the home of the world’s most famous mage. It was classified as an A2-infiltration-difficulty dungeon.

Kang Nan, the former chief secretary of the Tormia Magic Association, strolled leisurely through the labyrinth. Wherever she passed, tier-five monsters and above pressed themselves against the walls, writhing in suppressed agony. The sharp click of her high heels against the stone floor echoed with an oppressive aura that kept the beasts cowed. Her attire was a mess from a long battle: a missing button left her blouse gaping open, and her stockings were frayed and torn in places. In her right hand, she dragged a long, curved horn she had torn from a monster’s tail. The horn was still attached to the tail of a massive, green-scaled basilisk weighing well over two tons. The desert’s king, with its hideously depressed nose, loped along behind her, tongue lolling. It was an absurd sight—Kang Nan casually dragging a beast twenty times her size—but her light, unhurried gait betrayed that she felt none of the weight.

At the end of the labyrinth, Kang Nan pushed open a heavy stone door. Inside lay a vast cavity, its walls lined with countless desert creatures preserved like dried bark. At a table eighty meters away, a figure in a turban sat with his back turned, working intently. Instead of knocking, Kang Nan tossed the basilisk’s tail toward the table. *Whoosh!* A majestic echo reverberated through the Great Cavity.

“If you know I’m here, could you just let me in?”

Only then did the turbaned figure turn around. She was a slender woman draped in a desert-colored cape. Her large, almond-shaped eyes lacked double eyelids, and her chin was delicately slim. A pacifier rested between her lips, contrasting with her deep, dark eyes to create an oddly mysterious aura.

“It’s been a while, Kang Nan.”

The woman’s name was Zulu. She was a certified first-class archmage of the Paras Kingdom, the only tier-one summoner capable of creating a lich, and widely considered the strongest summoning wizard on the continent.

“Yes. You seem to be doing well.”

Kang Nan spoke in the local Middle Eastern dialect, but Zulu insisted on answering in the continental language.

“What am I? I haven’t been out much since then.”

Zulu’s speech retained the habit of attaching honorific suffixes to certain endings. It clashed with the grammar of the continental language, but it was a deeply ingrained habit she couldn’t shake.

“Sit down. I’ll bring you something to drink.”

Kang Nan’s expression darkened with concern. With Zulu’s magical prowess, she could conjure water in an instant, but the problem was that she refused to. It seemed that upon reaching the rank of first-class archmage, Zulu had developed a personal rationality that defied common sense.

True to form, Zulu poured an unknown liquid into a glass beaker and set it on the table. Kang Nan’s expression grew uneasy. In the desert, water was more precious than jewels. Even if it wasn’t for drinking, the experiment equipment in the Great Cavity required massive amounts of it. The fact that the preserved monsters around them were completely desiccated wasn’t due to eccentric taste—it was because every drop had been meticulously harvested.

Zulu removed the pacifier from her mouth and took a sip from the beaker. Since the water was so precious, Kang Nan had no choice but to pretend to drink hers as well.

“I’ll have a good drink. Surely it won’t taste like desert maggot urine like it did ten years ago?”

“I wouldn’t give you something like that after all this time. This is very precious water.”

Kang Nan tilted the beaker and took a cautious sip. As soon as her tongue touched the liquid, a refreshing exhilaration rose in her throat.

“It’s fine. So, what is it?”

“It’s my urine.”

Kang Nan choked, barely managing to keep the liquid from spilling onto the floor—a testament to her respect for the first-class archmage’s favor.

Zulu smiled. “Don’t worry. It’s distilled water.”

“Of course it is. But I feel like I’ve got a lot of it circulating in my body right now.”

“Whoops. You’re still the same, Kang Nan.”

Zulu’s gaze drifted, recalling the day they had first met ten years ago. Though Kang Nan had looked more rustic and youthful back then, the ruthless edge that gripped Gaold—the world’s madman—had been just as sharp.

*You’ve grown a lot,* Zulu thought.

Back then, Kang Nan had been a blood-soaked wolf, a hostile beast that bared her fangs at anyone who approached. Now, much of that belligerence had been tempered. If Kang Nan were to descend to the nineteenth floor of the pyramid without Gaold, she would have to assume Simma had already slipped away.

“The elements of the natural world cycle,” Zulu began, her voice calm. “There is a cycle within a cycle. The act of drinking water is not merely to possess it, but to intervene in the great cycle of life and death, setting another in motion. Humans can assimilate with nature when they entrust themselves to a perfect cycle, with nothing to discard or leave behind.”

Kang Nan listened quietly. It wasn’t magic that mattered here, but truth. At the pinnacle of any discipline, the principles converged. There was nothing to dismiss in the teachings of a first-class archmage.

“Is that the secret to commanding the King of the Dead?”

A lich was a thought-body forged by combining the flames of a great wizard. Because the target was already dead, it was said that the final stage of boss mechanics—extinction—could never be triggered. Rumors in the academic world claimed Zulu herself had become a lich, and the summoned beast was merely a doppelganger, but Kang Nan, having witnessed the summoning firsthand, knew it was a lie. Ten years ago, she had simply been overwhelmed by it. Now, serving as chief secretary, her curiosity had turned to obsession.

“How on earth is that even possible?”

Zulu turned her head away, her dark eyes clouded with sorrow. “This is the land of the dead.”

The explanation was simple, yet Zulu seemed to know Kang Nan well enough to understand her hesitation. If one truly grasped the principle of circulation—that a vessel can only be filled when it is empty—there would be no reason to hide the secret of the boss. But the truth of life was simply too vast to be captured in words.

“I see.”

The Great Cavity of the pyramid was Zulu’s cradle. Abandoned there as an infant, she had survived entirely on her own. Had the dungeon’s monsters become her parents? Zulu wasn’t sure. When she finally came of age, she was still alone, with nothing but a small black pacifier that her parents must have left behind. From that day on, Zulu claimed the massive dungeon as her home.

Death rested on her shoulders constantly. Whether she slept, ate, or simply breathed, it was always there. It was a survival far removed from the desperate struggle of the slums in Radum. For Zulu, survival wasn’t a longing for life; it was just another form of death, wearing a different mask.

Kang Nan had endured a harsh life, but she couldn’t even begin to imagine what Zulu’s existence had been like. She had spent her childhood among hundreds of monsters in a dungeon where food and water were nonexistent. It was perhaps inevitable that Zulu would grow up to become the world’s strongest summoning wizard.

“I came because I have a favor to ask of you.”

Getting straight to the point, Kang Nan bowed deeply, pressing her forehead against the table in a gesture of earnest pleading.

“Help Gaold.”

Zulu’s gaze drifted somewhere distant. After a long pause, she slowly opened her mouth and replied in the Middle Eastern dialect.

“I understand.”

Kang Nan’s head snapped up. The acceptance had come far faster than expected. Extracting Zulu from the pyramid was notoriously difficult. Many high-ranking mages, even members of the Paras royal family, had begged her to become her aides, only to be turned away.

Above all, Zulu was unaware of the current situation. The plan they had discussed ten years ago had completely unraveled. They were now asking her to take a gamble with a slim chance of survival.

“I’m sorry. There’s something I haven’t told you. Things have changed suddenly…”

“Gaold is like a child,” Zulu interrupted softly.

She was recalling the first time she had met Gaold.

“He isn’t trying to empty himself. He clings to the end, refusing to let go. He holds on so tightly that he’s about to explode, even though he’s dying in pain.”

Kang Nan fell silent, listening.

“Nevertheless, the reason I was able to hold on was because I was waiting for the right moment to pour everything into it. Now is probably not that time. If this is all part of a larger cycle, then I suppose Gaold might as well stop holding back.”

“Zulu.”

Kang Nan swallowed her tears, drawing strength from the words. Why was she crying over someone who understood the depths of a madman’s obsession? Was it sympathy for a genius abandoned after twenty years of relentless pursuit? Or was it something else?

“And.”

Zulu smiled, her eyes bright like a child’s. “Because whenever I’m with Gaold, something interesting always happens.”

Kang Nan’s lips curved into a wet, grateful smile. That was it. A pure, unadulterated obsession that ordinary people would never comprehend. Perhaps she was simply possessed by the madness of someone who had bet everything on a single outcome.

Zulu stood up, ready to leave. All she did was bite down on her pacifier and pack two water bottles into a small bag. It was a remarkably simple preparation for a first-class archmage.

“Let’s go. It’s a quick ride to the continent if we take the Kydra.”

The Kydra was a tier-three summon monster—a gigantic bird capable of flying seven thousand kilometers a day.

As Zulu walked out of the Great Cavity, Kang Nan watched her back and lowered her head in a traditional Ram Muay bow. In a way that differed from her respect for Gaold, this was genuine reverence.

*Thank you.*

Kang Nan glanced back at the beaker on the table.

*Cycle. It can only be filled when it is empty. If you can’t empty it, you can’t fill it.*

Taking a deep breath, she lifted the beaker and drank more than half of it in one go.

“Ugh.”

It still didn’t quite sit right. The taste was unmistakably water, but the distillation technique of the Ilsimjo wasn’t meant for human consumption. Wiping the lingering moisture from her mouth with her tongue, Kang Nan followed Zulu out of the Great Cavity.

* * *

The moment the sun rose, Shirone made his way to the principal’s office. The first task Gaold had given him was to deliver a note to Alfeas.

When Alfeas saw Shirone appear unexpectedly, he looked surprised, but after reading the note, he simply nodded in silent understanding. Shirone left the office without saying a word.

Kingdom intelligence agents were likely already swarming the school. As long as there was no confirmation that Gaold was coming, the school’s defenses wouldn’t be as tightly secured as the capital’s, but acting alone still felt too dangerous.

*If it’s the principal, he’ll handle it.*

Alfeas had survived every prelude battle, so Shirone could trust him to manage the situation. Besides, Olivia—a certified second-class archmage—was standing guard nearby. She would figure something out.

Now was not the time to worry about Gaold. Shirone was already a suspect, entangled with Gaold in ways that could cost him his life if he hesitated. In the face of a world-shaking event like Heaven, no one cared about Shirone’s well-being. He was on his own. He had to make his own judgment and carve out a way forward.

*Kariel…*

There was a reason to meet him at least once after burning all his bridges, but the situation was vastly different from a year ago. Even if he returned alive, he would be branded a traitor for the rest of his life, forever hunted.

Fortunately, Shirone still held the initiative. Once Gaold discovered the possibility of destroying Heaven, he would inevitably classify Shirone as a key asset to the project. If that happened, at least a foundation for survival would be laid.

*First, information gathering. I have to solve these problems one by one, as carefully as possible.*

Short-sighted thinking might actually be more effective if things spiraled completely out of control. For now, it was best to tackle the immediate crisis step by step, moving forward like a blind man navigating a dark room.
