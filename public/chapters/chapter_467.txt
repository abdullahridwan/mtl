Chapter 467  
The Day of the Full Moon (1)  
~*~

Silence.

In that silence, a storm of emotions flickered across Ethella’s face—surprise, recognition, calculation—before she smoothed her expression into something unreadable. She studied Horkin closely, as if seeing him for the first time.

He must have noticed her already. In this situation, the question wasn’t *how* he’d recognized her—it was irrelevant.

“It was an excellent performance,” Ethella said, her voice calm. “If you were part of Gaold’s group, you’d know we’re the Cage team sent by the Association.”

Years ago, when Horkin’s hair wasn’t yet streaked with gray, rumors had spread across the continent: a genius monk had emerged from the Carsis Monastery. Though Carsis was renowned, its inner workings were obscure unless one had a particular interest in monastic hierarchies.

Back then, Horkin had stopped by the monastery’s headquarters on a mission and witnessed a young girl at a succession ceremony.

*I almost missed it. I’m glad I haven’t lost my edge yet.*

Romi Ethella.

A teacher at Alpheas Magic School.

What were the odds of encountering a professor from Gaold’s own academy, here in this forsaken place?

Even if such a coincidence wasn’t impossible, Horkin was certain—Ethella was the key they’d been searching for.

“Is this the last resort of those who knew but pretended not to? A clever choice,” Horkin said. “Now that you’ve played your part, tell me—where is Gaold?”

Behind him, twenty wizards stood in formation, their combined energy pressing down like a storm cloud. The air itself seemed to tremble under their presence.

*It’s dangerous.*

Armand, assessing the situation as a mage, knew a direct confrontation would be suicide. His teammates were strong, yes—but so were the enemy. And now, Romi Ethella and Olifer Siina were among them.

While the others hesitated, watching for movement, Quan—the swordsman—stepped forward.

A warrior who had roamed battlefields for years, Quan understood the weight of a fight. But to him, war was simple: see the enemy, cut them down.

“Do nothing,” Horkin warned. “If you move at all…”

“What if I do?” Quan snapped, already lunging.

In an instant, his body blurred—forward, spinning, blade leading.

But Horkin was already gone.

A gust of wind tore through the space where he’d stood. Ten meters away, a man in a weasel-skin coat held Horkin aloft by the collar.

Stiklo, a fifth-class wizard specializing in sonar magic. A tracker.

Sonar traveled at about 1,500 meters per second through liquid. If the transmission speed exceeded Mach 4, a clumsy attack like Quan’s wouldn’t land from the start.

Stiklo spoke in a dry, emotionless tone. “You’re quick-tempered.”

“Do nothing,” Horkin repeated. “Not even a twitch of the tongue.”

Quan didn’t listen. He charged again.

And this time, the battlefield exploded into motion.

Twenty-five figures moved at once.

Aroella of Fire locked onto Shiina, flames erupting from her very hair. “Fire Extreme!”

A torrent of fire surged forward, thick and searing. But Shiina clenched her fists, eyes wide. The air cracked like breaking glass.

Thousandfold thorny flowers bloomed in the air—ice, crystallizing from nothing. The flames of Fire Extreme died instantly, drowned in a wave of cold.

*Definitely not allies.*

Both sides had tested each other. Now, the real fight began.

Team Cage advanced.

Rose, their leader, unleashed fragrance magic that clouded the mind. Mami’s speech magic sent waves through the earth. Buffs, shields, recovery spells—layered, precise. A battle mage, every bit as lethal as the swordsmen, carved through Armand’s defenses.

They were pushed back—two kilometers in minutes.

Then came the *Water Dragon*, a colossal surge of liquid summoned by Monrang. It crashed across the battlefield, unstoppable.

But Shiina raised her hands.

“Absolute Zero.”

The water dragon froze mid-attack, a monstrous sculpture of ice.

Ethella moved.

*Yin-Yang Wave Fist – Short Wall.*

From ten centimeters away, her fist struck Monrang’s side dozens of times in rapid succession. The liquid armor absorbed the blows, but the wave-like force shattered even that.

*We have to eliminate at least one!*

As Ethella stepped in for the finish, a bald man hurled himself at her, tackling her to the ground. He clung to her shoulders, immobilizing her.

Veins pulsed at his temples, swelling with unnatural strength.

*Keugh! What kind of woman is this strong…?*

He was the bishop of the Carsis Monastery—Benifis. A battle mage whose power grew by consuming his opponent’s flesh.

He opened his mouth wide—and bit into Ethella’s neck.

Flesh tore. Blood welled.

Benifis moaned in ecstasy, chewing slowly.

Ethella didn’t scream. She arched her back and drove her forehead into his face.

*Crack!*

Benifis’ nose shattered. Blood gushed.

But the buffs and recovery magic kept him locked in place.

“Now!” Benifis roared.

Tarvan raised both palms.

*Kaiser Blast.*

The vacuum explosion that had once destroyed the Demon God Belkyr detonated in a blinding wave.

Armand’s party, spread in a fan formation, was obliterated. The radius was too vast, the force too absolute.

When the haze cleared, Team Cage stood victorious.

“What…?”

There was nothing.

No bodies. No signs of struggle. Just a flat, barren plain—scoured clean, as if everything had been erased.

Tarvan turned, frowning. “This… who’s going to explain?”

Aroella spoke first. “It was magic. The blue-haired girl was fighting me up close. There’s no way she could just vanish.”

Monoros, an unlocker and fellow combatant, nodded. “If I had to guess, it’s the man with bandages. He’s an unlocker like me.”

Saint Moriac scoffed. “What kind of unlocker magic makes twenty-one Cage-level fighters disappear without a trace? Is there even such a spell in the world?”

Mami, her voice serpentine, narrowed her eyes. “It’s not gone. It’s… disconnected. Like something’s broken.”

“It’s *Stop*,” Horkin said.

Everyone turned.

“You mean *Stop Magic*?”

“Yes. I’ve heard of one in the Ivory Tower. A blind wizard who wields it.”

Roche, a metal mage, shook his head. “I can’t be sure. Just because someone’s blind doesn’t mean they’re from the Ivory Tower. And even if they are, there’s no guarantee they’d use *Stop* on us.”

Horkin’s logic was sound, but Roche’s caution was reasonable. Blind wizards were rare. Ivory Tower mages rarer still.

But there was only one person in the world who could use *Stop*.

“Besides,” Saint Moriac added, “why would the Ivory Tower help Gaold? Gaold doesn’t trust *anyone*. He’s been in hiding for twenty years.”

Rose raised a hand, silencing them. “Enough. There are too many variables. One thing is certain—the enemy has *Stop Magic*, or something just as powerful. And…” She hesitated. “They’re helping Gaold.”

Even Rose, usually unshakable, had to acknowledge it. The mission had just become infinitely more dangerous.

Horkin folded his arms. “This is what the manager fears. As long as that blind wizard is with Gaold, eliminating him is impossible.”

Tarvan scowled. “So we just sit here and do nothing?”

Wagan, the communication wizard, stepped in. “Wait. Before we panic—why were *they* here? If they’re helping Gaold, what were they doing in this location?”

Horkin stroked his beard. “Hmm. Good point. There’s no reason to scatter forces in such a dangerous area. In fact, we’re inefficiently grouped ourselves.”

“Exactly,” Rose said. “And Gaold is obsessed with Miro. If someone was important enough to split his team, that’s a major clue. If we trace their movements, Gaold might come out himself.”

“Indeed…” Horkin murmured.

Rose rested her chin on her hand. “Then it’s settled. Return to the mainland. Find out who’s manipulating Ethella. That’ll lead us to Gaold. But—combat is a last resort. The man in bandages must not be engaged.”

No one objected.

They weren’t the type to blindly follow orders. But even they agreed—stay as far from that bandaged man as possible.

“Then let’s move.”

Twenty-one figures shot into the sky and vanished.

* * *

It was early evening when Shirone returned from inspecting the units under the Second Rebel Command.

But the sky was bright.

The moon—ten times larger than on Earth—hung overhead, casting an eerie, silver light.

“Wow,” Shirone whispered, staring up as if entranced.

It was the Great Full Moon—the night when Purgatory’s negative laws reached their peak.

No wonder the forest near headquarters was alive with monster cries.

“Ominous,” Fleur said, stepping beside him. “Something’s going to happen.”

“Don’t worry,” Shirone replied. “Even angels aren’t patrolling these days. If it’s not an angel, group magic can handle it.”

He entered headquarters, nodding to the guards. “I’ll go in first. Thanks for your effort.”

“You too, Captain.”

Back in his room, Shirone collapsed onto the bed, exhaustion heavy in his bones.

He barely had time to close his eyes before the door burst open.

“Shirone!”

Fleur rushed in.

“Huh? Senior?”

After checking the hallway, Fleur shut the door and sat beside him.

“I need to tell you something.”

Shirone sat up. “You look serious. What happened while I was gone?”

“I checked the underground facility a few days ago. But… there…”

Fleur’s voice faltered. Her expression darkened.

A sharp pain lanced through Shirone’s skull. Just imagining the scene made his teeth grind.

Fleur was unshakable. For her to look like this—something was very wrong.

“What the hell is going on?” he demanded.

“A human clone,” Fleur said quietly.

“What? What clone?”

“I know why Reysis took our blood. It wasn’t just for experiments… not anymore.”

Then—

*Woo… woo… woo…*

An ominous sound wave rippled through the air.

Shirone’s heart stopped.

He didn’t remember hearing it before—but he *knew* it.

*No way…!*

By the time he and Fleur reached the hallway, headquarters was already in chaos.

The vibration of an angel.

Everyone in Purgatory knew that sound.

Outside, under the full moon’s glow, Ikasa hovered in the air—bare feet together, chin resting on her palm.

To the untrained eye, it was just a cliff.

But Babel’s search results didn’t lie.

No matter how well hidden, places where humans lived carried an artificial signature.

Babel, having scanned all of Purgatory, had pinpointed this location with 83.3785% certainty as the Rebel Headquarters.

“Is there anyone?” Ikasa called, voice echoing. “You insignificant humans! Show yourselves!”

The cliff remained silent.

Of course, she knew they were watching from within.

“Hmph. You’ll come out eventually.”

She spread her arms, absorbing the moon’s energy.

Fallen angels were weaker than their divine counterparts—but on the night of the full moon, even humans could be erased in an instant.

“Haaaaaa…”

With a cold groan, her astral body unfolded into a halo. Her consciousness merged with it.

Calculations completed.

Dark, ink-like magic circles spun in the halo like gears.

*I desire.*

*Infinite Justice Halo – Valhalla Action.*

*Puck!*

A fist slammed into the cliffside with a dull thud.

Then, like a storm of punches, grooves exploded across the rock face.

*Puff! Puff! Puff!*

The sound filled the world.

Dust billowed, rising faster than sight could follow. The mountain range trembled—then collapsed.

Inside, Norse guards screamed, clutching their heads as the headquarters crumbled beneath them.

Ikasa smiled, eyes half-lidded.

*Look how insignificant they are…*

In the moment her desire manifested, a dry pleasure curled up her spine.
