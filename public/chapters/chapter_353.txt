Second Outing (4)  
~*~

“Ten silver for ten snowballs? One silver apiece? That’s really expensive.”

For Shirone, who had grown up in the mountains and had never experienced anything like a festival, everything about the place was a culture shock.

Amy, however, accepted it as perfectly normal.

“Tourist attractions always add a premium. Besides, that Ice Queen stuffed toy is pretty famous. The borough makes them as a special product, and you can’t get one outside the winter festival. The design changes every year too, so there are people who collect them.”

“Oh, is that so?”

Shirone’s eyes sparkled.

If it was a festival-limited item, then it was only natural for its value to go up over time. And if he won the game, paying ten silver for ten snowballs would not be much of a loss.

“All right, I’ll try it. I’m looking forward to this.”

Shirone paid the ten silver.

“Ten snowballs, please.”

“Another brave challenger! May the Ice Queen’s blessing be with you.”

Shirone doubted anything good would come from the Ice Queen’s blessing, but he supposed the line was part of the attraction. After all, the Ice Queen herself was a tourist product.

He received a box of snowballs and entered the game area.

There was already someone ahead of him attempting the challenge, so Shirone waited for his turn while picking up one of the snowballs. It was compact and heavy, packed with water so that it had surprising weight.

‘With this much heft, it should reach, right?’

Throwing itself was not the problem.

But after watching the challenger, Shirone understood the trick. The snowball had to be sent to the spire on the opposite side through a hole in the wall.

Because the throwing angle was so limited, it was hard to put power behind the shot, and accuracy would drop sharply.

“Whew, this is harder than it looks.”

The challenger failed and stepped back, wiping sweat from his brow.

Those who came to the winter festival every year knew how difficult it was to win the Ice Queen collection, so no one seemed particularly disappointed.

At the host’s signal, Shirone stepped into position, set down the box, and picked up a snowball.

He rolled it between his fingers to gauge the distance, then opened his stance wide and threw with all his strength.

The snowball shot through the window, but gravity dragged it down. It struck the wall below the watchtower and burst apart.

Cheerful laughter rose from the spectators.

Normally, Shirone would have laughed along, but with Amy watching, his face grew hot.

Unable to bring himself to look back at her, he hurriedly picked up the next snowball and analyzed his mistake.

‘I can’t raise the angle, so I can’t draw out my usual power. This is much harder than I thought.’

Of course, since the prize was a festival-limited item, the game was not designed so that just anyone could win.

“Nngh…!”

Shirone clenched his teeth and threw again.

Perhaps his determination had some effect, because this time the snowball barely covered the distance. However, it flew past the soldier-shaped target and disappeared somewhere beyond it.

“Hah! Hah!”

Now fully invested, Shirone threw one snowball after another.

Since he had succeeded at Thunderstrike through repeated attempts, he thought that if he gradually reduced the margin of error, he would eventually manage it.

But by the time he had thrown five, he realized the truth.

‘Isn’t this harder than Thunderstrike?’

The rules were simple, but it was by no means a casual game.

It was made to look as if anyone could succeed, while the angle and distance had been cleverly adjusted so that only someone born with extraordinary strength or a trained professional could pull it off.

After throwing all ten snowballs, Shirone stood there gasping for breath.

Even in the cold weather, his body was hot, and sweat had formed on his forehead. Since he was wearing Armand at his waist, he could not even take off his coat.

Shirone bit his tongue in embarrassment and returned to Amy.

“Ugh, sorry. I failed.”

“What are you sorry for? It’s just a game. Anyway, that really does look difficult.”

After watching Shirone’s attempt, Amy guessed that the game’s difficulty was at the highest level.

Analyzing it with the Hong’s Eye, there were very few possible trajectories that could reach the target once force, gravity, and air resistance were all taken into account.

“Ha ha ha! What a spectacular failure. And you’re students from a magic school? I’d die of embarrassment.”

Shirone and Amy turned around at the same time.

A boy with muddy-colored hair approached with a sneer. Behind him stood a tall young man with curly hair and a girl wearing heavy makeup, both of them looking arrogant as if they had come to back him up.

“Long time no see, Amy.”

Zocre held out his hand.

Amy stared at him in silence for a long while, searching her memory.

After entering magic school, she had not really kept up with anyone outside her school friends—especially not boys.

“Um, sorry… Who are you?”

Zocre’s face crumpled.

How could she not remember him?

Even if she had rejected him, being confessed to by someone should have been an unforgettable memory.

At least, that was how Zocre thought.

He had never confessed to a girl before, but if someone had confessed to him, he would have been proud of it for the rest of his life.

“You don’t know me? Or are you pretending not to know? Zocre. From Alpheas Magic School. You really don’t remember?”

“Zocre? Zocre…”

Amy mumbled the name to herself, then opened her eyes wide and pointed at him.

“Ah! Ahhh! Wow! You’ve really grown up.”

It was not a delighted reunion, but her exaggerated surprise made Zocre’s shoulders rise in front of his friends.

The person recognizing him was none other than the famous Karmis Amy.

But he had not approached an old schoolmate merely to chat. He lifted his chin and looked down at her.

“Hey, shouldn’t you greet me properly?”

“Huh? What greeting?”

Zocre swept his arm toward the friends behind him, then pointed at himself with his thumb.

“From what I hear, you’re still in school. But we’re full-time wizards now. In other words, unofficial 10th-level wizards.”

Official wizard.

Amy felt her heart thump.

Since entering Alpheas Magic School at the age of twelve, those words had never once left her mind.

Praying that her expression would not stiffen, Amy held out her hand to Zocre.

“I see. Congratulations. You graduated after all.”

Amy had been thirteen at the time. She remembered little about Zocre himself, but the contempt she had felt when he ran away to another school was still vivid.

A smile spread across Zocre’s lips.

Amy’s poker face was perfect, but to him, her heart seemed as plain as if it had been laid open.

The genius of the Karmis family, recognized by the world, was still in the position of a student who had failed the graduation exam, while he stood before her as a wizard. She had to be resentful.

‘Well? How does it feel? When you rejected me, I felt far more miserable than this.’

Zocre turned his gaze to Shirone, who was standing there silently.

“And here we have a celebrity. Arian Shirone. They say you’re the kingdom’s greatest prospect?”

Shirone did not answer.

He understood that Zocre had not approached them with friendly intentions.

“As your senior, may I give you some advice? A prospect is only a prospect in the end. True victory comes after becoming a formal wizard. Well, for someone of your background, I suppose you’ve already done fairly well. Ha ha ha!”

Amy shouted, her face flushed with anger.

“Zocre! What is that supposed to mean? Apologize to Shirone right now!”

“Why should I? I didn’t say anything wrong. Oh, was that supposed to be an open secret? You’d be better off giving up for your own mental health. It’s already been published in academic journals. There isn’t a student alive who doesn’t know.”

“Really? Then should I say whatever comes out of my mouth too? Are you sure you’ll be all right with that?”

Zocre’s eyes widened.

He did not know what Amy intended to say, but he could not help feeling a sting of unease.

‘Amy…’

The Amy he had met again after five years had become far prettier than she had been as a child.

Her hair, which had looked different in the academic journals, was permed, and she had chosen bright, cute clothes. Anyone could see that she had dressed up with care.

‘Damn it. She must have dressed up for Shirone to see, right?’

Amy was the female student most talked about among the boys at Eins Magic School. If one counted the entire kingdom, the number of boys interested in her would surely grow even larger.

When Zocre imagined her putting on makeup for one person and choosing pretty clothes with care, he felt as if he had already lost to Shirone without even fighting.

‘I feel sorry for you, Amy. How can you date a commoner unless you’ve gone insane? No matter how talented he is, he’s still only a student.’

Seeing Shirone merely frown, unable to respond even after being insulted, annoyed Zocre further.

‘You were insulted, and you still won’t say a word? Are you looking down on me? No, he must be intimidated because I’m an official wizard. Hiding behind a woman—pathetic… Tsk, tsk.’

Zocre was mistaken.

To Shirone, who had fought against Bossun, a certified 4th-class magician, the title of official wizard did not carry much threat.

The reason Shirone was frowning was not because of Zocre.

It was because of Armand, hanging at his waist.

The human mind flowed like water.

And Armand’s rated control was such a delicate function that the synchro rate with the owner’s mind approached one hundred percent.

If Shirone did not perfectly control his emotions, Armand would move before his conscious mind could stop it.

That was why Armand was called a magic sword.

Of course, this likely had not mattered to the former owner, Jion. He could simply cut down whatever his emotions told him to cut down.

But Shirone had to continue living an ordinary school life.

As a commoner, if he committed murder, he could end up in an irreversible situation.

More than anything, ever since he had gone to Khazra, controlling his emotions had become much more difficult than before.

The Demon God Behemoth was sealed away, but through the keyword of anger, it remained connected to Shirone’s mind.

If his emotions rose to a level he could not control, Armand would slit his opponent’s throat before Shirone had time to reconsider.

‘Why did I have to come out wearing this? I’ll have to be more careful from now on.’

Seeing that Shirone did not rise to the provocation in the end, Zocre clicked his tongue. Then he pointed toward the game area and said to Amy,

“Looks like you can’t believe everything written in academic journals. He can’t even win one doll for his girlfriend. Shall we show you how it’s done?”

Amy shrugged.

It was a game anyone could play if they paid, so there was no reason for her to stop them.

“Sure. Do whatever you want.”

Zocre and the others entered the game arena.

Apparently conscious of Shirone’s earlier attempt, Vivian deliberately spoke loudly enough for them to hear.

“Guys, if you win the Ice Queen, you have to give it to me.”

“Give it a try. Well, if it’s Ludvans, it should be easy.”

The host handed over a box of snowballs.

Zocre was the one who paid, but Ludvans was the one who received the box.

Zocre turned to Shirone and Amy, who were watching with interest, and pointed at Ludvans.

“Ludvans is our school’s representative pitcher. You can look forward to this.”

Helm was a combat sport that originated in ancient warfare. It was a game in which two teams of five players struck each other with a rubber ball inside a covered arena.

Among amateurs, even children could enjoy it, but once it reached the semi-professional level of school competitions, fastballs could approach one hundred sixty kilometers per hour.

With tactics using the three-cushion and various breaking balls developed over time, it was a sport full of excitement and a high risk of injury.

Ludvans was the ace of his school, and Eins’s pitching team had been strong enough to reach the quarterfinals of the national competition the previous year.

“Well then… Shall I start by testing it out?”

Ludvans’s posture was completely different from that of an ordinary challenger. Every part of his movement had a mechanism behind it, and as those movements connected smoothly, he drew out the best possible throwing power.

He swung his arm forward as if toppling over.

With a sharp pop and a blast of wind, the snowball flew.

It struck the target dead on.

Vivian cried out in delight.

“Great! Success on the first try! As expected of our school’s ace!”
