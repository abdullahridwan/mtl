Chapter 470  
The Day of the Full Moon (4)  
~*~

The metal angel stood beneath the full moon, its smooth, gleaming body reflecting the pale light like a sculpture of living beauty. But Sein and the others saw past the surface. They felt the chilling emptiness beneath—the absence of soul, of warmth, of mercy.

Babel was beautiful, yes. But it was the beauty of a frozen lake: flawless, serene, and deadly cold. Just looking at it sent a shiver through the bones.

Sein’s Iron Wheel spun with a sharp, metallic whir, calculations racing faster than ever before. Yet even this speed felt insufficient.

*What is this?*

There was no overwhelming aura, no crushing pressure radiating from Babel. No signature of raw power. Instead, Sein sensed something far more unnerving—an unshakable, absolute stillness. A certainty that no matter how strong the opponent, Babel would not falter.

– Target setting: No priority.

– Run random target algorithm.

The red lens of Babel’s vision locked onto Lena.

No one could see where its focus truly lay—except one. Lena’s father, the Witcher, threw himself forward without hesitation.

“Dangerous!”

Was Lena’s selection—between her and Kanya—the result of a desperate father’s love manifesting as power?

The choice was correct. The Witcher’s body slammed into Lena, knocking her aside just as Babel’s hand pierced his chest.

“Oh… Dad?”

Lena’s face drained of color. Her body swayed, trembling on the edge of collapse.

“Dad!”

Kanya, blinded by fury, lunged at Babel.

– Detect enemy approach.

– Response type: Instant death.

Babel vanished—no blur, no afterimage. One moment it was there, the next, gone.

The Witcher’s body crumpled to the ground.

Due to the extreme relativity of Babel’s speed, Kanya’s brain couldn’t process the movement. In her perception, the Witcher fell first—then Babel reappeared directly in front of her.

Only one truth cut through the illusion.

*You’re dying.*

The cold tip of Babel’s blade halted just before Kanya’s neck, stopping with a soft, chilling click.

Over her shoulder, Sein stood in a combat stance, his Iron Wheel spinning sharply, recalibrating in real time.

One Equilibrium—just barely enough to account for the Witcher’s death.

But Babel had already moved beyond it.

It expanded its perception, analyzing the complex mechanics of Sein’s technique, then dismantled the intricate cogwheel operation of Equilibrium itself.

*Can it be?*

Equilibrium was the ultimate crowd control algorithm, simultaneously calculating Sanity and Emotion coefficients across multiple targets. To dismantle such a personalized, adaptive code in less than a second meant Babel wasn’t relying on pre-programmed responses.

It had *learned* it on the spot.

*It’s not intuition. Is it a stochastic algorithm?*

A method of using random iterations to simulate understanding—like rolling dice until the pattern emerges.

Even as a Servant, Sein’s computational speed was unmatched. Yet Babel’s processing power clearly surpassed his own.

“Back off,” Sein said, voice low and urgent.

“Oh, Dad…!” Lena sobbed.

“Hurry!” Sein snapped.

There would be no second chances. Hesitate, and they would all die.

Kanya still had something to protect—Lena. Clinging to that reason, she swallowed her rage and sprinted toward her sister.

“Lena! Come here!”

Babel didn’t move. It stood motionless, even as Kanya grabbed Lena and fled. Its red lens remained fixed on Sein, analyzing every micro-movement, every shift in stance.

– Target: Human. 99.8% accuracy.

– Type: Programmer.

– Consequences: Highest-level risk factor. Priority algorithm applied. 1st priority elimination target.

Slowly, deliberately, Babel turned its body toward Sein.

Two unparalleled minds, masters of arithmetic and logic, faced each other across the battlefield—recognizing, assessing, and challenging one another in silence.

“Emergency! All units, battle ready!”

Crude’s voice echoed through the night as she sprinted toward the hangar.

* * *

**Cloning Room**

Two aides sent by Reisis stood before a large vat, each holding a bucket of water.

The ingredients for Ilhwa’s sake were ready. All that remained was the final component: the corpse of Muusa, the Acid Poison King.

Reisis’s method was simple—immerse the body in water. The resulting acidic gas would be lethal within ten seconds, melting the lungs of any creature that inhaled it.

They had time to complete the task and escape. But as soon as the process began, the air grew thick and suffocating.

“If you take even one breath, you’re dead. Let’s finish this fast.”

“It’s fine, but… it’s kind of embarrassing. Even for clones.”

“You’re babbling. Have you even heard of dignity? It’s just meat. Get on with it.”

The iron door creaked open, releasing a wave of hot, humid air.

Inside, the cultivation chamber was vast and dimly lit. Clones moved about, absorbed in their tasks, unaware of the death creeping toward them.

One aide placed the bucket at the center and pulled out Muusa’s corpse. The moment it touched the water, the liquid began to bubble violently.

“Let’s go. Now.”

Covering their mouths with their collars, the rising white smoke already curling from the tank, the two aides bolted for the exit.

* * *

With Ikasa’s help, Shirone and Fleur had escaped the cage and were now racing through the underground corridors of the headquarters.

Most of the members were preoccupied with Ilhwa’s sake—no one noticed them.

Of course, their path was also protected by the fact that only a few had access to the core areas.

“We can avoid the grunts,” Shirone said, “but Akamai is the real problem. If we run into him, we’re finished—like a demon trapped in a barracks.”

“It’ll be fine—until Reisis realizes we’re gone. Let’s move fast. Where’s Garras?”

“I saw a map in the genetics lab. Let’s head there first.”

Fleur led the way, recalling the layout from memory.

Inside the genetics lab, Fleur focused on the massive schematic hanging on the wall while Shirone quickly scanned the documents scattered across the desks.

Thanks to the Ultima system, the text was instantly converted into code with just a glance.

It was a research report—on cloning.

Shirone’s clone had to be here somewhere. She flipped through the pages, her movements growing slower, more deliberate. Then she stopped.

Her hands trembled.

“How could you do this… What do you even know about humanity?”

The documents detailed the grotesque process of mass-producing clones using the genetic triad of Shirone, Fleur, and Garras.

Her fingers, once flipping pages rapidly, now froze.

Her eyes, reading through the Ultima system, widened in horror.

“Senior,” Fleur said, squinting at the map as if fighting a headache. “Have you seen this file? Clonal culture research data.”

Shirone exhaled slowly.

She wanted to tell him what she’d read—but the words stuck in her throat.

*What do we do? I’ll have to say it.*

“Found it,” Fleur said suddenly. “Mormot Containment Cell. It’s not far.”

Without another word, he turned and left the lab.

Time was slipping away. They didn’t know when Reisis would discover their escape.

Shirone felt a strange sense of relief. Sometimes, ignorance was a blessing.

Fleur stopped before a massive iron gate that stretched from floor to ceiling.

Shirone drew the phoenix-shaped artifact from Kubrick and unleashed a torrent of fire magic. The lock glowed red, then melted into molten iron.

They pulled the heavy doors open.

A roar erupted from within—followed by a sight so surreal it stole their breath.

Like the Ark from ancient myth, the containment chamber housed a grotesque menagerie—creatures of all shapes and sizes crammed into a space the size of a fourteen-story building.

At the far end, separated by a thick glass wall instead of iron bars, stood a single coffin-like tube.

Inside, a black, writhing mass the size of a human torso pulsed within a two-meter-tall glass cylinder.

Garras. The King of Breeding.

Even among this horde of abominations, its isolation spoke volumes.

Eight spider-like legs clung to the glass. Beneath its body, a lip-like organ twitched, ready to expel something unspeakable.

On its skeletal head, a single eye stared out—wide open, lidless, frozen in perpetual surprise.

“…Is that you, Garras?” Shirone whispered.

The creature’s gelatinous head folded in half, its single eye turning to face her.

When their gazes met, Shirone felt a primal chill.

This wasn’t just a monster.

It was a being of singular purpose. Singular desire.

There were creatures stronger than Garris. But no *species* stronger.

Garras shifted its gaze to Fleur, its eye probing, analyzing, dissecting his very biology.

Fleur winced. The creature’s eyelids—thin membranes that slid over its pupil like mucus—closed halfway, as if smiling.

A sharp pain shot through Shirone’s left temple. She remembered the warehouse. The horror. The headache returned, throbbing.

“Kiaaaaaa!”

Garras screamed, straightening all eight legs like spears.

Shirone and Fleur flinched backward, their bodies instinctively flipping away from the sudden surge of force.

Only when they felt the cold sweat on their backs did they realize how terrified they’d been.

“What do we do, senior?” Fleur asked, voice tight. “Are we really going to release it?”

They needed Garris’s power—to counter Akamai, to stop Ilhwa’s ritual.

But opening this cell wasn’t freeing a weapon.

It was opening Pandora’s box.

No—this was the door to *hell*.

Once Garris was free, command would collapse.

Every living thing would become a target for reproduction. The number of hosts would multiply exponentially.

“We have no choice,” Shirone said. “We release it now and run. Rescue the rebels, then strike the races.”

That was the only viable path.

There was no time to plan, no room to hesitate.

“Then… I’ll open it.”

Shirone’s hand trembled as it reached for the release mechanism.

No one knew what would happen next.

She pressed the button.

A soft *click*.

The lock disengaged.

They turned at the same moment, not daring to look back.

Shirone cast teleportation.

**Bang!**

The glass tube exploded outward, shards raining down like ice.

Garras’s body wobbled on its eight legs, adjusting to the open space—then began to expand, growing to fill the chamber.

“Kiaaaaaa!”

The scream grew louder, more resonant, vibrating through the walls.

Shirone and Fleur, already fleeing, felt the sound claw at their minds, as if the creature’s shriek was echoing inside their skulls.

“Aaaaaaa!”

They didn’t stop. They threw themselves forward, rolling, slamming the iron door shut behind them with all their strength.

Silence.

Then—screams.

Not from Garris.

From the countless creatures trapped within.

The thumping against the gate grew louder, more frantic.

Shirone finally understood.

They hadn’t opened a prison.

They had opened the gates of hell.

* * *

“Stupid children!”

Lacey burst through the door, fury in her eyes.

As the researcher had reported—Shirone and Fleur were gone.

Akamai lay collapsed in the corner, deflated and broken. Only Ikasa remained, sitting calmly inside the cage.

“I told you to watch them!” Lacey snarled.

“Well, I thought since Akamai was here, I could—”

The researcher’s head snapped sideways with a wet *pop*.

The attack was invisible—too fast for the human eye. But Ikasa saw it.

A grotesque whip, hidden beneath Lacey’s cloak.

“Hey,” Ikasa said, smirking. “You really wear that thing? It’s hideous. Even Gorgol, the ugliest of Maras, wouldn’t claim it.”

*Creeper!*

Lacey’s chest split open between interlocking fangs, revealing a pulsating mass beneath.

“You dare mock this form, weak angel? Soon, you’ll be digesting in my belly.”

“Ho-ho-ho! Yes, my beautiful body must be driving you mad with envy. But really—nothing can fix *that* face.”

Ikasa’s taunts flowed without pause.

She couldn’t act—not until the cause of Valhalla was fulfilled.

But an angel’s pride was worth more than life itself.

“I’ll tear you apart!”

Lacey stomped forward, each step shaking the ground.

Her skin rippled, her face twisting into a monstrous maw. With a deafening roar, she charged at the cage.

“Quaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaa!”
