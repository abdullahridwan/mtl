Chapter 394  
Special Training (1)  
~*~

Shirone was registered as an official tour student and began living at the Association. It had been Kang Nan’s idea. If they made his status public from the start, it would be safer than letting strange rumors spread.

The first thing he did was write a letter home. Kang Nan inspected it, and once she granted permission, Shirone went into the city to send it off. By the time he returned, Fleur was waiting for him on the third floor.

“Did you send the letter?”

“Yes. They said it would take about five days.” Shirone tilted his head. “But were you waiting for me?”

“Yes. The chief secretary asked me to.”

Shirone was still reeling from his interview with Gaold. When Kang Nan was involved, he could not help tensing up.

“Starting today, I’ll be helping you with your training.”

He had expected Gaold to offer some kind of assistance while he was here, but he had not imagined he would be taught directly by a professional mage.

Even so, he could not bring himself to be happy about it. Of all people, he had been assigned to Fleur—the nag.

“What’s with that look? Are you sick?”

“Oh, no. It’s just… sudden.”

“What is? The special training, or me?”

“That’s…” Shirone hesitated. “Both.”

Fleur giggled brightly.

“Good. That means it went well. You don’t have to be so nervous, though. I’m not the one who’ll be teaching you.”

“What do you mean?”

Fleur rummaged through her inner pocket. She stuck out her tongue and rolled her eyes as she searched, as if she had shoved it into some impossibly deep place.

“Ta-da! I got this from the secretary. Do you know what it is?”

In her hand was a large golden key.

“Well… it looks very much like a key.”

“This is the key to the magic library, the symbol of the Magic Association! You’re about to enter an area no one can access without the president’s approval!”

Shirone considered how he was supposed to react. He could not decide whether he ought to be delighted or shocked without even knowing what the magic library was like.

“What are you standing there for? Let’s go.”

Gaold had been the one considerate toward Shirone, but Fleur was the one practically bouncing with excitement.

The place she took him to was the sixteenth floor of the Association.

When the elevator doors opened, a solid iron door stood directly in front of them. Shirone stepped forward out of habit and nearly bumped his head against it.

Fleur smiled knowingly and inserted the key.

The door opened automatically, and a breathtaking sight unfolded before him.

The entire sixteenth floor was a library. Bookshelves stretched out in row after row, continuing so far into the distance that they looked like reflections between two mirrors.

“Welcome. Are you the tour student?”

A graceful beauty in her mid-thirties appeared beyond the iron gate.

She wore a formal black suit, and her black hair was neatly pinned up. Pink lipstick softened her light makeup, her eyebrows were finely trimmed, and her large eyes, set beneath deep double eyelids, were dark and striking.

“Hello, Isabelle.”

Fleur bowed politely, then turned to Shirone and gestured for him to come forward.

“Introduce yourself. This is Isabelle, the certified third-class mage in charge of the magic library.”

Shirone started and quickly lowered his head. Her rank and her youthful appearance did not seem to match.

“Hello. I’m Arian Shirone.”

“Yes, I heard about you from the president. The Magic Association has its eye on your talent, so work hard from now on. Come in. I’ll show you around the library.”

Isabelle turned first, and Fleur and Shirone followed.

“This floor stores magic books. There are roughly twenty-eight thousand of them. You could say that most of the prescribed magic registered in the kingdom is gathered here.”

“Pardon? Twenty-eight thousand?”

Shirone could not hide his astonishment as he looked around.

Every book here contained magic someone had developed. His whole body trembled with excitement.

Fleur raised a finger as if to say, See?

“That’s what I was telling you. Being allowed in here is an enormous privilege.”

Shirone barely heard her.

Some of the books in the library were thin volumes of only ten pages, while others were thick tomes of a thousand pages. Their covers varied in color and design depending on attribute and category, and even their heights were different.

It looked like a work of art that embodied the countless magics spread throughout the world.

‘Ah… so that’s why an archmage has to manage it.’

A magic book recorded the omniscience and omnipotence related to a specific spell.

If a person could enter the Spirit Zone and possessed an exceptional mind, the information inside was detailed enough for them to teach themselves a new magic.

Advanced magic books would be extraordinarily expensive even if a price were set on them, and a collection of twenty-eight thousand volumes had a value so far beyond imagination that even trying to calculate it would be troublesome.

“I’m sorry,” Shirone said. “I just couldn’t believe it at first. You look too young to be a great mage.”

Isabelle covered her mouth with her fist and laughed. Most mages were proud, and she seemed pleased to hear that she looked young.

“I know what you mean. But being an archmage doesn’t necessarily mean you’re strong or destructive. I do office work. Red Line ranks are determined by reputation and achievement points, but when I first came to the magic library, I was only an official seventh-class mage. Then I organized fifteen hundred magic books over ten years, and when my predecessor retired, I became third class. My achievement score rose abnormally high. In exchange, my reputation is extremely low.”

Achievements and fame varied greatly depending on one’s field. That was why, even among mages of the same class, some had high combat power while many others specialized in entirely different directions.

If she had worked in the Magic Association’s library for ten years, Isabelle likely had almost no practical experience.

But Shirone was impressed for another reason.

“Fifteen hundred books…”

The process of registering prescribed magic was done through interviews or correspondence with the magic’s developer.

When the omniscience and omnipotence of a spell were submitted in the form of a thesis, Isabelle reviewed them, summarized them, and bound them into a magic book.

In short, it was impossible work without an outstanding understanding of magic and vast knowledge.

‘Well, if she organized fifteen hundred magic books in ten years, it makes sense that she’d become an archmage.’

Of course, he also understood that enduring those ten years must have been incredibly difficult.

In that sense, Isabelle was more than qualified to be a certified third-class archmage.

“Anyway, just think of me as a comfortable older sister,” Isabelle said. “To be honest, being called an archmage feels too burdensome.”

It was an unfamiliar feeling for Shirone. Until now, the archmages he had met had all been terrifying people who seemed far removed from common sense.

Thanks to that, however, he was able to look around the magic library more comfortably.

The spellbooks were broadly classified by system, then further subdivided by chronology.

Some were very old, while others represented the latest trends in magic.

When Shirone saw a copy of photonization magic, he felt a faint thrill.

After the introduction, Isabelle told them to look around at their leisure and returned to her desk.

As she rested her arms on the desk and looked through her documents, a pen moved by itself.

Since Shirone had worked at the Ogent Great Library, he could guess how much Isabelle’s work speed must have accelerated after ten full years of practice.

‘With that level of skill, fifteen hundred books in ten years really isn’t impossible.’

Fleur slapped Shirone lightly on the back.

“Hey, what are you staring at? Isabelle is married. Don’t tell me she’s your type?”

Shirone waved his hands with a serious expression.

“Absolutely not.”

In any case, Fleur folded her arms and trembled with emotion.

“The chief secretary said it was fine for you to look into anything. First, let’s choose the magic you want to practice.”

Take anything and learn it.

Shirone understood Gaold’s intentions.

Beyond simple training, Gaold wanted him to look through a variety of magic and search for hints about a spell that could destroy heaven.

But even so, he had given Shirone permission to use the magic library.

It seemed Gaold had not been lying when he said he would spare no support.

Fleur was not stupid either, so she must have known that Shirone’s special training was connected to Gaold’s project. However, just as she had promised before entering the president’s office, she showed no sign of it.

‘No… maybe she really is just enjoying this.’

The two of them went to the section containing photon-type magic books, Shirone’s specialty.

Fleur looked through the books on the shelves and gave him advice.

“There’s no need to learn magic that’s too simple. The efficiency is low. For example, what are you going to do if you learn something like [Let’s Make a Ping-Pong Ball with Photons and Have Fun!]? A performance mage might need it, I suppose.”

Shirone took the three-page book Fleur handed him. It described a game where one bounced flares using mirrors, and it was written that the whole family could enjoy it and that it was effective for dieting.

“The same goes for magic developed too long ago,” Fleur continued. “If it’s outdated, that means a lot of research has probably already been done on countermeasures. On the other hand, you don’t have time to learn highly difficult magic. You’d need to invest at least half a year, and you have to go back to school.”

“Ah, I see.”

Her points were things Shirone had not considered, and they sharpened his mind.

He had to take into account not only the efficiency of the magic, but also the time required to learn it. The key was to find magic he could master while he was at the Magic Association.

“Of course, the choice is yours. Omnipotence involves personal preference. But you still need guidelines. First, choose magic that can make up for your weaknesses. Second, choose magic that can create synergy with the magic you already use. Third, balance quality and quantity. That should be enough.”

From then on, Shirone visited the magic library every day and searched through the books.

The sooner he chose a magic book, the more time he would have to practice, but this was not an opportunity that came often, so he wanted to choose carefully.

‘There are so many. It would take years just to look through the photon series.’

There was magic so trivial it made him laugh, magic so unexpected and ingenious that it surprised him, and magic so difficult it made him wonder whether it was even possible.

It was a whole other world. He felt like a child in a candy store, unable to collect his thoughts.

‘No, calm down. If I run out of time and can’t learn anything, I’ll regret it forever.’

After getting used to how the magic books were classified, Shirone focused on searching for magic that met Fleur’s guidelines.

Then, in the section of magic registered ten years ago, he found a title that caught his eye.

Teleportation Advanced Technique: Scatter Movement.

“Hmm, teleportation.”

He was interested because teleportation was one of his strengths. The fact that the book was not very thick also influenced his choice.

The clean white cover indicated that it was a photon-type magic book, and beneath the title Scatter Movement was the name of the magic’s developer: Kindra Buki.

“Kindra Buki. I’ve never heard that name before.”

Even though he did not know the mage’s face, Shirone respected him.

Whether independently developed magic was registered as kingdom-prescribed magic was left to the mage’s own discretion until death.

That meant there must be countless unregistered spells in the world, and Shirone could not help feeling sympathy for a mage who willingly revealed the core of his magic for the sake of later generations.

Without people like that, there would be nothing to learn at magic school.

“Then I’ll read it.”

Shirone reverently opened the book and read for about ten minutes.

“Hoo… I see.”

It was quite an ingenious spell.

‘No, this definitely works. It’ll work in actual combat too.’

Scatter Movement was a spell that scattered photon output in all directions immediately after casting teleportation.

The target would experience an optical illusion, as if the mage had spread out in every direction at once.

‘Hmm. So it uses passive magic, then transitions into active magic. Since passive magic lingers, it can produce the same effect as active magic.’

From the middle of the book onward, the omniscience and omnipotence techniques behind the spell were described.

As Shirone read with his chin resting on his hand, a brilliant idea suddenly flashed through his mind.

‘What if I combine this with God Particles?’

In other words, instead of emitting simple photon output, he would teleport while firing photon cannons in all directions.

If Buki had created Scatter Movement as a disruption tactic, Shirone could evolve it into a movement skill with offensive power.
