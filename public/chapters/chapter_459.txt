Chapter 459

Opportunity for Revenge (1)

Team Cage B had arrived on the mainland.

As twenty-one wizards marched down the road, civilians instinctively cleared a path. On the mainland, where law was dictated more by unwritten codes than formal rules, survival demanded sharp instincts. People had evolved a near-animalistic sense for danger—and Cage B radiated it.

Yet the team didn’t flaunt their power. Though composed of 4th to 6th-class mages, most were young prodigies, the kingdom’s finest talents. Only three—team leader Rose, vice-leader Horkin, and communication mage Wagan—had ever experienced true combat in the higher realms.

“Ahh… ahh…”

A small woman with short bobbed hair and bangs tied back scratched furiously at her arms and chest.

Aroella of Flame—certified 4th-class fire mage—was known for her volatile temper and flames that burned at 3,200 degrees Celsius. She also had a peculiar history: every man she’d ever dated had somehow managed to steal her entire estate.

“Why are you so itchy? Is the air bothering you?” Summoning mage Malloy asked, frowning.

“I’ve been scratching since we landed,” Aroella snapped, digging her fingers under her collar. “It’s not the air. My armpit’s been driving me insane. Beam, have you ever been bitten by some weird bug here?”

Beam, the Insect Mage, replied flatly:

“It’s because you don’t shave.”

“What? I don’t have hair there!”

Tarban, the team’s Terminator, snorted. “That’s even weirder.”

“Shut up!” Aroella snapped, sparks flickering around her fingers. “I’m in a terrible mood. Don’t push me, or I’ll burn you all!”

Tarban ignored her and turned to Rose. “So what’s the plan? Do we know where Gaold is? Should we just leave it to Jonah in the city center?”

Vice-leader Horkin shook his head. “Arthur. Even for a Cage-level team, the mainland isn’t easy. If we recklessly open the Spirit Zone and get tricked by some local con artist, the mission could collapse.”

Benifis, a meat-obsessed mage with a grizzled look, smirked. “Maybe it’s time you retired, old man. We’re the kingdom’s elite—demons fall before us. You really think we can’t handle a few mainland thugs?”

“A Cage team can crush demons,” Horkin replied coolly. “But here, even demons form alliances. They hunt in packs.”

Silence fell.

Rose finally spoke. “Heaven is dangerous. And our target—Mikea Gaold—is the kingdom’s strongest battle mage.”

Mysore Mami, a mage with sharp cheekbones and crooked teeth, scoffed. “So what? No matter how strong Gaold is, he can’t fight an entire team alone. Our combat power isn’t inferior.”

Horkin’s expression turned icy as he turned to her. “Then go catch him yourself.”

Mami swallowed hard, saliva sliding down her throat.

Rose continued, “Ideally, we eliminate Gaold without losing a single member. But… it wouldn’t be surprising if someone died. This isn’t a numbers game like first-class missions. Gaold is different.”

Only then did the team’s expressions harden with true resolve. It was the first time in years that Rose had placed the target above all else.

Tarban, meanwhile, showed no fear. It wouldn’t make sense for the team’s most destructive mage to hesitate before battle.

“If I can’t use the Spirit Zone,” he said, “I’ll just run on foot.”

Without another word, he turned and strode into a narrow alley.

The rest followed. They all knew they’d been watched since landing.

As they entered the alley, a group of armed men emerged—twenty in total, matching Cage B in number. Their eyes gleamed with predatory hunger.

“Who the hell are you?” one demanded.

Spirit Mage White linked the spirit channel and translated Tarban’s reply. The bandits didn’t flinch.

Their leader stepped forward, drawing a jagged sword. “You don’t know who we are? I could tell you were outsiders the second you started gawking like country bumpkins. Thought you’d be safe in a group?”

On the mainland, information was as valuable as strength. For first-timers, ignorance was fatal—and profitable for those who preyed on it.

“Tell me,” Tarban said, “what are you?”

The leader smirked. “Fine. I’ll give you three golden rules. One: when someone from the mainland speaks, you kneel. Two: you give them everything you own. Three: you run—leave the women behind. Now, follow them.”

Tarban glanced back at his team.

No one moved.

He sighed and stepped forward. “Fine. Here or there… doesn’t matter.”

Five minutes later, all twenty bandits lay sprawled across the alley floor, faces bloodied and broken. They hadn’t even forced Tarban to use his signature Kaiser Blast.

“Please… spare me!” the leader begged as Tarban grabbed him by the collar and lifted him off the ground.

“Just one question,” Tarban said.

“Anything! I’ll tell you everything—no, even if I don’t know, I’ll find out!”

Tarban almost laughed. The man’s surrender was faster than any spell he’d seen.

Wagan stepped forward, smiling. He activated a vision spell, projecting Gaold’s face into the air.

The bandit leader paled instantly.

“You’ve seen him,” Rose said.

Spirit Mage White approached. “You got what you wanted, Tarban.”

“Yeah, I already knew that,” Tarban said, though no one believed him. The result spoke for itself.

These were bottom-feeders—thugs who preyed on the unprepared. If Gaold had been careless enough to be followed by such fools, he wouldn’t have survived this long.

“Tell me everything,” White ordered the leader.

The man’s mind spun wildly, his thoughts flashing through the mental channel:

*‘The value of the information… weigh lives against cost…’*

“I… I don’t know much. But—”

White didn’t wait. He turned and walked back to the team. “It’s over. Let’s regroup.”

Tarban raised his fist in a silent victory gesture.

The leader, realizing he was about to be discarded, cried out in panic: “Wait! I saw something! I saw the truth!”

“Too late.”

Tarban slammed the man’s head into the wall. He crumpled, unconscious.

Tarban dusted his hands and turned to Horkin. “Disgusting. Is this really a place where demons roam like Cages? Feels more like a den of rats.”

Before Horkin could reply, movement at the alley’s far end caught their attention.

Tarban turned—and for the first time since landing, his eyes narrowed with genuine caution.

Ethella was walking down the alley, scanning a hunting list given to her by Frankwine, when she suddenly looked up. Something felt off.

Twenty-one people occupied the narrow passage.

Quan’s hand drifted to his sword hilt without a word. Just from their auras, these were all masters—each one a peak expert in their craft.

Tension crackled like static. Dozens of eyes locked onto one another, assessing, calculating.

Tarban had to retract his earlier words. This wasn’t just a backwater alley. This was the mainland—where even the shadows held warriors who didn’t flinch at the sight of twenty mages.

Horkin stroked his beard, a curious smile playing on his lips.

*‘Hmm. What now?’*

The alley was a dead end. The only way forward was through.

Fighting wasn’t inevitable—but neither side would back down.

Quan took a step forward.

Limping.

Twenty-one pairs of eyes tracked his uneven gait.

*‘He’s coming alone? Is he insane?’*

Unless he intended to surrender, there was no reason to advance down a blocked path.

*‘Funny. If you want us to leave, you should be the ones to move.’*

Tarban exhaled a wave of hostility, blocking the way.

Quan’s aura flared instantly—like a chemical reaction igniting.

“Wow…” someone on the team murmured.

Even Tarban’s lips twitched—not in anger, but in reluctant respect.

*‘He’s ready to die. No hesitation.’*

There was no such thing as a peaceful life in this world. But when someone walked forward knowing they might not walk back, it meant they’d already made their choice.

*‘Yeah. This is the level I need to be at.’*

Quan advanced, step by limping step. His gaze never wavered.

One step closer.

Then another.

Horkin finally spoke, voice low. “Don’t do this…”

But it was too late.

As Quan’s sword blurred into motion, Tarban became a flash of light—diverting sideways just in time.

“Tch!”

A swordsman’s reflexes were faster than a mage’s incantation. Only Horkin’s precise warning had prevented a bloodbath.

*‘Did he dodge? Or was the strike cut short?’*

A cold line ran down Tarban’s nose. Wind pressure? Or a shallow cut?

He didn’t check.

“Wait,” Ethella said, staring at Horkin. “Are you from the continent?”

Cage Team B tensed—then relaxed slightly. She’d spoken their language.

“Of course,” Horkin said smoothly, stepping forward with a diplomatic smile. “Didn’t realize we were countrymen. Almost started a blood feud over nothing. Nice to meet you. On a mission too?”

Ethella shook his hand, smiling back. “Yes. We nearly fought on the same side.”

*‘Same side?’* Wagan thought, stepping forward with calculated friendliness. “We’re looking for someone. Have you seen this man?”

Gaold’s face appeared in the magical projection.

Ethella’s eyes widened. “What? This is Mikea Gaold!”

Shiina added, “He’s the president of the Tormia Magic Association. Why are you hunting him on the mainland?”

Horkin blinked. *‘Hmm…’*

Technically, Gaold was the *former* president. But that wasn’t worth correcting.

Tarban cut in. “It’s classified. Just part of the mission.”

The atmosphere shifted. If both groups were on covert operations, prying further would be unwise.

“Understood,” Ethella said, bowing slightly. A polite but firm request to let them pass.

Some of the younger team members bristled, but Rose silenced them with a cold command.

“I’ll open it.”

Cage Team B split left and right. Armin and his party moved through the gap.

Most avoided eye contact—but Aroella glared at Shiina until the very end.

*‘That woman… she’s frozen inside. I can feel it.’*

Aroella had always believed that unexplained bad moods were the natural enemy of fire. Strangely, her instincts were rarely wrong.

As Armin’s group vanished down the street, Rose turned to White.

“What did you get?”

“Nothing conclusive. Their minds were shielded—no thoughts leaked. I couldn’t press without starting a fight.”

Rose nodded. “And the bandit?”

White gestured to the unconscious leader. “He did follow Gaold. But Gaold didn’t engage—left the mainland within an hour of arrival. Two others were with him: Kang Nan and an unknown woman.”

“An unknown woman? Hmm.”

Zulu, the world’s greatest summoning mage, was reclusive and foreign—few knew her face.

“But that’s strange,” Rose said. “If they left immediately, why come at all?”

“According to his memory, Gaold didn’t enter any building or rest. He arrived and left within the hour.”

“Then finding the reason is our fastest path to him. We’ll retrace his steps.”

As Rose led the team out of the alley, Horkin suddenly stopped.

“Wait,” he said, looking back. “That woman… I’ve seen her before.”

“Who?” Tarban asked.

“The one who shook my hand. She’s from the magical world—I’m sure of it. But I can’t place her.”

Tarban shrugged. “With that level of skill, you might’ve seen her at any summit. Doesn’t matter. I’m starving. Let’s move.”

Horkin didn’t answer. His gaze lingered on the empty path where Ethella and her group had disappeared.

Something about her…

But for now, the hunt continued.
