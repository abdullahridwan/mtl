Chapter 437

Final Decision (7)

The group emerged from the collapsing maze of time and space, regrouping in the hidden chamber beneath Alpheas. Gaold sat cross-legged on the stone floor, hands resting on his knees, his expression unreadable. Though the maze had vanished before they could fully explore it, that wasn’t his immediate concern.

“The final war may begin sooner than we anticipated,” Gaold said, turning to Shirone. “We need to move faster. Kang Nan is on his way to Galliant. One more person might join us—or might not. But now is the time to choose.”

He paused, letting the weight of his words settle.

“If the war breaks out, the one who stands against the enemy becomes a hero. But if you follow us now, you’ll be branded a traitor—one who accelerated the war’s arrival.”

To Shirone, the labels meant nothing. Hero or traitor, it made no difference. What mattered was survival. And if Gaold, a man of such skill and ruthlessness, was willing to risk everything, then Shirone’s own chances of living through this were less than one percent.

“If I ask for time,” Shirone said, “how much can you give me?”

“We leave for Galliant tonight,” Gaold replied. “I’ll wait for Kang Nan—but no longer than two days. If you decide to come, meet us in Galliant. Inspiration will handle the school affairs. Though, honestly, what does it matter when we’re walking straight into death?”

Gaold wasn’t afraid of risk. But he had no patience for hesitation.

“I need your ability,” he admitted. “But if you’re going to follow us out of blind desperation, you’re better off staying behind. Dragging a frightened child into battle only ensures we all die.”

Armin stepped forward, his bandaged form radiating quiet tension. Even after confirming the maze’s destruction, his purpose remained unchanged: the contract.

“Shiina does not go to heaven.”

Gaold exhaled sharply. “Ah, so that was the condition. Fine. Shiina, you’re out of the team. Effective immediately.”

Shiina shook her head. If Armin had come only to enforce this clause, and if Gaold had invited him solely for that reason, it stung his pride as a certified Fifth-Class Wizard.

“Why should I obey someone else’s decree?” she asked. “I’m going to heaven. That’s my decision.”

“You insist?” Gaold’s voice was cold. “Then the contract is void.”

Light flared from within Armin’s bandages. Keira swallowed hard as the blade at her side trembled, reacting to the rising tension.

“I’m not in the mood for games,” Armin said. “Fulfill the contract properly. If you want to reach heaven in one piece.”

Shiina had revealed her identity knowing full well she’d be hurt—only to prevent herself from being dragged into this suicide mission. As for rescuing the maze? That was a lie. Armin, an agent of the Human Security Enforcement Department, knew Heaven’s military structure better than anyone. Gaold, though a rare Red-Line First-Class specialist focused solely on combat, would struggle even against a single angel—let alone an archangel.

It wasn’t impossible, of course. No outcome in battle was absolute. A single variable, amplified, could shift the tide. But Heaven’s forces were unlike anything in the human world. Without intelligence, any clash would be decided purely by overwhelming force.

That was why both the Ivory Tower and the Temple sought to delay the final war—until they had more information.

Gaold, however, remained unfazed.

“What are you lecturing me about?” he said, shrugging. “I don’t need your favors. I’m no parasite like you. Even if I don’t take her, Shiina will go to heaven on her own.”

Armin’s temples throbbed with blood. “Keep it in check, Gaold. If you want to die, die alone. Your madness only drags the world down.”

“Sorry to inconvenience you,” Gaold sneered. “But what really pisses me off is how you ignore the suffering *you’ve* caused the world. I endured mine alone. You claim mankind suffered together—don’t whine to me. It makes me sick just listening.”

Armin snorted. “Suffering the world caused you? No. You’re just a madman consumed by twisted desires. Can you really say Miro had no choice? Judgment Day for the 20…”

“What do you know?” Gaold’s voice cracked like thunder.

His face went pale. Fire blazed in his wide, unblinking eyes.

“You… what do you know about mazes?”

A shrill scream tore through the air as Fleur flinched, shutting her eyes. The pretas shrieked as superheated hellfire surged across the boundaries of space. Even the teachers recoiled, overwhelmed by the raw agony radiating from Gaold—emotions so intense they transcended the body.

It was self-destruction incarnate, enough to shatter a weaker mind.

Armin’s nose twitched. His glowing eyes flickered eerily as a wave of light erupted from his back, rushing toward Gaold. From the center where they stood, two opposing forces—eternal flame and infinite stillness—collided.

“Don’t do anything reckless,” Armin warned. “Don’t think you’re the only one who’s suffered. This is your final warning. Promise me you won’t take Shiina.”

“Now I see,” Gaold said, voice low and venomous. “You’re just a clot of blood who understands nothing. Eternal Observer? You, who hid in a world that never changed…”

Gaold’s spirit zone erupted.

“Who are you to speak of hell?”

Woooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooooo!

The collision of their spirit zones sent shockwaves rippling outward in perfect spheres, collapsing the chamber walls in a clean, circular collapse. Shirone staggered, barely holding consciousness.

The crushing pressure bore down on everyone, a suffocating blend of heat and eternity. Then, suddenly, two golden cogwheels pierced through the curtain of hell, interlocking and spinning.

The distorted landscape imploded, sucked inward as if the center of a rug had been pulled. From the chaos, the original world reemerged.

When the hellfire vanished, all eyes turned to the golden wheels—now transformed into Sein’s amber irises, spinning rapidly, glowing like a halo.

*Cheolryunan – Equilibrium.*

The world’s organ, calming all phenomena by balancing the coefficient of reason and the coefficient of emotion.

Gaold allowed himself a small nod of approval. His former student’s skills hadn’t dulled.

Still, it irked him to be grouped with Armin as allies pursuing the same goal.

“What?” Gaold said. “Even if we fight together, it won’t be enough. Planning a three-way battle?”

“You stand before your master,” Sein said, his tone cool. “You may not recognize it at other times, but be careful now.”

His demeanor toward Alpheas was identical to Gaold’s—respectful, yet unyielding. When the entire world turned its back on the Supernatural Psychic Research Society, Alpheas was the only one who stood by them.

Gaold had no choice but to acknowledge him.

Of course, Gaold wasn’t someone swayed by guilt or self-denial. But if there was one person in the world he didn’t want to burden, it was Alpheas.

Sein turned to Armin.

“I apologize for exploiting your emotions,” he said. “But we need at least one more skilled member. If you knew this all along, why didn’t you convince Shiina yourself? As I’ve made clear, we have no intention of forcing anyone. This isn’t a mission that can succeed through coercion.”

Shiina stepped forward.

She hadn’t wanted to look at Armin again—but she couldn’t bear to watch others fight over her.

“Answer me one thing, Armin oppa.”

Armin, who hadn’t even turned his head until now, finally spoke, his voice strained.

“Shiina… I’m sorry for hiding it. But I—”

“Is Keira your brother’s wife?”

Keira flinched, her face brightening for an instant—then draining of color. She stepped back, sensing the terrifying presence that flickered behind Armin as he turned his head slightly.

Armin faced Shiina again. The presence vanished. Once more, he looked like a gentle lamb.

“No,” he said. “Keira is my watchdog. She entered a sham marriage for the mission. Everything I did… it was for you. Please, give me a chance to explain.”

A cold breath escaped Shiina’s lips.

*A fake marriage? A watchdog?*

You’re telling me this *now*?

*How hard I tried… every night, I vowed to forget. I trembled with loneliness, crushed the urge to steal even a glance at you, and wished you happiness with Keira.*

And it was all a lie?

After deceiving me completely, playing a loving couple right in front of my eyes—was it all *for my sake*?

“Shiina, if you want to be angry, I’ll accept it,” Armin whispered. “But—”

“No,” Shiina said, raising her hand. “Let me speak.”

There must have been reasons. Even if there weren’t, she had no right to be angry with Armin. She could only imagine the life he’d lived after losing both eyes to save her.

“Your brother saved my life,” she said. “Even if he deceived me, it doesn’t change that fact.”

Armin closed his mouth, his brows lifting.

*You don’t have to thank me.*

*If you want, I’ll tear out my heart right now. Shiina, if that’s what you desire.*

Shiina walked to him and embraced him.

Even as she was held in the arms of the man she had longed for, her heart stung as if lashed by a whip.

“Thank you, brother,” she said. “I’ll never forget. I’ll always be grateful for the life you gave me. But we’re adults now. We can’t live in the shadow of childhood feelings. Please… stop. Let me go. That is my final wish.”

“Shiina…”

Armin wept inwardly. With no pupils to shed tears, his sorrow remained trapped inside.

*I have to let her go. She can do as she pleases.*

*And I’ll wait. Because one day, she might miss me.*

*Even if she never does… I’ll wait my whole life.*

“Alright,” he said, voice breaking. “I’ll do as you say. Oppa… you can do whatever you want.”

He reached to caress her cheek—but she turned away, fist clenched.

“Let’s go,” she said.

“Armin, you can’t—” Keira started, her expression urgent.

But Armin’s flicker magic activated first.

In a crackle of electricity, the two vanished.

The spatial-transcending flicker magic reminded everyone: Armin was an Unlocker.

Gaold exhaled, his expression easing.

“Let’s finalize the plan. I can’t guarantee Kang Nan will succeed in recruiting, but once he arrives, Util won’t be an issue. When Shirone joins, our strategic options expand. As for dealers, Shiina and I are sufficient. Crowd control falls to Sein. One operator, one searcher.”

Fleur swelled with quiet pride at being acknowledged.

Originally, this mission had no place for an Eighth-Class Wizard. But the team’s power leakage was severe—proof of how desperate the situation had become.

Gaold’s expression hardened.

“I don’t want to build a cage-class team,” he said. “But wouldn’t it work if we had a balanced mix?”

“I think…” Sein said slowly, “we need one more hitman. If we can guarantee the elimination of a single target, our tactical range expands dramatically. Is there no skilled knife wielder? Someone who knows how to cut properly?”

“Where do you find someone like that on short notice?” Gaold asked. “Didn’t you have a magic swordsman in your guard?”

“Two updates,” Sein replied. “He’s probably dead. But consider this: even if he dies, he takes one target with him. We don’t have to deal with the aftermath. In short—a man who wants to die.”

“You’re asking too much.”

“If even one of us fails, I won’t save you,” Ethella said. “We’re the Suicide Squad. Anyone who follows us for reward is dead weight.”

“Why not look for a certified assassin?” Fleur suggested. “If he’s a renowned swordsman, his skill is guaranteed.”

“No,” Sein said. “Those who cling to belief burn uselessly. We’re not fighting a war. A hitman is perfect—focused solely on reclaiming the maze.”

He checked the time.

“For now, let’s head to Galliant. There might be someone with a knife in the Kergo Autonomous District.”

Then Shiina raised her hand.

“Hey,” she said. “What about a knight without faith?”

All eyes turned to her.

Gaold raised an eyebrow, as if about to object—then reconsidered.

“A knight without faith? Can you even call that a knight? No matter. But why?”

“There’s someone,” Shiina said. “Someone who knows how to *bell* properly.”
