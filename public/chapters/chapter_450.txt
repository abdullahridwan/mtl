Chapter 450  
In Heaven (2)  
~*~

When I entered the command center building and the iron door clicked shut behind me, the cheers from outside vanished as if I had been pulled from a dream.

The hallway lights were dimmed to a minimum, and soldiers stood guard at precise intervals along the corridor. Armed with the first-tier Mecha Combat System, they never bothered to look back at Shirone’s party. Yet the intensity of their stares was enough to pierce through steel.

*They’re all elite soldiers.*

Mecha pilots didn’t possess superhuman bodies or innate magical abilities, but they compensated for both with cutting-edge technical skill. In war, victory was all that mattered, so it was better not to dwell on the fact that their power might seem inferior to traditional magic users.

The colonel escorted Shirone and the others down to the underground bunker.

The bunker’s security systems were no less advanced than those of the Association. When the heavy iron doors slid open, rows of mechanical devices hummed with activity. Technicians moved quickly, their hands flying over consoles as they monitored screens, while a man in a commander’s cape stood with his hands clasped behind his back, watching their work.

“Commander, Shirone has been brought to you.”

At the colonel’s report, the commander turned around.

He was a man in his late twenties, with a long scar running down the side of his face from his temple to his jaw. His thin lips were set in a stubborn line, and the cool, sharp eyes behind his glasses betrayed both discipline and passion.

Crude, the first commander of the rebels.

As Kanya had mentioned, he was surprisingly young. But age mattered little in war, especially when defeat loomed so heavily. What soldiers needed was a commander willing to charge toward death first, paired with the tactical brilliance to save as many lives as possible. Crude fit that description perfectly.

He was a genius pilot who understood every aspect of Titan maintenance, operation, and combat within the third-tier Mecha Combat System. He was also a mecha war hero, credited with defeating a Mara.

“Are you Shirone?”

Crude extended a hand.

“Nice to meet you. I’m Crude, first commander of the rebels.”

“I’m Shirone.”

Crude studied Shirone and the others carefully. Years of battlefield experience allowed him to gauge their strength at a glance, but his dignified gaze never wavered. Then his eyes lingered on Fleur for a moment, softening into the look of a twenty-eight-year-old engineer who had spent his life surrounded by machinery.

She was a slim, straight-featured woman with blue hair braided down to her waist. What stood out most were her eyes. Among her companions, she was the only one radiating raw, unfiltered power. She looked like a heroine stepping onto the battlefield for the first time.

But that was all.

Crude knew the legends of Shirone, the light of District 73. Yet the war he had actually fought was too brutal and unforgiving to rely on myths.

“Go to a quiet place.”

Crude led them to a strategy room deep within the bunker. There was no tea served. As soon as they took their seats, they got straight to business.

“I heard you wanted to meet me?”

Sein sat at the negotiating table as their representative.

“We will attack Heaven. We ask for the cooperation of the rebel command.”

“Refuse.”

Crude’s answer was immediate.

“I heard there was a conflict with your battalion commander. Of course, a broken-hearted soldier is useless to us, but if you think about it coolly, there’s no chance of going to war right now. Attacking Heaven to subdue a giant who has lost its identity is nothing more than a delusion.”

Sein nodded meekly.

Shirone had been worried when he saw the battalion commander crushed by defeat, but the commander’s perspective was different.

“That means we haven’t given up on the war.”

“There is always room for compromise, whether through a truce or otherwise. I cannot kill those who believe in me and follow my orders. But I don’t think Heaven will see us as worthy of compromise. If we have to fight, we must win to survive.”

“What if we had a strategy to win?”

“Heh. Bullshit.”

Hundreds of thousands of rebels, including former heretics and subjects who had escaped from Heaven, had fought against Heaven. Yet only a handful had ever come close to making a dent. The union of Nor and Mecca hadn’t even managed to corner a triangular Mara, let alone a normal angel. What could ten humans possibly add to that equation?

“I will paralyze the Aegis system with the Targis. Then you can infiltrate Heaven and wage guerrilla warfare.”

The Aegis was Heaven’s core air defense system, an impregnable shield capable of tracking and intercepting seven thousand targets simultaneously by cross-referencing two hundred search methods. If they could breach it undetected, the rebels would have a sliver of hope.

“Hehehehe.”

Crude covered his face and let out a sharp laugh.

“I don’t know where you heard the word *Targis*, but it’s one of the technologies already lost to Mecca.”

Crude lowered his hand, his smile vanishing.

“Use common sense. The angels wouldn’t just leave behind technology capable of paralyzing Heaven’s interception system. We’d need access to the Akashic Records to bring it back online. But to do that, we’d have to break through the Aegis system first. Do you understand now? The order of operations has changed, you idiot.”

Sein leaned back in his chair and crossed his legs.

“If… what if I said I had that skill?”

“What?”

Crude’s expression went blank.

“What nonsense is that?”

“There’s no law stating that technology lost in Heaven can’t exist in the land kingdoms. For twenty years, I’ve wandered the world researching ancient weapons. Now, I have the blueprints for a perfectly restored Targis stored in my memory. How’s that for spinning your head?”

Crude’s eyes narrowed, confusion giving way to suspicion.

“If it’s false… we can restore it right now. There’s no forgetting in my mind. You’re an engineer yourself, so you’ll be able to follow the blueprints well enough.”

*I see. So on the black line…*

Shirone looked at Sein with new eyes.

Ancient weapons were top secret across every kingdom. Merely accessing information about them was a capital offense, punishable by death, unless one was trying to clear a labyrinth.

*Hmm…*

Crude was equally conflicted.

*Who the hell are these people? Why are they trying to do something that hundreds of thousands of rebels failed to achieve?*

*No, what matters is whether it actually works.*

Even if they paralyzed Aegis and slipped into Heaven, angels, Maras, giants, and fairies would be waiting. Defeating them and infiltrating Arabot, where Ankera was held, was the equivalent of carrying gunpowder into a fire.

*But you can still jump in. There’s still a one percent chance.*

*Is this a miracle? Or the last flicker of a candle right before it goes out?*

“Three… There are conditions.”

There were more conditions than expected, but Sein felt a strange sense of trust. It was proof that Crude was taking the plan seriously.

“Listen first.”

Crude raised a finger.

“First, use the light of District 73, Shirone, to appease the Second Headquarters. Even if you infiltrate Heaven, if Mecca and Nor don’t unite, you’ll be hitting a rock with an egg.”

“I suppose so. Next?”

“Second, even with the blueprints, a Targis large enough to affect all of Heaven cannot be built here. We need to rent a factory on the mainland. Get their permission and bring it to us.”

Sein recalled his first meeting with the rebels.

“Is it a military community?”

“Yes. To be precise, it’s a defense industry called ‘Night Maeng.’ It’s a newly emerging force that has grown rapidly by exploiting Heaven’s civil war, monopolizing weapon production by seizing all military complexes. The rebels despise them. However, without their resources, an all-out war with Heaven is impossible.”

“Understood. What’s the final condition?”

Crude hesitated. He didn’t know how capable Shirone and his party truly were, but this next requirement was beyond what a single team could handle alone.

“You’ll need a Black Elixir to construct the Targis.”

“Black Elixir?”

“Most lifeforms in Heaven absorb the planetary life stream into their bodies to create elixirs. There are five types: red, green, yellow, white, and finally, black.”

“Does it have to be black? As far as I know, elixirs can be circumvented depending on the mixing ratios and ingredients, right?”

“Each elixir type has a unique effect. Red governs heat, green controls biochemical elements, yellow alters energy properties, and white manipulates atmospheric elements. The rocks of Heaven are so durable they don’t weather even after tens of thousands of years. That’s why you need an elixir that contains the raw power of the planet itself.”

Crude pointed toward the warehouse district.

“For example, it takes eight thousand red elixirs to smelt the metal required for one Guroi. But if you mix in white elixirs, the thermal reaction becomes far more efficient. Mixing white and yellow can even generate electricity. Elixirs are the means to achieve almost anything in this world.”

Fleur spoke up.

“Then what does the Black Elixir do?”

Crude looked at Fleur for a moment, then turned his gaze to Sein.

“The Black Elixir, simply put, governs time.”

“Time? Is it some kind of magic?”

“No. To be precise, it reverses or accelerates irreversible phenomena. If you apply a Black Elixir to a mixture of ink and water, they will never separate again. In the case of burns, Black and Green elixirs can repair cellular damage.”

“I can’t…”

Shirone couldn’t believe it.

It was a phenomenon that completely ignored the laws of chemistry he had learned in school.

Zulu asked, “What about palpation?”

“It shortens the reaction rate. Green Elixir is the basic element that makes up living things, and when mixed with Black Elixir, it can trigger mutations. I’ve heard that in some cases, evolutionary products emerge thousands of years later.”

Gaold interjected.

“It’s a Philosopher’s Stone.”

Shirone had heard of it too.

It wasn’t a term commonly used in magic, but alchemists called it the *dream material*. Heaven had its own version of a fantasy artifact capable of turning iron into gold or curing incurable diseases.

*No way… then?*

One incident flashed through Shirone’s mind.

The other day, when Kanya’s mother became the subject of an anecdote, a large glass sphere had been filled with a black liquid. It was a cruel technique that melted a human into a statue of a giant, then used the giant as a mold.

The horror of it stemmed from a phenomenon his mind couldn’t fully grasp: a black liquid that dissolved and reconstituted living things.

If such a thing was possible in this world, a Black Elixir that defied chemical reactions would certainly be involved.

Sein grasped Crude’s intentions.

“You’re talking about using the Philosopher’s Stone to shorten the production process of the Targis, aren’t you?”

“Exactly. The Targis can be crafted with existing elixirs. But melting rocks to extract and purify metals will consume all your time. Equipment will be provided. Melt it down with the Black Elixir and process the metal needed for the Targis. Those are my three conditions.”

Armin asked, “What’s the probability if we collect it by hunting?”

Crude shook his head.

“Zero percent. Black Elixir is not created within living beings. It’s an element that can only be obtained from entities that have transcended the flow of time—that is, from the dead.”

If collecting planetary energy varied depending on the creature’s nature, it was understandable that the Philosopher’s Stone was so elusive.

“Undead creatures dwell in the Erosion Valley northwest of the mainland. But even if you hunt a thousand, I doubt you’ll find one. In other words, it’s not even a 0.1 percent chance.”

Gaold said indifferently, “Then what are you telling us to do?”

“There is one place. A location where you have a thirty, no, fifty percent chance of obtaining the Black Elixir.”

Sein asked, “Where is it?”

Crude hesitated again. But realizing he couldn’t stop now, he widened his eyes and spoke.

“The World of the Dead, Niflheim.”
