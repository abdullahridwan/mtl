Chapter 450

In Heaven (2)

When Shirone stepped into the command center and the heavy iron door sealed shut behind him, the roaring cheers from outside vanished as if they had belonged to a dream. The hallway ahead was dimly lit, the ceiling lights reduced to a minimal glow. At precise intervals, soldiers stood in silent vigil, their postures rigid.

Clad in the first tier of the Mecha combat system, they didn’t so much as glance at Shirone’s group. Yet their stillness carried a weight—eyes sharp enough to pierce through steel, a presence that spoke of relentless discipline.

*They’re all elite soldiers.*

The Mechas lacked superhuman strength or innate magic, but their mastery of technology more than compensated. In war, victory was all that mattered. It didn’t matter if their bodies weren’t enhanced—what counted was their ability to win.

Colonel Ludvans led Shirone and the others deeper underground, toward the rebel bunker. The security systems here rivaled even those of the Mage Association. When the final iron door groaned open, the interior hummed with activity—machines whirred, technicians moved swiftly between monitors, and data flickered across glowing screens.

At the center of it all stood a man in a commander’s cape, hands clasped behind his back, observing the operation with calm authority.

“Commander, Shirone has arrived,” Ludvans announced.

The man turned.

He was in his late twenties, with a long scar tracing from his temple down the side of his ear. His lips were thin, pressed together in quiet determination. Behind his glasses, his eyes held a chilling blend of cold calculation and fierce passion.

Crude. The First Commander of the Rebels.

As Kanya had described, he was young—but age meant little in war, especially one defined by endless defeat. What the soldiers needed wasn’t a seasoned veteran, but a leader who would charge into death without hesitation and possess the tactical genius to save lives. Crude was that man.

A prodigy pilot, he understood every aspect of the Titan—a third-tier Mecha unit—from maintenance to combat operation. He was a war hero, famed for defeating Mara in single combat.

“Are you Shirone?” Crude asked, extending a hand.

“Pleasure. I’m Crude, First Commander of the Rebels.”

“This is Shirone,” Sein replied, stepping forward as the group’s representative.

Crude studied Shirone and his companions with a veteran’s eye. Years of battle had honed his ability to assess strength at a glance, and though he saw power in the group, his expression remained unshaken.

Then his gaze landed on Fleur.

For a brief moment, his stern composure softened. His eyes lingered—on her blue hair, braided neatly to her waist, on her sharp, elegant features. But most of all, on her eyes. Among the group, she was the only one whose aura burned with unrestrained intensity.

Like a heroine stepping onto the battlefield for the first time.

But that was all.

Crude had heard the legends—Shirone, the beacon of District 73. Yet the war he fought was too real, too brutal, to place faith in myths.

“Let’s go somewhere quieter,” he said, turning toward a side corridor.

He led them to a strategy room deep within the bunker. There would be no tea, no pleasantries. As soon as they were seated, Sein got straight to the point.

“I heard you wanted to meet me?” Crude asked.

“We’re planning to attack Heaven,” Sein said. “We’re asking for the Rebel Command’s cooperation.”

“Refused.”

The answer came instantly.

“I know about your conflict with the battalion commander. A broken soldier is useless to us. But beyond that—be realistic. Attacking Heaven now? It’s delusional. You can’t possibly hope to defeat a force like that with just ten people, especially when your own side is fractured.”

Sein nodded quietly. He had expected resistance, but Crude was different from the others—calm, rational, not ruled by emotion.

“That doesn’t mean we’ve given up on the war,” Sein said.

“There’s always room for compromise,” Crude replied. “A truce, a negotiation—anything to avoid throwing lives away. I can’t afford to sacrifice those who trust me. But Heaven won’t see us as equals. If we must fight, we have to win. Otherwise, we die.”

“What if we had a strategy to win?”

Crude let out a short, bitter laugh.

“Bullshit.”

Hundreds of thousands of rebels—heretics, defectors from Heaven, entire armies—had fought Heaven before. Few had achieved anything. Even the combined forces of Nor and Mecca had failed to capture a single Triangular Mara, let alone challenge an angel. What could ten humans possibly do?

“I can paralyze the Aegis system using the Targis,” Sein said. “Once it’s down, you can infiltrate Heaven and wage guerrilla warfare.”

Aegis—the core air defense system of Heaven—was a fortress in itself. It could detect and intercept up to 7,000 targets simultaneously, using two hundred different scanning methods to prioritize threats. Breaching it without bloodshed was nearly impossible.

But if it could be disabled…

The rebels would finally have a chance.

Crude covered his face, then let out a long, disbelieving breath.

“Heh… heh… heh…”

He lowered his hand, his smile gone.

“I don’t know where you heard the word *Targis*, but that technology has been lost for centuries. Heaven would never allow the means to disable Aegis to survive. To restore it, we’d need access to the Akashic Records. But to reach those, we’d have to get past Aegis first.” He leaned forward. “Do you see the problem? The order is reversed. You’re asking the impossible.”

Sein leaned back, crossing his legs.

“What if I told you… I already have the skill?”

Crude froze.

“What?”

“There’s no law saying that technology lost in Heaven can’t exist elsewhere. For twenty years, I’ve traveled the world, researching ancient weapons. Now, I have the complete blueprints for the Targis—restored, perfected—stored in my mind. How’s that for a revelation?”

Crude’s eyes narrowed.

“If this is a lie…”

“We can begin construction immediately. There’s no flaw in the design. You’re an engineer—look at the schematics yourself. You’ll know I’m telling the truth.”

Shirone studied Sein with new respect.

Ancient weapons were state secrets in every kingdom. Merely accessing such knowledge was a capital offense—punishable by death. To have spent two decades uncovering them…

“Hmm…”

Even Crude was shaken now.

*Who are these people? Why would they attempt what even armies failed to do?*

But the more pressing question was: *Could it work?*

Even if Aegis fell, Heaven was filled with angels, Maras, giants, and fairies. Reaching Arabot—the fortress where Ankera resided—would be like carrying gunpowder into a fire.

*But… even a 1% chance is a chance.*

Is this a miracle? Or the final flare of a candle before it burns out?

“Three conditions,” Crude said at last.

More than expected, but Sein felt a flicker of hope. Conditions meant Crude was taking the plan seriously.

“Speak,” Crude said, raising a finger.

“First: Use Shirone, the Light of District 73, to secure the support of the 2nd HQ. Even if you breach Heaven, without the full alliance of Mecca and Nor, you’ll be throwing eggs at a stone wall.”

“I understand. Next?”

“Second: Even with blueprints, we can’t build a large-scale Targis here. We need a factory on the mainland. Get permission from ‘Night Maeng’ and bring their resources.”

Sein recalled his first encounter with the rebels.

“Night Maeng? The military-industrial complex?”

“Yes. They’ve risen rapidly during Heaven’s civil war, seizing control of all major weapon production. They’re despised by the rebels—but without them, an all-out war is impossible.”

“Understood. And the third condition?”

Crude hesitated.

He still didn’t know the true strength of Shirone’s group. This wasn’t a mission for a single team.

“You’ll need Black Elixir to construct the Targis.”

“Black Elixir?”

“Most beings in Heaven absorb the planet’s life stream and produce Elixir. There are five types: red, green, yellow, white, and black.”

“Why black? Can’t we substitute with a mixture?”

“Each Elixir has a unique property. Red governs heat, green handles biochemical processes, yellow alters energy states, and white manipulates atmospheric elements. But Heaven’s structures are forged from rock that doesn’t erode—even after millennia. To shape that, you need something that commands the planet’s core power.”

Crude gestured toward a nearby warehouse.

“For example, casting the metal for a single Guroi requires 8,000 red Elixirs. But mix in white, and the thermal efficiency doubles. Combine white and yellow, and you generate electricity. Elixir is the foundation of everything here.”

Fleur spoke up.

“What does the Black Elixir do?”

Crude looked at her, then back at Sein.

“Simply put… it governs time.”

“Time? Like magic?”

“No. More precisely, it reverses or accelerates irreversible processes. Pour ink into water, and normally it disperses. But with Black Elixir, the ink and water can be separated again. For burns, combining black and green Elixirs can repair cellular damage instantly.”

Shirone couldn’t believe it.

This defied every law of chemistry he’d ever learned.

Zocre asked, “What about mutation?”

“It accelerates reaction rates. Green Elixir is the base component of life. Mix it with Black, and you can trigger rapid evolution. I’ve heard of cases where creatures evolved thousands of years’ worth in minutes.”

Gaold exhaled.

“It’s the Philosopher’s Stone.”

Shirone had heard the term too. Not common in magic, but alchemists called it “dream material”—a legendary substance capable of turning iron to gold or curing any disease.

Then it hit him.

The day Kanya’s mother became a subject of experimentation… the large glass sphere filled with black liquid.

A horrific technique—melting a human into a liquid, then reshaping them into a giant like molten metal in a mold.

The cruelty wasn’t just in the act, but in the impossibility of it. A phenomenon his mind couldn’t grasp.

A liquid that dissolved and reformed living matter.

If that existed… then Black Elixir, defying natural laws, had to be real.

Sein understood Crude’s intent.

“You’re saying we use the Philosopher’s Stone to accelerate the Targis production process?”

“Exactly. Targis can be built with standard Elixirs. But extracting and refining the necessary metals from raw rock would take years. We have the equipment. Use Black Elixir to dissolve and reshape existing materials—forge the components instantly. That’s the only way.”

Armand’s voice echoed in Shirone’s mind.

*What are the odds if we hunt for Black Elixir?*

Crude shook his head.

“Zero. Black Elixir isn’t produced by living beings. It can only be harvested from those who’ve transcended time—meaning, the dead.”

Given how Elixir production varied by a being’s nature, it made sense. The Philosopher’s Stone was never meant to be natural.

“Undead roam the Erosion Valley, northwest of the mainland. But even if you captured a thousand, you’d be lucky to find one with usable Black Elixir. Less than 0.1% chance.”

Gaold shrugged. “Then what are you suggesting?”

“There is one place. A location where you have a 30% chance—no, 50%—of obtaining Black Elixir.”

Sein leaned forward.

“Where?”

Crude hesitated again.

But he knew there was no turning back.

His eyes widened.

“The world of the dead. Niflheim.”
