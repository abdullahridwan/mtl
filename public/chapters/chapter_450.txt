Chapter 450  
In Heaven (2)  
~*~

When Shirone stepped into the command center building and the iron door closed behind him, the cheers outside were cut off at once, as though he had woken from a dream.

The lights along the hallway ceiling were kept to a bare minimum, and soldiers stood guard at precise intervals down the corridor. Armed with the 1st-level Mecha combat system, none of them so much as glanced at Shirone's party as they passed. And yet the intensity radiating from their eyes felt sharp enough to pierce stone.

*They're all elite soldiers,* Shirone thought.

Mecha pilots didn't have superhuman bodies or magical power, but their technical skill more than made up for both. In war, winning was all that mattered — it would be a mistake to assume they were weak simply because they lacked raw strength.

The colonel led Shirone and the others down into the underground bunker. Its security system was in no way inferior to the Association's own technology, and when the iron door slid open, row after row of mechanical devices whirred to life inside.

Technicians worked briskly at their monitors, while a man in a commander's cape stood a short distance away, hands clasped behind his back, watching over them.

"Commander, I've brought Shirone."

At the colonel's words, the commander turned around. He was a man in his late twenties, a long scar running from his temple down past his ear. His lips were pressed into a stubborn line, and behind his glasses, his eyes carried both coolness and a quiet passion.

This was Crude, first commander of the rebels. Just as Kanya had said, he was young. But age mattered little in war — especially one marked by repeated defeat. What soldiers wanted was a commander with the courage to rush toward death first, and the tactical mind to bring as many of them back alive as possible. Crude fit that description exactly. He was a genius pilot, versed in every detail of maintaining, piloting, and operating the Titan-class machines of the 3rd-level mecha combat system, and a war hero remembered for having brought down Mara.

"Are you Shirone?" Crude asked, extending his hand.

"Nice to meet you. I'm Crude, first commander of the rebels."

"I'm Shirone."

Crude studied Shirone and the others closely. Having lived through countless battles, he could gauge the strength of a group at a glance, and his composed eyes betrayed nothing. But when his gaze reached Fleur, it lingered there a moment — carrying him back, briefly, to his own days as a twenty-eight-year-old engineer who had spent his life wrestling with machines.

She was slender, with straight blue hair braided down to her waist. What struck him most were her eyes — among the whole group, hers alone still burned with an undimmed fire, like a heroine setting foot on a battlefield for the very first time.

But that was all. Crude, too, had heard the tales of Shirone, the Light of District 73 — but the war he had actually lived through was far too brutal for him to place his faith in legends.

"Let's go somewhere quiet."

Crude led them to the strategy room deep inside the bunker. Naturally, no tea was served; the moment they sat down, they got straight to business.

"I hear you wanted to see me?" Crude said.

Sein settled into the negotiating seat as the group's representative.

"We intend to attack Heaven. We're asking for the rebel command's cooperation."

"Refused." Crude's answer came instantly. "I heard you clashed with the battalion commander. A soldier who's lost his nerve is useless to us, granted — but think about this calmly. There's no chance of going to war right now. Attacking Heaven to bring down some giant who's lost his own identity is nothing but a delusion."

Sein nodded meekly. He had been anxious after seeing how thoroughly defeat had worn down the battalion commander — but this commander was different.

"That means you haven't given up on the war either," Sein said.

"There's always room for compromise — a truce, if nothing else. I can't bring myself to get the people who believe in me and follow me killed. But I doubt Heaven sees us as worth compromising with. If we have to fight regardless, then we need to win in order to survive."

"What if we had a strategy to win?"

"Heh. Bullshit."

Hundreds of thousands of rebels — heretics and defectors who'd fled Heaven alike — had fought against Heaven over the years, and only a handful had ever accomplished anything worth remembering. Even the combined strength of Norr and Mecha hadn't managed to bring down so much as a triangular-rank mara, let alone a proper angel. What difference could ten more people possibly make?

"I'll paralyze the Aegis system using the Targis. Then you can slip into Heaven and wage guerrilla warfare."

Aegis was Heaven's core air-defense system — an impregnable shield capable of engaging seven thousand targets simultaneously, prioritizing interception order through two hundred separate detection methods. If they could break through it and enter without shedding blood, the rebels would have a real glimmer of hope.

"Hehehehe." Crude covered his face with one hand and let out a hollow laugh. "I don't know where you heard the word Targis, but that's technology that's already been lost to Mecha." He lowered his hand, no longer smiling. "Use your common sense. The angels aren't about to leave lying around a technology that could paralyze Heaven's own interception system. To revive it, we'd need access to the Akashic Records — but to reach the Akashic Records, we'd first have to break through Aegis. Do you see the problem now? You've got the order backward, you idiot."

Sein leaned back in his chair and crossed his legs.

"What if... what if I told you I already have that technology?"

"What?" Crude's face went blank. "What nonsense is that?"

"There's no law saying that technology lost in Heaven can't survive somewhere in the kingdoms of the land. For twenty years I wandered the world, researching everything I could find about ancient weapons. And now I carry the complete, fully restored blueprint of the Targis in my head. So — is your head spinning yet?"

Confusion crept into Crude's eyes.

"If this is a lie—"

"We can start restoring it right now. Nothing in my head has been forgotten. You're an engineer yourself — once you see the blueprints, you'll understand well enough."

*I see,* Shirone thought, looking at Sein in a new light. *So that's how — through the black market.*

Ancient weapons were top secret regardless of the kingdom. Merely accessing such information was a crime punishable by death — to say nothing of surviving whatever network of secrecy protected it.

"Hmm..." Crude murmured, thoroughly confused now. Just who were these people? Why would they attempt something hundreds of thousands of rebels had never managed?

*No — what matters is whether it can actually succeed,* he thought. Even if they paralyzed Aegis and got inside Heaven, angels, maras, giants, and fairies would all be waiting. Fighting through all of that to reach Arabot, where Ankera resided, would be like carrying gunpowder and leaping into a fire.

*Still — it's a fire you could jump into. There's at least a one percent chance.*

Was this the beginning of a miracle? Or merely the last desperate flare of a candle burning brightest right before it went out?

"Three," Crude said finally. "There are three conditions."

It was more conditions than Sein had expected, but if anything, it reassured him — proof that Crude was taking the plan seriously, and dealing with it realistically.

"Hear them out first," Crude said, holding up a finger. "First: use Shirone, the Light of District 73, to win over 2nd HQ. Even if we get into Heaven, if Mecha and Norr don't unite, we'll be throwing an egg at a rock either way."

"Fair enough. What's next?"

"Second: even with the blueprints in hand, a Targis large enough to affect the whole of Heaven can't be built here. We'll need to rent a factory on the mainland — go and secure their permission."

Sein recalled the first time he had met the rebels.

"Is that the military community?"

"Yes. More precisely, it's a defense conglomerate called 'Night Maeng.' They're an emerging power that's grown rapidly by exploiting Heaven's civil war, seizing control of the military-industrial complexes and monopolizing weapons production. The rebels despise them. But without borrowing their strength, an all-out war with Heaven simply isn't possible."

"Fine. What's the last condition?"

Crude hesitated before voicing this one. He didn't know just how capable Shirone and the others really were — but this wasn't something a single team could pull off alone.

"You'll need a black elixir to build the Targis."

"Black elixir?"

"Most lifeforms in Heaven draw the planet's life-stream into their bodies and produce elixirs. There are five kinds: red, green, yellow, white, and finally, black."

"Does it have to be black? As far as I know, there are ways to work around it depending on the ratio you mix the ingredients — isn't that right?"

"Each elixir has its own unique effect. Red governs heat, green governs biochemical processes, yellow alters the properties of energy, and white is bound up with atmospheric elements. The stone of Heaven is so durable it doesn't weather even after tens of thousands of years — which is exactly why you need an elixir that carries the power of the planet itself."

Crude gestured toward the warehouse.

"For instance, casting the metal for a single Guroi takes eight thousand red elixirs on its own. But mix in white elixirs, and the efficiency of the thermal reaction rises dramatically. Mix white and yellow together, and you can even generate electricity. Elixirs are the means by which everything in this world gets done."

"Then what does black elixir do?" Fleur asked.

Crude studied her face for a moment, then turned to Sein and answered.

"Black elixir, simply put, governs time."

"Time? Is that some kind of magic?"

"No. To be precise, it reverses or accelerates phenomena that are normally irreversible. Use black elixir on a mixture of ink and water, and the ink and water will never separate again. For burns, mixing black and green elixir together can even repair damaged cells."

"That's impossible..." Shirone said. He couldn't believe it — it defied every law of chemistry he'd ever learned.

"What about the acceleration effect?" Zulu asked.

"It shortens reaction time. Green elixir is the base element that composes living things, and mixing it with black elixir can trigger mutations. I've heard that in some cases, it produces evolutionary results that would otherwise take thousands of years."

"It's a philosopher's stone," Gaold said.

Shirone had heard of it too. It wasn't a term commonly used in magic, but alchemists spoke of it as the substance of dreams — a legendary material said to turn plain iron into gold, or cure incurable disease.

*No way... could it be?*

A memory surfaced in Shirone's mind. The other day, when Kanya had told the story of her mother, he remembered the great glass sphere filled with black liquid — the cruel technique that dissolved a human being and recast them as a giant, poured into shape like a mold. What made it feel crueler still was that it was a phenomenon his mind simply couldn't comprehend. A black liquid that dissolved living things and reconstituted them into something else. If such a thing was possible in this world, then a black elixir capable of overriding chemical law had to be involved somehow.

Sein grasped Crude's meaning.

"You mean using the philosopher's stone to shorten the production time of the Targis — that's what you're getting at?"

"Exactly. The Targis can be built with ordinary elixirs. But just melting down rock to extract and refine the metal alone would eat up all your time. I'll provide the equipment. Whatever raw material you use, melt it down with black elixir and process the metal the Targis needs. Those are my three conditions."

"What's the probability of collecting it by hunting?" Armand asked.

Crude shook his head.

"Zero percent. Black elixir doesn't form inside a living body. It's an element that can only be obtained from beings who have transcended the flow of time — in other words, from the dead."

It made a strange kind of sense — if the way a creature absorbed the planet's energy depended on its nature, then a substance as imperfect and rare as the philosopher's stone would naturally have to come from outside the realm of the living.

"Undead creatures live in the Erosion Valley, northwest of the mainland," Crude went on. "But even if you caught a thousand of them, you'd be lucky to get even one elixir out of it. In other words, we're talking less than a 0.1 percent chance."

"Then what are you telling us to do?" Gaold asked indifferently.

"There is one place," Crude said. "Somewhere you could get a black elixir with... thirty percent odds. No — fifty percent."

"Where is it?" Sein asked.

Crude hesitated once more. But knowing he couldn't stop now, he steadied himself and opened his mouth.

"The world of the dead. Niflheim."
