Chapter 455  
Definition of Creature (1)  
~*~

Back in the Commander’s Office, Reisis donned a crimson cloak and fell into deep thought. The aide who entered shortly after noticed her distant gaze and spoke cautiously.

“Commander, how will Shirone be handled…?”

It wouldn’t have mattered if they had revealed the Nephilim’s abilities from the start. After all, Nephilim were beings of such nature. But Shirone had already surpassed even Reisis’s own potential—and that would inevitably ripple through the Norse rebels who placed their faith in Spirit Force. This was a delicate situation, one that demanded careful handling.

‘I knew you didn’t trust me,’ Reisis thought bitterly, ‘but I never expected them to stab me in the back like this.’

“Commander… your instructions?” the aide pressed, growing impatient.

Outside, unrest was spreading. Rumors about Shirone’s role were swirling, and with each passing hour, more began to question Reisis’s absolute authority.

*Knock, knock, knock.*

Reisis lifted her head and smiled—softly, enigmatically. The aide frowned, confused.

She stopped smiling and turned to him. “I’ve finally found the perfect factor to compensate for my flaws.”

“Then… Shirone…?”

“Yes. Shirone’s immense Spirit Force will become a vessel—one capable of absorbing the traits of any creature. I can’t let this opportunity slip.”

The aide’s eyes darted uneasily. He had seen Shirone’s power firsthand; there was no denying its strength. But Shirone was also a symbol of the rebellion. If public opinion turned, everything they had built could collapse in an instant.

Reisis raised an eyebrow. “I know what you’re thinking.”

“I apologize, Commander. But this path is dangerous.”

With a graceful flick of her cloak, Reisis turned. “We’ll create clones using a genetic sample from Shirone. I’ll conduct clinical trials on the clone first. Once I’m certain of success, I’ll absorb it.”

“Is that even possible?” the aide asked, frowning. “Shirone isn’t just a test subject. Human cloning is highly sensitive, and even the slightest genetic variance could lead to failure. Subjects and their imitations may be biologically human, but they’ve evolved independently over generations, isolated from one another. Even with identical chromosome counts, evolutionary divergence makes successful implantation in a cloning incubator extremely unlikely.”

“Not to mention,” he added, “it could waste precious black elixir.”

Reisis waved a hand dismissively. “No need to worry. Humans are just complex machines. It’s all a matter of design. I’m already imagining the possibilities—what abilities could I equip Shirone with?”

Her eyes gleamed with excitement, her imagination running wild. Then, her expression hardened.

“We’ll need cooperation from the military. Gather genetic material from powerful monsters—only the strongest traits. I’ll combine them with Shirone’s clone. Strength, resilience, regeneration—only the peak traits will be selected.”

“Understood,” the aide said, bowing before exiting.

Once alone, Reisis settled onto the sofa, crossing her legs. She flicked her red tongue playfully.

Experimenting on a Nephilim’s body was unprecedented. But if successful, the rewards would be limitless.

“Whoa, whoa,” she murmured, grinning. “This is going to be fun.”

It was a gamble—one that could define her legacy.

* * *

Shirone had been promoted to leader of the Riot Police, a special unit under the Second Command. Unlike the First and Second Mobile Squadrons, the Third Mobile Squadron—his new unit—answered directly to the Commander and consisted of the most elite members, all possessing over 20% Spirit Force.

Fleur, now vice-captain of the Third Mobility Corps, glanced at the three squad members. Though Shirone’s promotion was a meteoric rise in rank, an undercurrent of tension lingered. They were being watched.

“It’s kind of annoying. I mean, *them*,” Fleur muttered.

“It was expected,” Shirone replied. “Ever since I activated the Immortal Function.”

Reisis had promised an alliance with the First Command on the condition that Shirone be placed under the Second. But trust was thin. Shirone knew she was using him—positioning him as a tool to be wielded in case of emergency.

“I know,” Fleur said, “but still…”

She turned her head, and the three squad members quickly looked away, pretending they hadn’t been staring. That alone told her what orders they’d received.

‘They’re supposed to monitor him. Probably waiting for a chance—maybe at night?’

Lost in thought, Fleur was startled when Jo Won approached.

Tall, with a long philtrum and an overbearing gaze, Jo Won was technically Shirone’s subordinate—but his demeanor suggested otherwise.

“Commander,” he said, “our first mission has been assigned. It’s on the Highlands of Corruption…”

“Hold on,” Shirone interrupted. “Why are *you* delivering the report?”

Fleur fell silent.

“Haven’t you heard?” Jo Won replied coolly. “The reporting system’s been restructured. The leader is clearly Shirone.”

He stood motionless, expression unchanged. But as the silence stretched, he finally bowed his head, as if conceding defeat.

“My apologies. A misunderstanding occurred. I was the previous leader, so I assumed the responsibility. I’ll report this to higher command and ensure it doesn’t happen again.”

He bowed deeply and stepped back.

Fleur exhaled. There was no point in escalating—especially since they’d have to work together for now. Better not to stir unnecessary conflict.

“Fine. Continue.”

A drone translated Fleur’s words. While it handled casual conversation well, it was too slow for battlefield commands.

“There are ancient ruins on the Highlands of Corruption,” Jo Won said. “The rebels call it the ‘Tomb of the Angel.’ It may have once been a gateway for angels, but now it’s abandoned.”

The history of Heaven was so ancient it had become myth to most. But even myths held fragments of truth—and Shirone and Fleur were intrigued.

“Hmm,” Shirone mused. “What’s the objective?”

“Only angels and Nephilim can enter—the ruins are sealed by angelic magic. And among the current rebels, the only living Nephilim is you, Chief Shirone.”

“Aha. Of course.”

Shirone could feel Reisis’s hand in this—using him fully while keeping him under surveillance. He didn’t know the full relationship between the Norse and the wizards, but her approach was undeniably wizard-like in its calculation.

“Fine. Let’s go.”

The ruins might hold information vital to their mission—regardless of personal interest.

‘We have to push forward,’ Shirone thought. ‘The seniors are on even more dangerous missions.’

With the Spirit of Light, Ellizer, activated, the Third Task Force was deployed to the Highlands of Corruption.

* * *

Armin, Shiina, Etela, and Quan—four of them now—had camped in the forest before returning to the mainland at dawn.

The mainland’s first impression was chaotic, reminiscent of the World’s Fair they’d once visited. There was no uniformity—strange creatures roamed the streets, and the architecture varied wildly. Huts, timber houses, brick buildings, and mechanical structures stood side by side, as if time itself had been layered unevenly.

“Wow, this is amazing,” Etela said with an innocent smile. “Nowhere else looks like this.”

But no one replied.

When the full team of ten had been together, the tension hadn’t been obvious. But now, with only four, the distance between Shiina and Armin was palpable.

Shiina was the one most unsettled.

She had expected Armin to tell her first. At the very least, an explanation for how he’d followed her to Heaven.

‘You said you left the Ivory Tower?’ she thought. *If it was that simple, why didn’t you tell me earlier?*

Before she could stop herself, the words slipped out.

“It’s fine?”

Etela, who had been trying to lighten the mood, fell silent. After a long pause, Armin finally spoke.

“What do you mean?”

“Keira. What happened to her?”

Silence.

There were some things he could never tell Shiina. And even beyond that, staying silent was his way of honoring Keira.

“Are you really leaving the Ivory Tower?” Shiina pressed.

Keira had stared at Armin with wide, fearful eyes when he told her.

Shiina hadn’t been shocked—she’d expected it. The real problem was the Oath of Pact binding Armin to the Ivory Tower.

The condition of the pact—tied to the ability of the Regular Eater—would trigger if broken, rendering him unable to cast magic forever.

So Keira had taken out the Oath of Agreement.

At the bottom, Armin’s signature was clearly visible—the same one he’d used when he first took Shiina as a hostage. He had hoped never to tear it. But if this was the price of moving forward, so be it.

“If you go,” Keira said, voice trembling, “our relationship ends here. If you’re okay with that, then leave this room.”

Armin smiled—softly, sadly.

It was a smile he’d never shown before. Keira recognized it for what it was: a farewell.

“Thank you,” he said quietly. “For everything.”

He didn’t say it, but he knew how much she had sacrificed for him.

“Huh!” Keira snapped, tears welling. “Don’t try to make me feel better! Don’t act like you’re the only one suffering! Or are you just going to stop time again and run away?”

Armin shook his head. “I can’t use stop magic like that anymore. I’ve already consumed too much of the timeline. I’m just a burden now.”

“What does that matter?” she shot back. “If you tear the oath, you’re nothing! I’d be a blind man who can’t even go to the bathroom alone!”

“Then it’s unavoidable,” Armin said calmly. “You’ve caused me nothing but trouble anyway. Do as you please in the end.”

He turned and walked toward the door.

Just as his hand touched the doorknob—*snap!*

The sound of tearing paper.

Armin turned, startled.

The oath had been broken—but not by him.

Keira stood there, one of the fallen oaths at her feet, her own signature now visible.

She had destroyed her own Oath of Pact—the one she’d taken to monitor Armin on behalf of the Ivory Tower.

“Go,” she said, voice breaking. “The situation has changed. If you care for her that much… then go!”

“Keira, I—”

“Don’t come any closer!”

She shouted, tears streaming.

She didn’t want to be pitied. She didn’t want to be remembered as someone weak, clinging to the past.

“Go to Heaven and protect her. But…” She lifted her tear-streaked face, eyes burning with emotion. “Come back alive. Protect her—and come back alive, you bastard son of the world!”

Armin said nothing.

Keira was a wizard of the Ivory Tower, recognized by the world itself. She had never believed her achievements were lesser.

“I will repay you,” Armin whispered.

Then, casting Flicker Magic, he vanished in an instant.

Now, Armin’s party stood before the main gate of the military complex on the outskirts of the mainland—known as ‘Night Mang.’

The complex was massive, its walls towering over the landscape, chimneys belching smoke into the sky.

“I don’t think we need to explain ourselves,” Armin said, stepping forward.

He had brought a drone for emergencies, but he hadn’t learned the language of Heaven yet—so it was useless now.

“Excuse me. This is—”

The two guards at the gate closed in, blocking their path. They wore tight, unfamiliar suits and glasses embedded with black, egg-like lenses.

“Stop. Identify yourselves.”

“I’ve come with a request. May I speak to the person in charge?”

As the gate creaked open, ten more guards in identical suits emerged, forming a silent, emotionless wall before Armin and his companions.

“Turn back. My brother’s in a bad mood today.”

“I apologize,” Armin said, “but we’re running out of time. At least let us speak to someone.”

A large, heavily built guard stepped forward, cracking his knuckles.

“I don’t care. Tell me, guys—do you even know where you are?”

As over ten guards unleashed their presence at once, a wave of oppressive energy crashed over the group.

‘This…’

All four felt it instantly.

A temperament—raw, overwhelming—that could never exist in the original world.

“Be careful,” Quan said, drawing his sword. “These aren’t human.”
